Leadership in Organizations

Prof. Mike North · NYU Stern Section 30 · COR1-GB.1302 · Mondays 6–9pm · Feb 2 – May 4, 2026

Frameworks to analyze leadership behavior in organizations, and tools (and reasons) to act on what you learn. Premise of the course: leadership is a behavior, not a position — and you have to act to learn. Pedagogy is roughly 1/3 application, 1/3 experiential, 1/3 lecture.

Recurring themes across the course

  1. Leadership = developing people through work — not just managing tasks.
  2. Status quo is gravity — change requires overcoming institutional inertia.
  3. Leadership = IQ + EQ — logic alone isn't enough; emotion and connection matter.
  4. Know your constituents — who are you leading for?
  5. Past behavior predicts future behavior — but self-awareness can break the cycle.
  6. Descriptive ≠ prescriptive — understanding how things work doesn't mean endorsing them.
  7. Group processes can overcome individual blind spots — but watch for groupthink, the Abilene paradox, and polarization.
  8. Incentive systems are control mechanisms — align goals, but "not everything that counts can be counted."
  9. Fairness is a powerful motivator — fair process effect: people accept bad outcomes when the process was fair.
  10. Feedback is essential but hard — self-serving bias, sugarcoating, timing, insecurity all undermine it. "Do you want to feel better today or perform better tomorrow?"

Class 1 · Feb 2

Introduction & alignment

Case: Mt. Everest 1996

Leadership vs management

  • Leadership = developing people through work.
  • Management = developing work through people.
  • Both are needed; this course focuses on the leadership side.

Status quo as gravity

The status quo exerts a gravitational pull — friction forces multiplied by institutional inertia. Past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior. Changing the status quo requires overcoming this force.

Two halves of the course

  1. Individual leadership muscles — vision, change, listening, courage, persuasion.
  2. Leading organizations, groups, and individuals — org design, networking, motivation, norms.
Class 2 · Feb 9

Vision & leading others

Activity: "Shooting at the Moon" Vision Workshop

What makes a vision effective

Vague, laudable visions ("be the best," "deliver excellence") don't motivate. JFK's "man on the moon before the end of the decade" is the gold standard — concrete, time-bound, audacious.

Four elements of an effective vision

  1. Vivid image-laden portrait of the future — people need to see it.
  2. Link between vision and daily work — how does my job connect to the vision?
  3. Shared reference points — common language and touchstones the team can rally around.
  4. Build leader confidence — the vision must make people believe the leader can get there.
Moonshot thinking: aim for 10x, not 10%. Big visions are more motivating than incremental ones.
Class 3 · Feb 23

Leading change

Activity: Change Game (Zoom display name change with stakes)

Why change attempts fail

  • Over-reliance on formal power/authority.
  • Distrust of the change agent.
  • Reluctance to change (status quo gravity).
  • Scarcity of time.
  • Absent vision.
  • Emphasis on the change itself rather than unfreezing first.

Organizations are built to be stable. That's the fundamental challenge of change — you're fighting the system's design.

Push + Pull = leadership

  • Push = logic, data, rational arguments (IQ).
  • Pull = emotion, inspiration, connection (EQ).
  • Effective change requires both: Leadership = Logic + Emotion.

The 20/60/20 rule

  • Top 20% — Friends: already on your side.
  • Middle 60% — Fringe: persuadable, watching to see which way things go.
  • Bottom 20% — Foes: actively resistant, unlikely to convert.
Key move: target the fringe first, not the foes. Foes consume disproportionate energy with low ROI. Win the middle.

Change through feedback

  • Gap between how we see ourselves and how others see us.
  • Accuracy in self-assessment is a leadership skill.
  • Change is gradual — must unfreeze before you can move.

Leading generations

Case: "Pokémon? NO" — generational conflict in the workplace

Key takeaways

  • Work values shift over time across generations.
  • Culture add vs culture fit — hiring for "fit" can exclude; hiring for "add" brings new perspectives.
  • Aging population means more multi-generational workplaces than ever.

The "silver tsunami"

  • US population aging dramatically (1950–2060).
  • Labor force growth increasingly driven by 55+ workers.
  • Wealth gap widening: ages 65–74 saw +72.4% net worth growth vs. −19% for ages 35–44.
  • Generational tension in politics and economics is downstream of this demographic shift.

North's research focus is ageism and intergenerational dynamics — this is his core expertise area.

Class 5 · Mar 9

Leading by listening

Case: Naomi Chen at WestWave

The Naomi Chen case

WestWave product launch — Naomi sensed fear and sadness in the room during the launch presentation. She made the courageous call to cut things short and delay launch by one week. Result: $30M profit year, the most successful product launch in company history. She got promoted, then left to start her own company.

Core lesson: Leadership = "balancing your head and your humanity."

Listening defined

Attention + Comprehension + Relation. Non-judgmental, empathic, authentic, respectful.

Three rationales for listening

  1. Learn from someone — gather information.
  2. Evaluate someone — assess competence/fit.
  3. Relate to someone — build connection.
  4. (Bonus) Give them space to hear themselves.

Six types of non-listeners

  1. Faker — pretends to listen, checked out.
  2. Interpreter — projects their own meaning onto what you say.
  3. Intellectual — listens only for logic/data, ignores emotion.
  4. Fisher — listens only for what they want to hear.
  5. Rebuttal Maker — listens only to build a counterargument.
  6. Advice Giver — jumps to solutions before understanding the problem.

Why listening matters (the data)

  • Alters story quality and fluency in the speaker.
  • Shapes self-knowledge.
  • Can "change" personality (Rogers, 1952).
  • Increases psychological safety; decreases negative affect.
  • Meta-analysis: listening positively associated with performance (r = 0.29).
Class 6 · Mar 23

Courage for whom?

Cases: MBA Hackers + Speed Ventures · Game-of-Thrones-themed slides

The MBA Hackers case

MBA applicants hacked into the ApplyYourself admissions system to view their admission status early. School responses ranged from blanket rejection (HBS, MIT, CMU) to individualized review (Stanford, which still admitted none of the 41).

School# HackedResponse
Harvard119All rejected. "Unethical at best."
MIT Sloan32All rejected; reapplication allowed in a year.
CMU Tepper2Both rejected.
Stanford GSB41Individual review; ultimately none admitted.

MBA Hackers takeaways

  1. Closeness matters. The closer decisions are to you — the more you resonate with the people in question — the harder it is to act on behalf of others.
  2. For whom are you acting? Leadership is not primarily about who you are acting against, but who you are acting for. Ask: "Who are my current/primary constituents?"
  3. Inputs should match outputs (congruence). Slippery slope — where does a small transgression end? Leadership is easier when you stick with pre-determined principles vs. enacting them case-by-case.

Speed Ventures takeaways

  1. Take calculated risks. Courage matters, but avoid being rash. "Going for it" sounds impressive but risk must be weighed accordingly.
  2. Avoid sampling on the dependent variable / confirmation bias. Use the entirety of available data, not just data points that confirm existing beliefs.
  3. Be aware of loss aversion. We're more likely to take risks when faced with a prospect of loss. Consider "we might lose everything" alongside "we might miss out."

The 5 W's for hard decisions

  • WHO are my primary (and all) constituents? Not just people who are physically/emotionally close.
  • WHY/WHAT are my org's core values and their implications for this decision?
  • WHERE am I focusing my lens? Myself vs. current students vs. former affiliates vs. business schools broadly.
  • WHEN do I need to respond? How quickly are competitors/comparisons responding?
  • HOW am I making this decision? Am I using all available evidence? Did I give it enough time?
Leading often means making courageous decisions. Simplify by sticking to your principles.
Class 7 · Mar 30

Organizational design & structure

Activity: Words-in-Sentences (manufacturing simulation)

Words-in-Sentences exercise

Groups "manufacture" sentences using letters from a word as raw material. Two production runs with reorganization opportunity in between. A Quality Review Board (one member from each group) scores all groups' output.

  • Each sentence: 3–6 words.
  • Sentences must make grammatical sense (need not be logical).
  • Letters can be reused between words but not within a single word.
  • Cannot repeat nouns between sentences. Verbs OK to conjugate.
  • Round 1 raw material: LEONARD N STERN. Round 2: WASHINGTON SQUARE PARK.
  • Most groups improve significantly in round 2 after reorganization.

Take-homes

  1. Structure is hard — and sometimes chaotic.
  2. This simulates real-world restructuring. Assign roles, see what works, change. Restructuring is often necessary, especially in complex/fluid environments.
  3. Many regulations make structure even more difficult.

The lecture's framing questions

  • What is organizational structure and why does it matter?
  • Why do organizations adopt different structural arrangements?
  • Why do these change over time?
  • How do those changes affect organizational processes and outcomes?
Class 8 · Apr 6

Persuasion & decision-making

Activity: Survival Exercise (stay vs. walk; rank 13 items)

Survival exercise scoring

  • Individual influence = |individual ranking − group ranking|
  • Individual expertise = |individual − expert|
  • Group expertise = |expert − group|

Expert recommendation: stay with the wreckage. Key debrief question: was the most influential person also the most expert? (Often: no.)

Liking → persuasion

People comply more with requests from people they like. Factors that increase liking: similarity, familiarity, flattery, cooperation, humor. We like people who like us.

One study: students with high ingratiatory behavior in interviews received 1.45 more offers and ~$2,650 more in starting salary on average.

Authority → persuasion

People are more persuaded by perceived authority, expertise, and credibility — independent of argument strength. Authority is sensitive to superficial cues: height, dress (suit/uniform), graying hair, eyeglasses, British accent, deep voice.

Descriptive ≠ prescriptive. Just because liking and authority tactics work doesn't mean they should. Who do these advantage? Who do they disadvantage? How could a process be redesigned to reduce reliance on them?

Bounded attention

Limited attention → limited decision-making. We "see" only a subset of perceivable information and have little awareness that we're missing anything.

Gorilla experiment: 83% of radiologists failed to notice a gorilla superimposed on a CT scan. ~100% of naive observers missed it. Yet radiologists were far better at detecting actual nodules. Expertise focuses attention but creates blind spots.

Pitfalls of group decision-making

  1. Groupthink — tendency of group members to conform to the consensus viewpoint. Asch conformity experiment: 75% of participants conformed on at least one trial; overall rate 32%.
  2. Abilene paradox — group agrees on a course of action that no individual actually wants. Everyone assumes others want it.
  3. Group polarization — groups make more extreme decisions than individuals would alone.

Counters to bounded attention

  • Different people focused on different issues — argument for diversity in teams.
  • Norms that allow for dissent.
  • Mechanisms to reconcile and synthesize divergent opinions and data.
Class 9 · Apr 13

Networking

Case: Claude Grunitzky · Activity: Murder Mystery — "Who Killed Robert Guion?"

Murder Mystery exercise

Information was distributed across three versions of packets. Groups had to share uncommon/unshared information to solve correctly. Demonstrates the importance of information sharing across networks. (Eddie did it — implicated by multiple sources, exonerated by no one.)

How groups form

  • Proximity (Festinger, Schacter, Back, 1950): we form relationships with people who are physically close. Those nearby tend to share knowledge and experiences.
  • Homophily: we like and prefer to interact with others who are similar, familiar, and close.
  • Study of 816 new entrepreneurs: biggest predictor in founding-team formation was similarity in gender, ethnicity, occupation.

Racial composition of US social networks (PRRI 2013)

  • White Americans' close networks: 91% White.
  • Black Americans' close networks: 83% Black.
  • Hispanic Americans' close networks: 64% Hispanic.
  • We overwhelmingly discuss "important matters" with people who look like us.

Homogeneous vs heterogeneous groups

Homogeneous — ProsHomogeneous — Cons
Similarity breeds likingNarrow perspectives
High cohesionLow motivation to change/learn
Low start-up costsLack of new ideas
Easy to agreeGroupthink & conformity risk
Heterogeneous — ProsHeterogeneous — Cons
Richer pools of informationHigh start-up costs
More learningHigher potential for conflict
More creativity and innovationRisk of marginalization, ostracism
More specializationDifficult to assemble

Watson, Kumar & Michaelsen (1993): homogeneous groups are often better in months 1–2, but performance differences disappear by month 4. Diverse groups have higher startup costs but eventually outperform.

Take-homes

  1. Not everyone loves networking — but the benefits are real, so push yourself.
  2. Networking is beneficial but presents costs (personal, professional, even network-related — too many close ties precludes weak ones).
  3. Balance depth (strong ties) with breadth (weak ties). Too much reliance on similarity limits perspective. Strive for new perspectives.
Class 10 · Apr 20

Motivation & designing incentives

Case: Lincoln Electric · Reading: "On the Folly of Rewarding A While Hoping for B" (Kerr) · Super-Mario-Bros-themed slides

Motivation defined

The psychological forces within a person that determine the direction of behavior, level of effort, and persistence in the face of obstacles. If employees are unmotivated, their skills and abilities are wasted.

Sources of motivation

  • Situational: extrinsic rewards (pay-for-performance, piece-rates, commissions, promotions) and intrinsic rewards (challenge, growth, fairness, equity).
  • Individual: personality differences — need for achievement, power, growth, affiliation.

Motivation theories

TheoryCore idea
Maslow / Alderfer's ERGHierarchy of needs (physiological → safety → social → esteem → self-actualization). People care about more than just money.
Two-Factor (Herzberg)Motivators (achievement, recognition, growth): presence motivates, absence is neutral. Hygiene factors (pay, conditions, policies): presence doesn't motivate, but absence demotivates.
ExpectancyMotivation = f(expectancy, instrumentality, valence). Effort → performance → outcome → value. If any link breaks, motivation collapses.
EquityPeople evaluate input/output ratios against past precedents and peers. Perceived inequity → less motivation. Fairness is an incredibly powerful motivator.
Goal-settingSpecific, challenging-yet-realistic goals beat "do your best." 50 years of empirical support. Commitment is higher when employees are involved in goal-setting.

Ultimatum game: proposers offer ~40% (many offer 50/50). Majority of responders reject offers below 40%, even with high stakes. People will sacrifice money to punish unfairness.

Pay for performance

Compensation = s + b · x
s = salary (fixed) · b = rate of reward contingent on performance · x = performance measure (often imperfect)
Three models: piece-rate only (s=0), salary only (b=0), mixed.

Downsides of pay-for-performance

  • Performance is hard to measure accurately.
  • Can undermine teamwork (especially with relative payouts).
  • Encourages short-term focus over learning.
  • Encourages unethical behavior, "fudging" the numbers.
  • Once introduced, hard to change — people are distrustful of pay changes.

"On the Folly of Rewarding A While Hoping for B"

Kerr's classic: misaligned incentives are everywhere.

  • Vietnam War: military goal was "win"; GI goal was "get home alive."
  • Consulting: progress reports written by the same people who recommended the change.
  • Doctors, students, budgeting: same pattern.

Take-homes

  1. Incentive systems are hard. Hard to measure everything that matters and get the "right" system.
  2. Pay-for-performance may seem like a panacea — but it has real downsides (health, risk, undermining intrinsic benefits, precluding teamwork, oversight cost).
  3. Incentive clashes are common: orgs reward one dimension when many are relevant; multiple metrics interfere; employees value unquantifiable things; extrinsic clashes with intrinsic.
Class 11 · Apr 27

Collaborative learning — failure & feedback

Case: Wolfgang Keller at Königsbräu · Activity: Smith-Burke performance review role-play

The Wolfgang Keller case

Keller (34, HBS MBA, turnaround artist, hands-on, blunt, impatient) is managing Brodsky (44, formal, delegatory, slow, analytical). Different styles. Keller's reservations: too distant, too slow, too passive, too formal, not proactive, too delegatory.

The case decision: (a) fire Brodsky / deny salary increase; (b) coach him up; (c) re-organize / restructure; (d) wait and see.

Why feedback is essential

  • It's hard to know how we're truly performing or how we're "seen" by others.
  • Many self-protective mechanisms get in the way.
  • To improve, you must know how you're actually performing.
  • Self-assessment accuracy is itself a leadership skill.

Errors in performance appraisals

  • Stereotyping — placing an employee in a category based on one or a few traits.
  • Overestimation — people overestimate their relative performance.
  • Unskilled-and-unaware (Dunning-Kruger) — least skilled overestimate competency; most skilled underestimate it.

Why most feedback is useless

  • Sugarcoated: painful to give, painful to receive — managers withhold or distort positively.
  • Too late: by the time it's said, it's irrelevant.
  • Too vague: doesn't say what to actually change.
  • Inconsistent: evaluations and ratings don't match. Standard deviation matters — averages hide everything.

Self-serving bias

Success → internal/personal attributes ("I'm clearly awesome"). Failure → situational factors ("my boss doesn't like me"). It blinds us from honest feedback and is really annoying when observed in others.

Fair process effect

People are more willing to accept outcomes — good or bad — if the process to determine those outcomes is fair. It's not just what you do, it's how you do it.

Six criteria for procedural fairness

  1. Are rules and procedures applied consistently across individuals?
  2. Are decisions made impartially, unaffected by self-interest?
  3. Are decisions made using accurate information?
  4. Is there opportunity for appeal or correction after decisions are made?
  5. Are all stakeholders represented — do they have a voice?
  6. Do procedures and decisions follow moral / ethical standards?

Plus: quality of interpersonal treatment (dignity, respect, neutrality, explanation of decisions).

It doesn't matter if you think you're acting fairly — benefits emerge only when you're seen to be fair. Fair procedures make unfavorable outcomes especially more acceptable.

Preventing surprise

  • As feedback recipient (Burke): manage up. Ensure your manager sees your situation as you see it.
  • As feedback giver (Smith): employees should never be surprised. Provide ongoing candid feedback.
  • Procedural fairness is critical for sustaining motivation.

Take-homes

  1. The feedback process is tough — task-based feedback easily spills into relationship-based tension. Stay focused on aligned goals.
  2. Giving feedback: let the recipient speak first (gives voice). Make it concrete, actionable, task-focused — not personal or vague. Don't wait.
  3. Receiving feedback: overcome self-serving bias. Treat feedback as a learning opportunity (even if you disagree). Focus on process over outcome.
Class 12 · May 4

Values, norms, inclusivity / wrap-up

Activities: Barnga card game · Cross-cultural quiz · Gold Watch exercise · Reading: "Can Your Employees Really Speak Freely?" · Case Memo 2 (Wolfgang Keller) due

Barnga card game (cultural simulation)

Trick-taking card game played silently in small groups. Each table has slightly different rules; winning pairs rotate to higher-numbered tables across rounds. No one is told the rules differ, and after Round 1 no one can talk — only physical gestures (and "enforcers" penalize violations).

  • Diagnosing culture is hard. The "rules" are unwritten and only show up when you break them.
  • It's hard not to punish/exclude those who "don't fit in" — even when newcomers are doing exactly what worked at their old table. Newcomers don't add zero value; they're just operating on a different rulebook.
  • It's hard to learn the "rules" if you're new. Asking trusted sources accelerates learning.
  • Approach life more like Round 3 — give people grace as they're learning. Cultural rules change, and not everyone gets it on day one.

Culture matters (cross-cultural quiz)

Compliment a German client's pen set → they tell you where to buy one. Looking an elder in the eye is rude in Nigeria. Scratching your head in Russia means "I am The Machine." Ariana Grande's "7 Rings" tattoo translated to "BBQ Grill" in Japanese. Culture matters, and the cost of getting it wrong is real.

Morris & Peng (1994): shown a single fish swimming ahead of a school, people from the US see an individual leading; people from China see a group chasing. Same image, different culture, different story.

What is organizational culture?

The set of values, attitudes, beliefs, and expected behavior shared by members of an organization — its "internal personality." What's really important or accepted, which can differ from what management says.

Glassdoor / Fast Company: top three reasons people quit are (1) company culture, (2) salary, (3) getting stuck in the same job.

Schein's three levels of culture (the iceberg)

LevelWhat it isExamples
Artifacts (tip)Tangible — what you can see, hear, observe.Foosball tables, ball pits, bringing dogs to work.
Espoused valuesStated principles, standards, goals."Bring your whole self to work." "Work hard, party hard." "Rockstars and Ninjas!"
Assumptions (underwater)Taken-for-granted beliefs about reality. Often unspoken, out of immediate awareness. Discerned from how people justify what they do."We give you free stuff so don't have a life outside work." "If you're over X, or have family obligations, don't bother applying."
Foosball or fool's ball? The visible perks (artifacts) are the smallest part. Most of culture lives below the waterline — and that's where it actually shapes behavior.

Six content dimensions of organizational culture

  • Competitiveness — compete vs. cooperate.
  • Innovativeness — encouraged to challenge the status quo, or not.
  • Conflict tolerance — air conflicts openly, or not.
  • Individual initiative — degree of responsibility, freedom, independence.
  • Tolerance of failure — accept failure vs. demand success.
  • Power distance — hierarchy emphasized vs. equals.

How culture is manifested

  • Symbols — objects/acts/events that convey meaning (and the org's values).
  • Stories — true-event narratives shared and retold to new employees.
  • Heroes — figures who exemplify the culture; role models.
  • Slogans — phrases that succinctly express a key value.
  • Ceremonies — planned events for an audience.

Why culture matters

  1. Guides and controls behavior — provides cues to what's expected, accepted, encouraged, valued. Exerts conformity pressure through desire for acceptance.
  2. Provides identity to members.
  3. Generates commitment to the org's goals.
  4. A strong culture creates dedication and motivation often not achievable through formal means.
  5. Fit between culture and strategy, structure, and systems is critical.

Adaptive vs. non-adaptive cultures

AdaptiveNon-adaptive
Risk-takingBureaucratic
Open to change and innovationReactive and risk-averse
Value entrepreneurshipInformation doesn't flow easily
Managers continuously align practices with environmental realitiesEmphasis on maintaining current processes

Kotter & Heskett-style data — 32 companies, 11 years:

"Never fail to fail" (Susan Wojcicki, YouTube). "Reward thoughtful failure" (Laszlo Bock, Google). "Fail often to succeed sooner" (Tim Brown, IDEO). Adaptive cultures explicitly reward intelligent risk-taking.

Cultural strength

  • Strong: values, assumptions, beliefs are widely held and agreed upon — exert strong impact on behavior.
  • Weak: disagreement or confusion around values — behavior is shaped through formalized systems and bureaucracy instead.

Culture fit vs. culture add

Hiring for "fit" feels safe but has real downsides:

  • Reinforces lack of diversity.
  • Provides cover for bias.
  • Misses talent — referrals tend to mine similar networks.
  • Bakes unintended bias into products: airbags initially safer for men than women/children; AI facial recognition that fails for non-white faces.

Inclusive leadership

Mindset + Noticing + Challenging.

  • Mindset — Growth (vs. fixed) / self-threat. Aim to "be good-ish," not perfect.
  • Noticing — see through the SMOG (the everyday haze of micro-exclusions you stop registering).
  • Challenging — model behavior and speak up. Small steps add up. Use your ordinary privilege. Simple ≠ easy; courage and difficult conversations required.

Diagnosing a culture (espoused vs. enacted)

  • What do I have to do/say to fit in? Get the boss's attention? Get ahead? Feel part of the group?
  • What's really important around here — and how do things actually get done?
Espoused valuesEnacted values
Stated values (mission statements). The "desired" culture.Perceived values. The "real" culture.
Misalignment example: "We really value you — here's some pizza!" but the daily reality is "we treat you like crap." Result: confusion, turnover, demoralization. Alignment is needed both between espoused and enacted, and with the values of organizational members.

How culture forms and reinforces itself

  • Founders hire and keep employees who think and feel the way they do.
  • Founders socialize them via training, mentors, rewards (formal and informal) — "learning the ropes."
  • The founders' own behavior is a role model that pulls people into identifying with their beliefs and values.

Attraction-Selection-Attrition (ASA) theory

The culture of a whole organization is largely a product of the personalities of its workers.

  • Similar personalities are attracted to the same kind of org.
  • Similar personalities are selected by the same kind of org.
  • People with different personalities attrite — they leave.
  • Net effect: birds of a feather flock together, and culture self-reinforces.

The Gold Watch exercise (subtle value differences)

Read a short story; rank the six characters (Kim, Mario, Charles, Claire, Ms. Grey, Jane) from most to least objectionable. Same characters read differently depending on the lens you bring: Double-crosser or opportunistic? Shady or savvy? Dictator or man of his word? Unethical or supportive friend? Loan shark or helpful family friend? "Snitches get stitches" or "see something, say something"?

  1. Subtle value differences sit at the heart of most cultural conflicts. Different members of your team will see the same situation differently.
  2. Whom you personally identify with most strongly shapes how you read ambiguous situations. Avoid favoring only those viewpoints (the common knowledge effect).
  3. Past experiences inevitably shape perception. Try to individuate each situation and person — not pattern-match — to lead inclusively.

Bringing it home — four threads of the course

  • Push + Pull — logic + emotion, IQ + EQ. Neither alone moves people.
  • Pluralistic ignorance — many people privately disagree with a norm but each thinks they're alone, so the norm holds. Naming it breaks it.
  • Process over outcome — fair process effect. How you decide matters as much as what you decide.
  • Courage — to act and create change, and to sit down and listen.

Reframing success and failure

"Success is the ability to go from one failure to another with no loss of enthusiasm" (Churchill). Failure on challenging tasks is a near-certainty if individuals and groups don't learn from it.

  • Success and failure are not end states or labels — they're stages in a process, information to be used.
  • "The greatest barrier to success is fear of failure." Mild fear is motivating; moderate-to-extreme fear is debilitating.
  • Learning from failure: identify the underlying cause → correct issues and build new processes → make space for future success → share "worst" practices.
  • Paradox of success: doing good work leads to being asked to do more with less. Without a long-term plan and clear definition of success, you can't tell if you're winning.
  • Iceberg illusion (Sylvia Duckworth): the public sees "SUCCESS!" — the persistence, failure, sacrifice, dedication, hard work, and good habits underneath are invisible.

Final Leadership Commitments Plan (LCP)

5–7 pages double-spaced · Due May 10, 9pm · 25% of final grade · +5% for peer evaluation

The LCP is the capstone — a personal document built up across the semester through Exercises A and B, peer drafts, and feedback rounds. The final asks eight questions:

  1. What is your leadership vision, and how is it informed by your values — personal, cultural, and/or generational? How will you influence others going forward?
  2. What are your motivations, including goals and near-term priorities?
  3. How will you use and prioritize inputs and resources (people, time, money) to achieve your goals?
  4. How will you define and measure success?
  5. Where do you anticipate (mis)alignment and conflicts? How will you approach and resolve them?
  6. What are the 3 biggest risks to achieving your vision and goals? If you're unhappy in 5 years, what's the most likely reason?
  7. How will you ensure that you and others around you make good (or at least better) decisions?
  8. How do the lessons of creating a Leadership Commitments Plan apply to leading an organization?
