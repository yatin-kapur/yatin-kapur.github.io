Key ideas (full course — Lectures 1 through 11)

Refreshed after each lecture. Each lecture section also has its own "✦ remember this" callout at the end.

  1. A dollar today is worth more than a dollar later. Every valuation question reduces to: discount future cash flows by the right rate and add them up. PV, FV, annuity, perpetuity — different shapes of the same idea. Compounding is the snowball; discounting is the snowball in reverse.
  2. APR ≠ EAR. APR is a quoting convention (the per-period rate times the number of compounding periods per year). EAR is the actual annual rate you earn or pay. Always convert to EAR before comparing rates with different compounding frequencies.
  3. Continuous compounding is the limit as you compound more and more often. Continuous rates add over time instead of compounding multiplicatively, which is why all of derivatives pricing uses them.
  4. Annuities and perpetuities collapse infinite sums into one formula via the geometric series. Annuity PVAF = (1 − (1+r)−N)/r; perpetuity = C/r; growing perpetuity = C/(r−g) when r > g.
  5. Investors prefer more to less, and almost all are risk averse. Risk averse means: for a given expected return, prefer lower volatility; for a given volatility, prefer higher expected return. When one portfolio has higher E[R] AND higher σ, the call depends on the investor's risk-aversion level.
  6. Markets have structure. Limit orders provide liquidity (you sit on the book at a price); market orders demand immediacy (you cross the spread and pay the cost). Bid-ask spread is the price of immediacy and the profit of liquidity providers. Dealers manage inventory toward zero.
  7. The three ways to hold a stock — long, on margin, short — map to portfolio weights ω: in (0,1], >1 with negative riskless weight, <0 respectively. Percentage margin = Net Worth / Value of Stock; if it falls below maintenance margin, you get a margin call.
  8. The portfolio return formula R_p = Σ ωi Ri handles long, levered, and short positions with the same equation — only the signs and magnitudes of weights change. Weights sum to 1.
  9. Risk = standard deviation (for now). A return distribution is summarized by E[R] (its center) and σ (its spread). Covariance and correlation describe how two assets move together — and that joint behavior is the whole point of diversification.
  10. Regression slope = covariance / variance. b = σ[Y,X] / σ²[X]. When we run R_stock on R_market in Lecture 5, this slope becomes beta, the central risk measure in CAPM.
  11. One risky + one riskless = a Capital Allocation Line in (σ, E[R]) space. Slope = (E[Ri] − Rf) / σ[Ri] = reward per unit of risk. Between two risky assets, every risk-averse investor picks the one with the steeper CAL, regardless of their personal risk aversion.
  12. Two-fund separation (preview). Risk aversion determines where on the CAL you sit (more averse = closer to Rf, less averse = further out / on margin). It does NOT determine which CAL — that's a universal choice. This is the seed of CAPM.
  13. With two risky assets, the portfolio possibility curve bows leftward as correlation drops. Lower ρ → more diversification benefit. At ρ = −1 you can construct a riskless portfolio. Min-variance portfolio: ω₁* = (σ₂² − σ1,2) / (σ₁² + σ₂² − 2σ1,2).
  14. Adding a riskless asset changes the efficient frontier from a curve to a straight line. The CAL through the tangency portfolio T dominates every point on the curved frontier except T itself.
  15. Tangency portfolio T: for 2 assets, closed form using excess returns and covariances. For N assets, wT = Σ⁻¹μexcess / (1TΣ⁻¹μexcess). Max Sharpe ratio: √(μexcessTΣ⁻¹μexcess).
  16. For N assets, equal-weighted portfolio variance decomposes as σ²/N + (1 − 1/N) × avg covariance. First term → 0 (diversifiable / idiosyncratic risk). Second term → average covariance (systematic / undiversifiable risk). Even infinite diversification can't get below √ρ̄ × σ.
  17. Minimum variance frontier (MVF) is the leftmost boundary of achievable (σ, E[R]) — a hyperbola in (σ, μ) space, parabola in (σ², μ) space. Efficient frontier = upper half. Adding risky assets always shifts MVF left.
  18. Two-fund separation theorem: with N risky assets + Rf, every risk-averse mean-variance investor holds the same tangency portfolio T, just in different mix with Rf. The asset-selection problem and the risk-level problem fully decouple. This is the engine of CAPM.
  19. No arbitrage / law of one price. Two assets with identical cash flows must have the same price. Any price difference (after taxes, liquidity, transaction costs) is an arbitrage opportunity, and well-functioning markets don't let those last.
  20. CAPM: in equilibrium, the tangency portfolio T = the market portfolio M. Every investor holds Rf + M. The pricing equation is the SML: E[Ri] = Rf + βi,M·(E[RM] − Rf). βi,M = σ[Ri, RM] / σ²[RM] is the slope of regressing Ri on RM.
  21. CML vs SML: every asset is on the SML; only assets with ρ[Ri, RM] = 1 are also on the CML. Same β = same E[R] (SML rules), but inefficient assets (ρ < 1) plot to the right of the CML — they carry idiosyncratic risk that doesn't get rewarded.
  22. Why beta and not σ? Everyone holds M. The piece of σ[Ri] that's uncorrelated with M diversifies away in M. Only the correlated piece — captured by β — gets priced. σ²[Ri] = β²i,M·σ²[RM] + σ²[ei] (systematic + idiosyncratic).
  23. Performance: Jensen's α vs Sharpe. Jensen's αi = E[ri] − βi,M·E[rM] — use when the asset is one of many in a CAPM-optimal portfolio (α > 0 → underpriced). Sharpe = (E[Ri] − Rf) / σ[Ri] — use when the asset is the investor's only risky holding. They can disagree on rankings.
  24. CAPM fails empirically. Small-cap and value stocks earn persistent positive alpha. SML is too flat. Beta alone doesn't explain the cross-section.
  25. Multifactor extensions (post-midterm). ICAPM: investors care about state variables too, so M need not be tangency. Fama-French 3-factor (market + SMB + HML) is the empirical workhorse — explains the size and value premia.
  26. Bond pricing = PV of remaining cash flows. The unifying idea of all fixed income. T-bills: one cash flow, one discount. Coupon bonds: stream of coupons + par at maturity, each cash flow discounted at its own strip yield. The single-rate YTM is what you back out after, not the engine.
  27. Discount factor dT(0) = pT(0)/C = price of $1 paid for sure at time T. Most useful single object in L8 — every bond price is Σ dt·C(t). Recover the zero curve from coupon notes by bootstrapping, one maturity at a time.
  28. A coupon bond = a portfolio of zeros. Treasury STRIPS makes this physical; the law of one price makes it binding. Mispricing creates a clean arbitrage with strips.
  29. YTM of a coupon bond ≠ yield on a same-maturity zero unless the yield curve is flat. Upward-sloping curve → YTM(coupon) < yT(0); higher coupon rate pulls YTM further from yT.
  30. Default risk wedges YTM above E[R]. Corporate YTMs > equivalent Treasury YTMs for two reasons: (1) expected cash flow < promised; (2) defaults correlate with bad market states, so β > 0.
  31. Holding period return: HPR equals starting YTM only for the ½-year strip. For longer bonds, yields up during the period → HPR < YTMstart. This is the seed of duration.
  32. The conversion identity (most-tested fixed-income formula): (1+yt+α(0)/2)2(t+α) = (1+yt(0)/2)2t·(1+ft,t+α(0)/2)2α. Equivalently dt+α(0) = dt(0)·dt,t+α(0). Use the discount-factor shortcut for forwards: dt,t+α(0) = dt+α(0)/dt(0).
  33. FRA naming: "t × (t+α) FRA" with both in months. f½,1(0) is the 6×12 FRA; f5,7(0) is the 60×84.
  34. Expectations Hypothesis (EH): forward rate = expected future ½-year spot. Under EH, yield curve slope cleanly signals expected future rates: upward → market expects higher; downward → lower.
  35. Liquidity preference (empirical consensus): longer strips need a maturity-increasing illiquidity premium → forwards are upward-biased predictors of future spots. Upward-sloping curves are common regardless of expectations. Downward-sloping still implies expected rate cuts.
  36. Yield curve slope predicts GDP growth. Positive slope → low short rates → high investment → strong future growth. One of the best macro predictors.
  37. Duration. Macaulay D = Σ t·k(t), the PV-weighted average payment time. Zero bond: D = maturity; coupon bond: D < maturity; higher coupon → shorter D. Modified D* = D/(1+y/2) is price sensitivity: ΔP/P ≈ −D*·Δy. Portfolio duration is value-weighted: DC = ωADA + ωBDB.
  38. Immunization. Dedication = exact cash-flow match (no rebalancing). Target-date = match both value (L = A) and duration (DL = DA), neutralizing reinvestment vs liquidation risk; re-immunize as durations drift.
  39. Derivative payoffs (Lynch's X notation). Call holder max{S(T)−X, 0}; put holder max{X−S(T), 0}; writer = negative of holder. Forward buyer S(T)−FT(0); seller FT(0)−S(T). Derivatives are zero net supply.
  40. Put-call parity: S(0) = CX,T(0) − PX,T(0) + X·dT(0). Always discount the strike (X·dT(0), never raw X).
  41. No-arbitrage option floors (intrinsic value): C ≥ max{S(0) − X·dT(0), 0}; P ≥ max{X·dT(0) − S(0), 0}. Price − intrinsic = time value ≥ 0. American ≥ European; non-dividend call is never exercised early (c = C); American put may be (p ≥ P, no closed form).
  42. Black-Scholes: CX,T(0) = S(0)N(d₁) − Xe−r′TN(d₂), e−r′T = dT(0). Signs C(S+, X−, σ+, r′+, T+), P(S−, X+, σ+, r′−, T?). σ raises both call and put (payoff truncated at 0); the stock's expected return never enters. Implied vol = the σ that matches the market price.
  43. The law of one price is the spine of the second half. Build a synthetic copy of a contract from things you can already price, line up cash flows in a Time-0/Time-T table, and no-arbitrage forces the contract to equal its synthetic. This one move gives put-call parity, the option floors, forward-spot parity, covered interest parity, and the swap rate.
  44. Forward-spot parity: FT(0) = S(0)/dT(0) = S(0)[1+y*T(0)]T; with carry, FT(0) = [S(0)+C(τ)dτ(0)]/dT(0). The forward price is the FV of spot, not a forecast. Covered interest parity: the higher-yielding currency trades at a forward discount. Futures = daily-settled forwards.
  45. Swap = a portfolio of two bonds. Fixed-rate payer = long a floating bond + short a fixed bond. The swap rate is set so PV(fixed) = PV(floating), making the swap worth 0 at inception. Only the net difference changes hands → low credit risk; rates rise → fixed-rate payer wins.
  46. Valuation = PV of expected cash flows at the SML rate. DDM / Gordon: P₀ = E[D₁]/(E[R]−g), so E[R] = dividend yield + g. Capital budgeting: accept NPV > 0, discounting incremental cash flows at the project's β (ignore sunk costs). Growth g = b·ROE; reinvest only when ROE > E[R].
  47. Market efficiency is the closing warning. Price = unbiased estimate of value; realized = expected + mean-zero surprise. Three forms (weak/semi-strong/strong); U.S. markets ≈ semi-strong, so average mutual-fund α < 0 and good performance doesn't persist. Autocorrelation ≠ inefficiency if expected returns vary; every efficiency test is jointly a test of the pricing model.

Overview - what finance is about

Asset classes

  • Real assets: physical things - land, buildings, machines.
  • Financial assets (securities): claims on real or financial assets. Four flavors:
    • Money - medium of exchange.
    • Debt - claim to a predetermined payment stream.
    • Equity - residual claim on a firm's assets, usually with corporate control rights.
    • Derivatives - payoff depends on the value of another asset.

What the financial system does

  1. Allocates capital from savers to capital users; disciplines firm investment decisions.
  2. Lets investors smooth consumption over time.
  3. Repackages risk so investors bear less of it.
  4. Disseminates information (prices aggregate beliefs).
  5. Lets investors exploit economies of scale in trading.

Markets

  • Primary vs secondary: primary = new issues sold to initial buyers; secondary = previously issued securities trade among investors.
  • Money vs capital: money = short-term debt (< 1 yr maturity); capital = long-term debt and equity.
  • Mutual funds & index funds: low-cost vehicles for individuals to hold diversified portfolios. The S&P 500 is value-weighted (more $ in big firms), 500 stocks chosen to mirror the U.S. market.

Core concepts the course returns to

  • Investor preferences: all prefer more to less; almost all are risk averse.
  • Time value of money: a dollar today > a dollar later.
  • Diversification: portfolio volatility falls as assets are added; average returns are unchanged.
  • Risk adjustment: riskier assets must offer higher expected returns.
  • No arbitrage: two assets with the same cash flows must have the same price (law of one price). Differences only arise from taxes, liquidity, or transaction costs.
  • Option value: a right without an obligation has non-negative value today.
  • Market efficiency: in an efficient market, price is an unbiased estimate of value. The U.S. stock market is probably efficient w.r.t. publicly available info → you can't beat it on average using public info, risk-adjusted.
✦ remember this — Lecture 1
  • Four asset types: money, debt, equity, derivatives. Three things the financial system does that matter: allocate capital, smooth consumption, repackage risk.
  • Seven core concepts the course returns to: risk aversion, time value of money, diversification, risk adjustment, no arbitrage, option value, market efficiency. Everything that follows is just formal machinery for one of these.
  • Mutual funds (especially index funds) exist because of market efficiency. If you can't beat the market on average using public info, the rational play is to own the market cheaply.

Lectures 1–2 - Time Value of Money

The atomic operation of finance: take a cash flow at one date and figure out its equivalent value at another date, given an interest rate. Everything else stacks on top of this.

Single cash flows (discrete compounding)

Future value

\[ V_{t+n} \;=\; V_t \, (1 + r)^n \]

Money in a bank earns interest. Put $100 in a 5% account: one year later it's $105. The next year, that whole $105 earns 5%, so you get $110.25 - slightly more than $110, because you also earned interest on the interest. The (1+r)n just stacks that little bit of "interest on interest" n times.

Think of it like a snowball rolling downhill: each period it picks up new snow proportional to how big it already is, so it grows faster the longer it rolls. That's compounding - and it's why finance people obsess over starting to invest early.

Start with one period. By definition of "effective per-period rate r," money at t grows to:

\[ V_{t+1} \;=\; V_t (1 + r) \]

Apply the same rule again to Vt+1:

\[ V_{t+2} \;=\; V_{t+1}(1+r) \;=\; V_t (1+r)^2 \]

Iterating n times stacks the factor:

\[ V_{t+n} \;=\; V_t (1+r)^n \]

This is just "interest on interest" written compactly. The exponent counts how many compoundings happen between the two dates.

Present value

\[ V_t \;=\; V_{t+n} \, (1 + r)^{-n} \]

If someone promises you $110.25 in two years, how much would you pay for that promise today? You'd pay less than $110.25 - because if you had the money now, you could invest it and grow it yourself. At a 5% rate, you'd pay exactly $100.

"Discounting" is just compounding run in reverse. The further in the future the cash, the less it's worth today. This is the single most important idea in finance: every valuation - stocks, bonds, businesses - is some version of "what's this future cash worth today?"

Solve the future-value formula for Vt. Divide both sides by (1+r)n:

\[ V_t \;=\; \frac{V_{t+n}}{(1+r)^n} \;=\; V_{t+n} (1+r)^{-n} \]

So present-valuing is just future-valuing in reverse - discount instead of compound.

Effective n-period rate from the per-period rate

\[ r_n \;=\; (1 + r)^n - 1 \]

If you earn 5% per year for two years, your total return isn't 10% - it's slightly more (10.25%) because of compounding. This formula converts a "per-period" rate into the actual cumulative growth rate over n periods.

Useful when you need to compare returns over different timeframes: "is 8% over 5 years better than 7% over 6 years?" Convert both to the same horizon using this formula.

By definition, the effective n-period rate rn satisfies:

\[ V_{t+n} \;=\; V_t (1 + r_n) \]

But the future-value formula says Vt+n = Vt (1+r)n. Equating the two:

\[ V_t (1+r_n) \;=\; V_t (1+r)^n \quad\Rightarrow\quad 1 + r_n \;=\; (1+r)^n \]

Subtract 1 to get the formula. The "n-period rate" is just the cumulative growth factor minus one.

r is the effective rate per period, expressed as a decimal. The period must match the interval between cash flows - if cash flows are monthly, r is the monthly rate.

APR (nominal rate) vs EAR (effective annual rate)

The APR inom with m compounding periods per year is the per-period rate times m. It's a quoting convention, not a real rate. The EAR is the actual annual rate you earn.

Definition: per-period rate from APR

\[ r_{1/m} \;=\; \frac{i_{\text{nom}}}{m} \]

APR ("annual percentage rate") is just the banks' favorite quoting convention. When your credit card says "18% APR with monthly compounding," they really charge you 18% ÷ 12 = 1.5% per month. APR is the headline number; the per-period rate is what actually compounds.

So APR alone is meaningless without knowing how often it compounds. Two different lenders quoting the same APR can be charging very different amounts.

By convention, the APR is the per-period rate times the number of periods per year - a quoting rule, nothing more.

APR → EAR

\[ \text{EAR} \;=\; \left( 1 + \frac{i_{\text{nom}}}{m} \right)^{\!m} - 1 \]

EAR is the real annual cost - what you'd compare across loans. APR is the quoted number; EAR is what it actually costs you over a year after compounding.

An 18% APR compounded monthly is really 19.56% per year. The extra 1.56% is interest charged on interest you've already accrued during the year. The more frequently a loan compounds (daily > monthly > annually), the bigger the gap between APR and EAR.

The rule of thumb: if you're comparing two loans with the same APR, the one that compounds more frequently is the worse deal.

The per-period rate is r1/m = inom/m. A year contains m compounding periods, so applying the effective-n-period-rate formula with n = m:

\[ \text{EAR} \;=\; (1 + r_{1/m})^m - 1 \;=\; \left(1 + \tfrac{i_{\text{nom}}}{m}\right)^m - 1 \]

Two APRs with the same headline number compound differently if m differs - e.g., 18% APR compounded monthly (EAR ≈ 19.56%) vs daily (EAR ≈ 19.72%).

EAR → APR

\[ i_{\text{nom}} \;=\; \left[ (1 + r)^{1/m} - 1 \right] m \]

The reverse direction: you know what an account actually earns per year (EAR), and you want to back out the per-period rate so you can compare against a bank's APR quote, or use it in a monthly cash-flow calculation.

This is the operation a mortgage calculator performs internally - given an EAR, figure out the monthly rate to apply to the loan balance each month.

If the effective annual rate is r, the effective (1/m)-year rate satisfies:

\[ (1 + r_{1/m})^m \;=\; 1 + r \quad\Rightarrow\quad r_{1/m} \;=\; (1+r)^{1/m} - 1 \]

By definition, the APR is m × r1/m. Multiplying through gives the formula.

Watch out: APR with monthly compounding ≠ APR with annual compounding even if they're the same number. Always convert to EAR before comparing rates with different compounding frequencies.

Continuous compounding

Continuous rate from / to effective rate

\[ r' \;=\; \ln(1 + r) \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad r \;=\; e^{r'} - 1 \]

Imagine compounding interest more and more often: yearly, then monthly, daily, hourly, every second… each increase in frequency gives you a little more. You might guess "infinitely often = infinite money," but it doesn't - the gains taper off and approach a hard limit. That limit is the natural exponential function ex.

Continuous compounding is just the idealization of "compounded as often as possible." If a bank quotes an effective annual rate of 12%, the continuously compounded equivalent is about 11.33% - slightly lower, because continuous compounding makes each tiny bit of interest work harder.

Why bother? Because the math is way cleaner. Continuous rates add over time (5% for 2 years = 10% total), while discrete rates multiply ((1.05)2). Almost everything in derivatives pricing is in continuous time for this reason.

Start with an APR of inom compounded m times per year. The EAR is:

\[ \text{EAR}_m \;=\; \left(1 + \tfrac{i_{\text{nom}}}{m}\right)^m - 1 \]

Let m → ∞ (compound infinitely many times per year). Use the standard limit:

\[ \lim_{m \to \infty} \left(1 + \tfrac{x}{m}\right)^m \;=\; e^x \]

(This is one definition of e; equivalently ex = Σ xk/k!.) So:

\[ \text{EAR}_\infty \;=\; e^{i_{\text{nom}}} - 1 \]

Calling the continuously compounded rate r' = inom and the effective annual rate r = EAR∞:

\[ r \;=\; e^{r'} - 1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad r' \;=\; \ln(1+r) \]

Intuition: continuous compounding pushes you from a discrete (1+r/m)m to the smooth exponential er'. For small rates, r' ≈ r (since ln(1+r) ≈ r).

FV and PV with continuous compounding

\[ V_{t+n} \;=\; V_t \, e^{n \, r'} \qquad V_t \;=\; V_{t+n} \, e^{-n \, r'} \]

Same idea as discrete FV/PV - money grows or shrinks at a rate - but with the smooth exponential ex in place of (1+r)n. Time enters the formula as a smooth continuous variable instead of in discrete chunks.

The key magic: the exponent is just (rate × time). So a 5% continuous rate for 3 years grows your money by e0.15. To run it backwards (PV), you put a minus sign in the exponent. Nothing fancier than that.

Plug 1 + r = er' into the discrete FV formula:

\[ V_{t+n} \;=\; V_t (1+r)^n \;=\; V_t \left(e^{r'}\right)^n \;=\; V_t \, e^{n r'} \]

PV is the algebraic inverse. The exponent scales linearly with time, which is the property that makes continuous rates so clean.

Continuously compounded n-period rate

\[ r'_n \;=\; n \, r' \]

Discrete rates compound: 5% per year for 2 years is (1.05)2 − 1 = 10.25%, not 10%. Continuous rates just add: 5% per year for 2 years is exactly 10% total continuous.

This is the entire reason continuous compounding exists in finance theory - addition is simpler than multiplication, especially when you're stacking many different time periods, like in options pricing where you compound from "today" through a moving expiration date.

By definition the n-period continuous rate satisfies Vt+n/Vt = er'n. From the FV formula above, that ratio is en r'. So:

\[ e^{r'_n} \;=\; e^{n r'} \quad\Rightarrow\quad r'_n \;=\; n \, r' \]

Continuous rates simply add over time, instead of compounding multiplicatively like discrete rates do. This is why Black-Scholes is written in continuous time - the algebra is dramatically cleaner.

Used heavily in derivatives pricing (Black-Scholes) because continuous rates add instead of compounding multiplicatively - much cleaner algebra.

Annuities - N equal cash flows at equal intervals

First cash flow at t+1, last at t+N. r = effective per-period rate.

PV annuity factor - value one period BEFORE the first cash flow

\[ \text{PVAF}(r, N) \;=\; \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{r} \qquad V_t \;=\; C \cdot \text{PVAF}(r, N) \]

An annuity is just a stream of equal payments - like a salary, a mortgage payment, or a fixed pension. To value the whole stream today, you'd discount each future payment back to today and add them all up.

Doing that summation by hand for 360 mortgage payments would be miserable. The PVAF is a shortcut: a single number you multiply by the per-period payment C to get the present value. Think of it as "how many years of cash you're effectively buying" - a 30-year mortgage at 6% has PVAF ≈ 167 monthly periods, meaning $400K today buys $400K/167 ≈ $2,398/month.

The factor is always less than N because future dollars are worth less than today's.

The annuity pays C at t+1, t+2, …, t+N. The PV is the sum of PVs of each cash flow:

\[ V_t \;=\; \frac{C}{(1+r)} + \frac{C}{(1+r)^2} + \cdots + \frac{C}{(1+r)^N} \]

Factor out C and recognize a finite geometric series with first term a = 1/(1+r) and common ratio q = 1/(1+r):

\[ V_t \;=\; C \sum_{k=1}^{N} q^k \;=\; C \cdot a \cdot \frac{1 - q^N}{1 - q} \]

Plug in a and q:

\[ V_t \;=\; C \cdot \frac{1}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{1 - \tfrac{1}{1+r}} \]

The denominator simplifies: 1 − 1/(1+r) = r/(1+r). So:

\[ V_t \;=\; C \cdot \frac{1}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{r/(1+r)} \;=\; C \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{r} \]

The "magic" is just the geometric series sum formula. Sanity checks:

  • If N = 1: PVAF = (1 − (1+r)−1)/r = 1/(1+r). ✓ (one cash flow, one period away)
  • If N → ∞: PVAF → 1/r - that's the perpetuity formula. ✓

FV annuity factor - value AT the last cash flow

\[ \text{FVAF}(r, N) \;=\; \frac{(1+r)^N - 1}{r} \qquad V_{t+N} \;=\; C \cdot \text{FVAF}(r, N) \]

This is the retirement-savings formula. You save the same amount C every period (say, $500/month into a 401(k)) for N periods at rate r. How much do you have at the end?

The first contribution gets to compound for the longest, the last one barely compounds at all. The FVAF accounts for all those overlapping compounding windows in one closed-form number.

For 30 years of monthly savings at 7% APR, FVAF ≈ 1,220. So $500/month becomes 500 × 1,220 ≈ $610K - way more than 30 × 12 × $500 = $180K of contributions. The other ~$430K is pure compounding magic.

The cash flow received at t+k grows for N−k more periods to time t+N:

\[ V_{t+N} \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{N} C \, (1+r)^{N-k} \;=\; C \sum_{j=0}^{N-1} (1+r)^j \]

This is a geometric series with first term 1 and ratio q = (1+r):

\[ V_{t+N} \;=\; C \cdot \frac{(1+r)^N - 1}{(1+r) - 1} \;=\; C \cdot \frac{(1+r)^N - 1}{r} \]

Equivalently, take the PV annuity value Vt and compound it forward N periods - same answer:

\[ V_{t+N} \;=\; V_t (1+r)^N \;=\; C \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{r} \cdot (1+r)^N \;=\; C \cdot \frac{(1+r)^N - 1}{r} \]

Whiteboard example: Tom's retirement — two chained annuities

Tom is 35 today and wants to retire at 65. After retirement, he wants to withdraw $10,000 each year for 10 years (so 10 birthday withdrawals on his 66th through 75th birthdays). His savings earn 8% per year (EAR). Two scenarios:

(A) Tom deposits an equal amount each year from age 36 to 65 (30 annual deposits) to fund the withdrawals.
(B) Tom inherits a lump sum and deposits it once, on his 36th birthday.

Step 1 (both scenarios): How much money does he need at age 65?

On his 65th birthday (just after the last deposit), the balance must be enough to fund 10 future withdrawals of $10,000. That's a PV annuity at age 65, with first cash flow at age 66, last at age 75, r = 8%, N = 10.

PVAF(0.08, 10) = [1 − (1.08)^(−10)] / 0.08 = [1 − 0.46319] / 0.08 ≈ 6.7101 Balance needed at 65 = $10,000 × 6.7101 ≈ $67,101

This intermediate number is the bridge between the two halves of the problem.

Scenario A: equal annual deposits, 30 of them

30 deposits at ages 36 through 65 must accumulate to $67,101 at age 65. That's an FV annuity at age 65 with the last cash flow on the same day as the target — exactly the setup for FVAF.

FVAF(0.08, 30) = [(1.08)^30 − 1] / 0.08 = [10.0627 − 1] / 0.08 ≈ 113.28 C = 67,101 / 113.28 ≈ $592 per year

So if Tom puts away ~$592 every birthday for 30 years, he can withdraw $10K every birthday for 10 years after retirement. Total deposited: 30 × $592 = $17,760. Total withdrawn: 10 × $10K = $100,000. Compounding does the other ~$82K of work.

Scenario B: one lump-sum deposit at age 36

If Tom deposits a single sum at age 36, it grows for 29 years (from age 36 to age 65) at 8%. It needs to reach $67,101 by age 65.

Required lump sum × (1.08)^29 = 67,101 (1.08)^29 ≈ 9.317 Lump sum = 67,101 / 9.317 ≈ $7,201

About $7,200 today funds the entire retirement income stream — because it has 29 years to compound at 8% before withdrawals begin, multiplying it by ~9.3×. Then the balance fights off the withdrawals because it keeps earning interest during the payout phase too.

Comparison: 30 deposits × $592 = $17,760 total cash out of pocket (Scenario A), vs $7,201 lump sum today (Scenario B). The lump sum is <50% as much cash but achieves the same retirement income — because every dollar deposited at 36 has 29 years to compound, while the average deposit in Scenario A has only ~15 years.

Amortization: a level-payment loan is an annuity from the lender's side. Outstanding balance after any payment = PV of remaining payments. Interest accrued in a period = balance × per-period rate; the rest of the payment goes to principal.

Whiteboard example: 30-year mortgage

You take a $400,000 mortgage. 30 years (360 monthly payments). APR = 6% compounded monthly, so the per-month rate is r = 0.06 / 12 = 0.005.

(a) Monthly payment

The bank lends you $400,000 today; you make 360 equal payments. So $400,000 = C × PVAF(0.005, 360). Solve for C:

PVAF(0.005, 360) = [1 − (1.005)^(−360)] / 0.005 = [1 − 0.16604] / 0.005 = 0.83396 / 0.005 ≈ 166.79 C = 400,000 / 166.79 ≈ $2,398.20 per month

(b) How the first payment breaks down

Interest accrued in month 1: $400,000 × 0.005 = $2,000.00 Principal paid in month 1: $2,398.20 − $2,000 = $398.20 Loan balance after payment 1: $400,000 − $398.20 = $399,601.80

The first month, almost the entire payment goes to interest. Welcome to a mortgage.

(c) How the very last payment breaks down

By month 360, the balance is down to almost zero. Payment is still $2,398.20, but now it's almost all principal and almost no interest. The interest portion shrinks each month as the balance shrinks.

(d) Balance after 10 years (just after payment 120)

The outstanding balance equals the PV of the remaining 240 payments at the per-period rate:

PVAF(0.005, 240) = [1 − (1.005)^(−240)] / 0.005 = [1 − 0.30207] / 0.005 ≈ 139.58 Balance = $2,398.20 × 139.58 ≈ $334,750

After 10 years of payments ($287,784 total paid in), you've only knocked the balance down from $400K to ~$335K. The other ~$222K of your payments went to interest. Mortgages are front-loaded with interest — a key reason early extra principal payments save so much money.

(e) Refinance comparison

Suppose 10 years in, rates have dropped to 4% APR. Should you refinance? Your remaining balance is $334,750. Pay it off as a new 20-year loan at 4%/12 ≈ 0.333%/month:

New PVAF(0.00333, 240) = [1 − (1.00333)^(−240)] / 0.00333 ≈ 165.02 New monthly payment = 334,750 / 165.02 ≈ $2,028 per month Savings: $2,398 − $2,028 = $370 per month × 240 months = $88,800 over the life of the loan (ignoring closing costs)

If closing costs are, say, $5,000, you'd break even in ~14 months and save $80K+ over the remaining loan life. Refinancing math is literally just the annuity formula run twice.

Perpetuities - equal cash flows forever

PV perpetuity (value one period BEFORE the first cash flow)

\[ V_t \;=\; \frac{C}{r} \]

A perpetuity is a magic asset that pays you the same amount, forever. You might think it would be worth infinite - but it isn't, because each future payment is worth less than the one before. At a 10% discount rate, getting $1 forever is worth exactly $10 today.

Here's the most intuitive way to see why V = C/r: if you had X dollars in a 10% bank account, you'd earn X × 10% in interest each year without touching the principal. For that interest to be exactly C, you need X = C / r. So a perpetuity paying C per year is equivalent to a pile of money C/r earning interest.

This formula is the foundation of stock valuation, real estate cap rates, and "what's a business worth" rules of thumb.

Path 1: limit of the annuity formula. Take N → ∞ in PVAF:

\[ V_t \;=\; \lim_{N \to \infty} C \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{r} \]

Since r > 0, (1+r)−N → 0. So Vt = C/r.

Path 2: infinite geometric series directly. Sum PVs of all future cash flows:

\[ V_t \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{C}{(1+r)^k} \;=\; \frac{C}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - \tfrac{1}{1+r}} \;=\; \frac{C}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1+r}{r} \;=\; \frac{C}{r} \]

Same answer. Note this requires r > 0 - otherwise the geometric series diverges.

Growing perpetuity (cash flow grows at g per period; requires r > g)

\[ V_t \;=\; \frac{C_1}{r - g} \]

Like a regular perpetuity, but the cash flow grows over time - e.g., a dividend that increases 3% per year forever. This is the famous Gordon growth model used to value stocks.

The intuition: growth acts like a partial refund on the discount rate. If your discount rate is 9% and cash flows grow 3% per year, it's as if you're only really discounting at 9% − 3% = 6%. The effective discount is the gap between the two.

The "r > g" requirement is real, not a technicality. If cash flows grow faster than you discount them, the math says the asset is infinitely valuable - which is finance's way of telling you the assumption is unrealistic. No real cash flow grows faster than the discount rate forever.

Cash flow at t+k is C1 (1+g)k−1. PV sum:

\[ V_t \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{C_1 (1+g)^{k-1}}{(1+r)^k} \;=\; \frac{C_1}{1+r} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1+g}{1+r}\right)^{\!k} \]

Infinite geometric series with ratio q = (1+g)/(1+r). Converges iff |q| < 1, i.e., r > g. Sum is 1/(1−q):

\[ V_t \;=\; \frac{C_1}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - \tfrac{1+g}{1+r}} \;=\; \frac{C_1}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1+r}{r-g} \;=\; \frac{C_1}{r-g} \]

Special case g = 0 recovers the flat perpetuity Vt = C/r. If g ≥ r the value is infinite (or negative) - the series diverges. In finance "r > g" is a real constraint, not a technicality - perpetual growth at a rate above the discount rate would make any company infinitely valuable.

Lecture 2 - Equity markets

Primary market

  • Firm picks a lead underwriter (an investment bank) who contacts potential buyers.
  • Firm-commitment underwriting (the common case): the bank buys the shares from the firm and resells to the public at a markup. Best-efforts: bank doesn't take inventory, just helps place.
  • Types of issues: IPO (first public offering), seasoned issue, private placement (sold to a handful of institutions).

IPO stylized facts

  • Most IPOs are oversubscribed at the offer price.
  • Issue price is on average below the first-day market price → "IPO underpricing." Reason: informed investors only subscribe to underpriced IPOs, uninformed get full allocation of overpriced (bad) deals and only a fraction of underpriced (good) ones. To break even on average, deals must be underpriced.
  • Long-run: IPOs underperform comparable stocks.

Secondary market mechanics

  • Call auction: all orders aggregated at a fixed time, trade clears at the equilibrium price.
  • Continuous auction: trades happen throughout the day at prices that bounce around equilibrium. Solves the immediacy problem.
  • Two continuous auction structures:
    • Limit order ("order-driven") markets - used for stocks, options, futures.
    • Dealer markets - used for bonds, currencies, swaps, OTC derivatives. Dealers post bid/ask quotes and are always counterparty to customer trades.

Limit order book

  • Market order: execute now at best available price. Limit order: execute only at your stated price or better (provides liquidity).
  • Order book = collection of unexecuted limit orders, sorted in price-time priority (higher bids first; if tied, earlier order first).
  • Bid = highest unfilled limit buy = price a market seller receives. Ask = lowest unfilled limit sell = price a market buyer pays.
  • Spread = Ask − Bid > 0. Profit earned by limit orders for providing immediacy. Spread is the cost of demanding immediacy via a market order.
  • Marketable limit order: a newly-arrived limit order that can execute immediately against the book. Buy priced ≥ lowest ask, or sell priced ≤ highest bid.

How dealers set quotes

Dealers don't want directional exposure → they manage inventory toward zero.

  • Inventory too high (more sells than buys hitting them) → lower bid (discourage selling) and lower ask (encourage buying).
  • Inventory too low (more buys than sells) → raise bid (encourage selling to them) and raise ask (discourage buying from them).

Determinants of bid-ask spread

  • Volume: higher volume → lower spread (less inventory risk per trade).
  • Volatility: higher volatility → higher spread (more inventory risk).
  • Competition between dealers: more competition → tighter spreads.
  • Adverse selection: if market orders are coming from better-informed counterparties, dealers widen spreads to compensate.

Trading costs

  1. Commission - broker's fee.
  2. Bid-ask spread - cost of immediacy (market orders).
  3. Market impact - for large orders, you move the price against yourself.

U.S. secondary market structure

  • Most stocks trade in a fragmented, hybrid market - many venues (NYSE, NASDAQ, BATS, Direct Edge, ECNs), many mechanisms.
  • Exchanges linked by the consolidated quotation system. NBBO = National Best Bid and Offer = highest bid and lowest ask across all linked venues. Reg NMS forces routing to the venue with the better quote.
  • Why people trade: information advantage, liquidity needs (raise cash / deploy cash), or rebalancing.

  • Primary vs secondary markets. Primary = new issues sold to first buyers (IPO, seasoned issue). Secondary = re-trading of existing shares. IPOs are systematically underpriced — informed investors get the good ones; uninformed need underpricing on average to break even.
  • Limit orders provide liquidity; market orders take it. Limit orders sit on the book at a price; market orders cross the spread. Spread = Ask − Bid > 0 is the price of immediacy.
  • Order book obeys price-time priority. Higher bids and lower asks fill first; ties break by order arrival time. Large market orders "walk the book" and cause market impact.
  • Dealers manage inventory toward zero. Inventory too high → lower both bid and ask. Inventory too low → raise both. Spread widens with volatility and adverse selection; narrows with volume and dealer competition.

Lectures 2-3 - Stock positions & portfolio return

Return formula

Single-period return on an asset from t to t+1:

\[ R_{t+1} \;=\; \frac{p_{t+1} + c_{t+1} - p_t}{p_t} \;=\; \frac{p_{t+1} + c_{t+1}}{p_t} - 1 \]

Return is just the percentage change in your wealth over the period. Buy a stock at $100, sell at $110 → 10% return. If it also paid a $2 dividend, your total return is $12 / $100 = 12%.

The key idea: cash you got in your pocket (the dividend) and price appreciation count the same toward your return. The formula reflects that by putting price gain and cash flow in the numerator. This is sometimes called "total return."

where p is price and c is any cash flow paid (dividend, coupon). Convention: returns are calculated close-to-close.

Percentage margin

\[ \text{Margin} \;=\; \frac{\text{Net Worth}}{\text{Value of Stock}} \]

"Margin" tells you what fraction of your stock position is funded by your own money vs borrowed money. 50% margin means half your stock is yours, half is borrowed from the broker. 100% margin means you bought with all cash (no borrowing).

When the stock falls, your equity shrinks faster than the loan shrinks (the loan doesn't shrink - you still owe it). So your percentage margin falls. If it drops below the broker's maintenance margin (often 25–30%), you get a margin call: put up more cash or they liquidate.

Margin is your skin in the game. Lower margin = more leverage = bigger gains AND bigger losses.

  • U.S. initial margin minimum: 50%. If margin falls below the broker's maintenance margin, you must either post collateral or close out.
  • Long on margin: margin = % decline in stock price that wipes out your net worth.
  • Short selling: margin = % increase in stock price that wipes out your net worth.

Portfolio return formula

Return on a portfolio of N assets

\[ R_p \;=\; \omega_1 R_1 + \omega_2 R_2 + \cdots + \omega_N R_N \qquad \text{with} \quad \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i = 1 \]

Your portfolio's return is just a weighted average of the returns on each thing you own. If 60% of your money is in Stock A (which returned 10%) and 40% in Stock B (which returned 5%), your portfolio returned 0.6 × 10% + 0.4 × 5% = 8%. Simple as that.

The weights ωi are the fraction of your starting wealth in each asset. They must sum to 1, because the pieces add up to the whole. The interesting bit is that weights can be negative (when you short) or greater than 1 (when you buy on margin) — see the table below.

This formula is the foundation of everything in portfolio theory. Mean-variance, CAPM, the efficient frontier — they're all just questions about how to pick the weights ωi.

Let W0 be your starting wealth. By definition of "weight," the dollar amount in asset i at the start is ωi W0. Over one period, asset i's value grows to ωi W0 (1 + Ri).

End-of-period wealth is the sum across all assets:

\[ W_1 \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i W_0 (1 + R_i) \;=\; W_0 \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i (1 + R_i) \]

Portfolio return is end-over-start minus 1:

\[ R_p \;=\; \frac{W_1}{W_0} - 1 \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i (1 + R_i) - 1 \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i R_i - 1 \]

Since the weights sum to 1, the first sum is just 1 and cancels the trailing −1:

\[ R_p \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i R_i \]

So the return on a portfolio is exactly the weighted average of the asset returns, with portfolio weights as the weights. No surprise — but it relies on Σ ωi = 1 to work cleanly.

Lecture 3 - Characterizing the return distribution

So far we've talked about returns as if we already knew them. But the return on a risky asset is uncertain — we only know its distribution. To build a portfolio we need to summarize that distribution. Two moments do the job: expected return (the center) and variance (the spread). When we have multiple assets, we also need covariance to capture how they move together.

Notation

Suppose there are K possible "states of the world" at the end of the period (e.g., expansion, recession, normal). Each state s has probability prob(s) and a known return for each asset in that state. The probabilities sum to 1.

Expected return

\[ E[R_A] \;=\; \sum_{s=1}^{K} \text{prob}(s) \cdot R_A(s) \]

The expected return is just the probability-weighted average return across all the possible outcomes. If a stock returns 20% half the time and −10% the other half, its expected return is 0.5 × 20% + 0.5 × (−10%) = 5%.

"Expected" doesn't mean "what's most likely to happen" — it means the long-run average if you played the same gamble many times. In any single year you'll get one specific outcome, not the expected value.

This is also called the mean of the return distribution. It's the single best one-number summary of "what return should I bake into my plans?"

This is just the definition of expectation for a discrete random variable. If RA can take values RA(s1), RA(s2), … with probabilities prob(s1), prob(s2), …, then:

\[ E[R_A] \;=\; \sum_s \text{prob}(s) \cdot R_A(s) \]

The weights are probabilities; they sum to 1; we average. Nothing fancier.

Variance and standard deviation

\[ \sigma^2[R_A] \;=\; \sum_{s} \text{prob}(s) \cdot \left(R_A(s) - E[R_A]\right)^2 \qquad \sigma[R_A] \;=\; \sqrt{\sigma^2[R_A]} \]

Variance measures how spread-out the possible returns are around the mean. For each possible outcome, you take the gap between that outcome and the expected return, square it (so positive and negative gaps both count as "spread"), and average across all the states.

Two assets can have the same expected return but very different variance: one delivers 5% reliably every year, the other ranges from −30% to +40%. The second is way "riskier" in the everyday sense, and variance captures that.

Standard deviation (the square root of variance) is what people actually quote, because it has the same units as the return (percent). A stock with σ = 20% has typical year-to-year swings of roughly ±20% around its mean. Variance has units of "percent squared," which is unintuitive.

In finance, "risk" usually means standard deviation of returns — at least until we get to CAPM where the relevant risk measure changes.

Variance is the expectation of the squared deviation from the mean — the standard definition from statistics:

\[ \sigma^2[R_A] \;=\; E\!\left[(R_A - E[R_A])^2\right] \]

Expanding the expectation for a discrete random variable:

\[ \sigma^2[R_A] \;=\; \sum_s \text{prob}(s) \cdot (R_A(s) - E[R_A])^2 \]

Why square the deviation? Because if you just averaged the raw deviations (RA(s) − E[RA]), they'd cancel out and you'd always get zero — the mean is by construction the balancing point of the distribution. Squaring makes them all positive so they accumulate.

Standard deviation is simply √variance to recover the original units.

Covariance and correlation

Covariance between two assets' returns:

\[ \sigma[R_A, R_B] \;=\; \sum_{s} \text{prob}(s) \cdot (R_A(s) - E[R_A])(R_B(s) - E[R_B]) \]

Correlation (standardized version):

\[ \rho[R_A, R_B] \;=\; \frac{\sigma[R_A, R_B]}{\sigma[R_A]\, \sigma[R_B]} \qquad \rho \in [-1, +1] \]

Covariance asks: when asset A has a good year (above its mean), does asset B tend to also have a good year? If yes, covariance is positive. If A's good years coincide with B's bad years, covariance is negative. If they move independently, covariance is zero.

Think of two stocks: an airline and an oil producer. When oil prices spike, oil stocks soar while airlines suffer (jet fuel is a huge cost). They have negative covariance. Now imagine two banks — they move together because they're exposed to the same interest-rate environment. Strong positive covariance.

Correlation is just covariance rescaled to fit between −1 and +1, so you can compare across asset pairs that have wildly different volatilities. ρ = +1 = perfectly in sync. ρ = 0 = unrelated. ρ = −1 = perfectly opposite. Real assets are usually somewhere in between — most stocks are mildly positively correlated with each other (most things go up when the economy is good).

This is the magic ingredient in diversification: if you combine assets that aren't perfectly correlated, the portfolio's overall risk is less than the weighted average of the individual risks. That's the entire point of holding more than one thing.

Covariance is the expected product of the deviations of each asset from its own mean:

\[ \sigma[R_A, R_B] \;=\; E\!\left[(R_A - E[R_A])(R_B - E[R_B])\right] \]

For a discrete distribution this expands to the probability-weighted sum shown above. Two important special cases:

  • A and itself: σ[RA, RA] = E[(RA − E[RA])²] = σ²[RA]. Covariance with self = variance.
  • Correlation = ±1 means perfect linear dependence. If RB = a + b RA for constants a, b, then ρ = +1 (if b > 0) or −1 (if b < 0).

Covariance has awkward units (return × return) and depends on the scale of the assets. Correlation is the cleaned-up version: divide by both standard deviations to get a unit-free number that always lies in [−1, +1].

\[ \rho[R_A, R_B] \;=\; \frac{\sigma[R_A, R_B]}{\sigma[R_A] \, \sigma[R_B]} \]

By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, this ratio is always between −1 and 1, so correlation is a clean comparable measure across any pair of assets.

Linear regression

Regression of Y on X

\[ Y \;=\; a + b X + e \] \[ b \;=\; \frac{\sigma[Y, X]}{\sigma^2[X]} \qquad a \;=\; E[Y] - b \, E[X] \]

Regression draws the best straight line through a cloud of data points. Given two random variables X and Y, it answers: "if I know X, what's my best guess for Y?"

The slope b says "for each one-unit increase in X, Y goes up by b on average." The intercept a is the predicted Y when X = 0. The residual e is what the line missed — the gap between the actual Y and the predicted Y.

Why we care: in Lecture 5 we'll regress a stock's returns on the market's returns. The slope of that regression is beta — the central risk measure in CAPM. So this formula is the math behind every "beta" you've ever seen quoted on Yahoo Finance.

"Best" line means the one that minimizes the sum of squared residuals. Squared so positive and negative misses both count.

Pick a and b to minimize E[e²] = E[(Y − a − bX)²]. Take partial derivatives and set to zero.

Optimal intercept (first-order condition on a):

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial a} E[(Y - a - bX)^2] \;=\; -2 \, E[Y - a - bX] \;=\; 0 \]

So E[Y − a − bX] = 0, which means a = E[Y] − b E[X]. This forces the line to pass through the point of means (E[X], E[Y]) — and it means the residual e has zero expectation, E[e] = 0.

Optimal slope (first-order condition on b):

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial b} E[(Y - a - bX)^2] \;=\; -2 \, E[X(Y - a - bX)] \;=\; 0 \]

Substitute the optimal a back in. The condition becomes E[X·e] = 0, equivalent to σ[X, e] = 0. Expanding e = Y − a − bX and using a = E[Y] − bE[X]:

\[ E[X(Y - E[Y]) - b X (X - E[X])] \;=\; 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \sigma[Y, X] - b \, \sigma^2[X] \;=\; 0 \]

Solve for b:

\[ b \;=\; \frac{\sigma[Y, X]}{\sigma^2[X]} \]

Two properties that fall out for free: E[e] = 0 (residuals average to zero) and σ[X, e] = 0 (residuals are uncorrelated with the predictor). These are the two "orthogonality conditions" that define the OLS regression line.

Inputs to the portfolio choice problem

Given N risky assets plus a riskless asset, an investor needs three things to choose portfolio weights:

  1. E[Ri] for every asset (expected returns)
  2. σ[Ri] for every asset (standard deviations)
  3. σ[Ri, Rj] for every pair (covariances)

Under mean-variance assumptions, the investor cares only about the resulting portfolio's E[Rp] and σ[Rp]. Those two summary numbers are functions of the weights and the three inputs above. The whole rest of the course is variations on: "given these inputs and some assumption about preferences, how should I pick the weights?"

Lectures 3-4 - One risky + one riskless asset

The simplest portfolio problem: you have access to one risky asset (say, a stock) and the riskless asset (T-bills). How do you split your money?

Investor preferences, formalized

The mean-variance setup makes two assumptions about preferences:

  • All investors prefer more to less — for a given level of risk, higher expected return is always better.
  • Almost all investors are risk averse — for a given expected return, lower volatility is always better.

Stated more formally: for any risk-averse investor,

  • If two portfolios have the same E[R]: pick the one with lower σ.
  • If two portfolios have the same σ: pick the one with higher E[R].
  • If one has both higher E[R] AND higher σ: depends on how risk-averse the investor is.

Portfolio expected return — general formula

\[ E[R_p] \;=\; \omega_1 E[R_1] + \omega_2 E[R_2] + \cdots + \omega_N E[R_N] \]

The expected return on a portfolio is just the weighted average of the expected returns of the assets in it. Same logic as the portfolio return formula from Class 3 — but now we're averaging the expectations, not realized returns.

This works for any number of assets and any weight pattern (including margin and short selling). Clean.

From Lecture 2-3: Rp = Σ ωi Ri. Expectation is a linear operator, so it distributes over sums:

\[ E[R_p] \;=\; E\!\left[\sum_i \omega_i R_i\right] \;=\; \sum_i \omega_i \, E[R_i] \]

The weights ωi are known constants (decided at the start of the period), so they come out of the expectation. Clean and simple.

Standard deviation: one risky + one riskless

\[ \sigma[R_p] \;=\; |\omega_i| \cdot \sigma[R_i] \qquad \text{(one risky asset } i \text{ + riskless)} \]

The riskless asset has zero volatility by definition (it's "riskless"). So all the portfolio's volatility comes from the risky asset, scaled by how much you have invested in it.

If you put 60% in a stock with σ = 20%, the portfolio has σ = 0.6 × 20% = 12%. If you go on margin and have ω = 1.5 in the stock (financed by ω = −0.5 in the riskless), portfolio σ = 1.5 × 20% = 30%. Margin amplifies risk linearly.

Why the absolute value? Because risk is symmetric — going short a risky asset with ω = −0.5 gives you the same volatility as going long with ω = +0.5. Standard deviation doesn't care about direction; only magnitude.

For a two-asset portfolio (one risky + riskless), variance is:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \omega_i^2 \, \sigma^2[R_i] + \omega_f^2 \, \sigma^2[R_f] + 2 \omega_i \omega_f \, \sigma[R_i, R_f] \]

Now use two facts about the riskless asset:

  • σ²[Rf] = 0 (riskless = zero variance)
  • σ[Ri, Rf] = 0 (riskless return is constant, so it can't covary with anything)

The second and third terms vanish, leaving:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \omega_i^2 \, \sigma^2[R_i] \quad\Rightarrow\quad \sigma[R_p] \;=\; |\omega_i| \, \sigma[R_i] \]

The absolute value comes from taking the square root — variance is squared, so a negative weight squares to positive.

How (σ, E[R]) traces out as you vary the weight

Now we can plot it. As you change ω (your weight in the risky asset), the portfolio's (σ, E[R]) point moves along a specific path in mean-variance space. Using DFQ (E[R] = 11%, σ = 12.49%) and Rf = 6%:

Each weight ω on DFQ maps to one point in (σ, E[R]) space. Long positions trace out the upper red branch; short positions trace out the lower dashed branch. Both branches start at Rf (when ω = 0, you're 100% in T-bills).

Two things to notice:

  • The upper (red) branch has positive slope: more risk → more expected return. This is the "good" direction.
  • The lower (gray) branch has negative slope: more risk → less expected return. This is what shorting DFQ gets you, because DFQ has a positive expected risk premium (E[R] > Rf). Shorting it means you're betting against a winner — you bear the risk but lose the premium.

No risk-averse investor ever picks the lower branch. For any σ on the lower branch, there's a portfolio on the upper branch with the SAME σ and a HIGHER E[R]. That's Rule 1 in action (see next subsection).

Decision rules: how risk-averse investors choose

Lynch states two rules that any risk-averse investor follows when comparing two portfolios:

RuleIf you fix…… then preferWhy
Rule 1σ (volatility) constanthigher E[R]"More return for the same risk is always better."
Rule 2E[R] constantlower σ"Less risk for the same return is always better."

What happens when one portfolio has BOTH higher E[R] AND higher σ than the other? Then it depends — a more risk-averse investor might prefer the safer, lower-return one; a less risk-averse investor would take the higher-risk one. Risk aversion enters as a personal trade-off only in this case.

Rule 1, applied to the graph above: Take any σ value, say σ = 6.25%. The upper branch gives E[R] = 8.5% (ω = +0.5, long DFQ); the lower branch gives E[R] = 3.5% (ω = −0.5, short DFQ). Same volatility, but the upper-branch portfolio dominates. Every risk-averse investor picks it. The lower branch is eliminated entirely.

Rule 2 will become important in the next lecture when we have two risky assets — the portfolio possibility curve will bend, and you can find two portfolios with the same E[R] but different σ. Rule 2 then tells you to pick the leftward one (lower σ). On the current 1-risky-+-riskless setup, each E[R] uniquely maps to a single σ on each branch, so Rule 2 doesn't add anything new beyond Rule 1.

Where do risk-averse investors sit on the CAL?

After applying the rules, every risk-averse investor agrees: hold a portfolio on the upper (red) branch. But where on it? That depends on personal risk aversion.

Same CAL, different sitting positions. More risk averse → closer to Rf (more in T-bills, less in DFQ). Less risk averse → further along the line, possibly on margin (ω > 1).

A useful mental model:

  • Highly risk-averse (say, near retirement, can't afford big drawdowns): ω might be 0.2-0.4 in the risky asset, rest in T-bills. Low expected return but low volatility.
  • Moderately risk-averse: ω near 1.0 — basically all in the risky asset, no cash buffer.
  • Low risk aversion (young, long horizon, high tolerance for swings): ω > 1 — borrow at the riskless rate and lever into the risky asset for amplified expected return at the cost of amplified volatility.

The CAL is the menu; the investor's risk aversion picks the dish. Crucially, every risk-averse investor uses the SAME menu (same CAL), just orders different things from it. This is the seed of the two-fund separation theorem we'll see when we get to multiple risky assets.

Capital Allocation Line (CAL)

Slope of the CAL for risky asset i:

\[ \text{slope}[\text{CAL}_i] \;=\; \frac{|E[R_i] - R_f|}{\sigma[R_i]} \]

The Capital Allocation Line is the menu of all portfolios you can build by mixing one risky asset with the riskless asset. Plot it in (σ, E[R]) space and it's a straight line starting from the riskless asset (at σ = 0, E[R] = Rf) and passing through the risky asset (at σ = σ[Ri], E[R] = E[Ri]).

The slope of this line is excess return per unit of risk — how much extra expected return you earn for each unit of standard deviation you take on. This is sometimes called the reward-to-variability ratio. Higher slope = better deal.

Key insight: between two risky assets, a risk-averse investor will always prefer the one with the steeper CAL, regardless of how risk-averse they are. Risk preference only determines where on the CAL to sit, not which CAL to use.

If E[Ri] > Rf, you hold the risky asset with ω ≥ 0 (possibly on margin if ω > 1). If E[Ri] < Rf, you actually want to short the risky asset (ω < 0). Why hold something with expected return below the risk-free rate? You wouldn't — short it instead.

Take a portfolio of weight ω in risky asset i and (1 − ω) in the riskless asset. From the formulas above:

\[ E[R_p] \;=\; \omega E[R_i] + (1 - \omega) R_f \;=\; R_f + \omega (E[R_i] - R_f) \] \[ \sigma[R_p] \;=\; |\omega| \, \sigma[R_i] \]

Solve the second for |ω|: |ω| = σ[Rp] / σ[Ri]. Substituting:

\[ E[R_p] \;=\; R_f \pm \frac{\sigma[R_p]}{\sigma[R_i]} \cdot (E[R_i] - R_f) \]

(The ± reflects ω being positive or negative — long or short.) This is a straight line in (σ, E[R]) space passing through (0, Rf), with slope:

\[ \text{slope} \;=\; \frac{|E[R_i] - R_f|}{\sigma[R_i]} \]

Absolute value because by convention we plot σ on the positive horizontal axis even when ω is negative.

Choosing between two risky assets

If you have a choice of two risky assets A and B to combine with the riskless asset, which do you pick?

Answer: pick the one with the higher CAL slope. Doesn't matter how risk-averse you are. Why? Because any point on the lower-slope CAL is dominated by some point on the higher-slope CAL — the same σ, but more expected return.

Risk preference only affects the weight you choose on that CAL — i.e., how far up the line you sit. More risk-averse → lower weight on the risky asset, more in T-bills. Less risk-averse → higher weight, possibly on margin (ω > 1, levered into the risky asset).

Lecture 4 - Two risky assets

Now we add a second risky asset. With one risky + one riskless, the (σ, E[R]) menu was a straight line — easy. With two risky assets, the menu becomes a curve, and the curve's shape depends on the correlation between the two assets. This is where diversification benefit shows up as actual geometry.

Portfolio variance with two risky assets

Variance in terms of covariance

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \omega_1^2\,\sigma_1^2 + \omega_2^2\,\sigma_2^2 + 2\,\omega_1\omega_2\,\sigma_{1,2} \]

Variance in terms of correlation

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \omega_1^2\,\sigma_1^2 + \omega_2^2\,\sigma_2^2 + 2\,\omega_1\omega_2\,\rho_{1,2}\,\sigma_1\,\sigma_2 \]

The variance of a sum has three pieces: the variance of each asset weighted by ω², PLUS a "cross term" that depends on how the two assets co-move. If they move together (positive covariance), the cross term is positive and adds to portfolio variance. If they move opposite (negative covariance), the cross term subtracts — that's diversification doing its job.

The correlation form makes the role of ρ obvious: portfolio variance is highest when ρ = +1 (no diversification) and lowest when ρ = −1 (assets are perfect hedges). For any ρ < 1, you get some diversification benefit — portfolio σ falls below the weighted average of the individual σ's.

Start with the definition Rp = ω1R1 + ω2R2. Variance of a linear combination:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \mathrm{Var}(\omega_1 R_1 + \omega_2 R_2) \]

Use the general identity Var(aX + bY) = a²Var(X) + b²Var(Y) + 2ab\,Cov(X,Y):

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \omega_1^2\,\mathrm{Var}(R_1) + \omega_2^2\,\mathrm{Var}(R_2) + 2\omega_1\omega_2\,\mathrm{Cov}(R_1,R_2) \]

Rewrite with finance notation (σ² for variance, σi,j for covariance), giving the first form. For the second form, substitute σ1,2 = ρ1,2 σ1 σ2.

For a quick sanity check, take ρ = 1: σ²p = (ω1σ1 + ω2σ2)², so σp = ω1σ1 + ω2σ2 — exactly the weighted average of the individual σ's. Perfect positive correlation produces NO diversification. Any ρ < 1 produces some.

Portfolio possibility curve

As ω1 varies from 0 to 1 (no shorting), the pair (σ[Rp], E[Rp]) traces out a curve in mean-variance space. Its shape depends critically on the correlation between the two assets. Below: same DFQ and BML (E[R] = 11%, σ = 12.49% and E[R] = 10%, σ = 7.75%), but with the correlation varied. As ρ falls, the curve bows further to the left — that's diversification visible as geometry.

All curves connect the same two endpoints (BML and DFQ). The curve bows leftward as ρ decreases — that's diversification reducing risk for the same expected return. At ρ = −1, the curve hits σ = 0 (a perfect hedge can be built from two perfectly-negatively-correlated risky assets).

Minimum-variance portfolio (two risky)

Among all portfolios on the curve, the one with the smallest σ is the minimum-variance portfolio. Its weight in asset 1 is:

\[ \omega_1^{\,*} \;=\; \frac{\sigma_2^{\,2} - \sigma_{1,2}}{\sigma_1^{\,2} + \sigma_2^{\,2} - 2\sigma_{1,2}} \;=\; \frac{\sigma_2^{\,2} - \rho\,\sigma_1\,\sigma_2}{\sigma_1^{\,2} + \sigma_2^{\,2} - 2\rho\,\sigma_1\,\sigma_2} \]

The minimum-variance portfolio puts MORE weight in the asset with LOWER standard deviation, but the precise weight depends on covariance. When ρ < 0 (assets hedge each other), you sometimes hold a lot of the higher-vol asset because each "good" period for one offsets a "bad" period for the other.

When ρ = +1, the formula gives ω₁* = (σ₂² − σ₁σ₂)/(σ₁ − σ₂)² × σ₂/(σ₁−σ₂) — which can be negative (shorting the higher-vol asset to neutralize risk via the perfect linear relationship).

We want to choose ω ≡ ω1 (so ω2 = 1 − ω) to minimize:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p](\omega) \;=\; \omega^2 \sigma_1^2 + (1-\omega)^2 \sigma_2^2 + 2\omega(1-\omega) \sigma_{1,2} \]

Take the first-order condition (FOC) — differentiate with respect to ω and set to zero:

\[ \frac{d \sigma^2}{d\omega} \;=\; 2\omega \sigma_1^2 - 2(1-\omega)\sigma_2^2 + 2(1-2\omega)\sigma_{1,2} \;=\; 0 \]

Divide by 2 and expand:

\[ \omega \sigma_1^2 - \sigma_2^2 + \omega \sigma_2^2 + \sigma_{1,2} - 2\omega \sigma_{1,2} \;=\; 0 \]

Group terms in ω:

\[ \omega\,(\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\sigma_{1,2}) \;=\; \sigma_2^2 - \sigma_{1,2} \]

Solve:

\[ \omega_1^{\,*} \;=\; \frac{\sigma_2^2 - \sigma_{1,2}}{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\sigma_{1,2}} \]

Substitute σ1,2 = ρ σ1 σ2 for the correlation form. Second-order condition is automatically satisfied because σ²[Rp] is a convex parabola in ω: the coefficient of ω² is σ²1 + σ²2 − 2σ1,2 ≥ 0 (this is just Var(R1 − R2) ≥ 0).

Efficient vs inefficient region

The portfolio possibility curve has two halves:

  • Positive-sloped portion (above the minimum-variance portfolio): the efficient frontier. Higher E[R] for higher σ. Risk-averse investors only pick portfolios here.
  • Negative-sloped portion (below the minimum-variance portfolio): the inefficient region. For any point here, there's a point directly above it with the same σ but higher E[R] — so by Rule 1, nobody picks the lower point.

For the DFQ/BML curve at any ρ < 1, both halves exist. At ρ = +1 the curve is just a straight line so there's no minimum-variance portfolio interior to the segment (the lowest σ is just BML).

Lecture 4 - Two risky assets + a riskless asset

Now add T-bills to the mix. With two risky assets, the menu was a curve. Adding a riskless asset gives us back straight-line CALs, but now the CALs can be drawn from Rf to any portfolio on the curve. The question becomes: which CAL is steepest? That CAL passes through the so-called tangency portfolio T, and every risk-averse investor wants it.

The two-step decision

With two risky assets plus a riskless asset, the investor's choice splits cleanly into two steps:

  1. Find the best risky portfolio T — the mixture of the two risky assets whose CAL has the steepest slope.
  2. Pick how much of T vs. Rf to hold — this is the personal risk-aversion call, identical to the 1-risky-+-riskless problem from the previous lecture.

Crucially, Step 1's answer is the same for every risk-averse investor. The mix of the two risky assets is universal. Only Step 2 — how levered or conservative to be — depends on personal taste. This is the seed of two-fund separation that will later become CAPM.

Tangency portfolio T

The tangency portfolio is the risky-asset mixture that maximizes the Sharpe ratio (CAL slope). For two assets, the closed-form solution is:

\[ \omega_{1,T} \;=\; \frac{\mu_1\,\sigma_2^2 - \mu_2\,\sigma_{1,2}}{\mu_1\,\sigma_2^2 + \mu_2\,\sigma_1^2 - (\mu_1 + \mu_2)\,\sigma_{1,2}} \]

where μi = E[Ri] − Rf is the excess return of asset i.

The tangency portfolio is the unique risky portfolio whose CAL is steepest — the highest Sharpe ratio achievable in the (σ, E[R]) plane. Geometrically, the CAL through Rf just touches (is tangent to) the upper edge of the portfolio possibility curve at T.

The formula weights each asset by its excess return μ times the OTHER asset's variance, minus its excess return times the covariance. Intuitively: the more an asset contributes (high μ) and the less it covaries with the other (low σ1,2), the more weight it gets.

Note: tangency weights can be negative — when one asset has such a poor Sharpe ratio relative to the other that you'd rather short it to fund a bigger position in the other. That's not pathological; it's the math telling you the lower-Sharpe asset is just dominated.

The Sharpe ratio of a portfolio with weights (ω, 1−ω) is:

\[ S(\omega) \;=\; \frac{E[R_p] - R_f}{\sigma[R_p]} \;=\; \frac{\omega\,\mu_1 + (1-\omega)\,\mu_2}{\sqrt{\omega^2\sigma_1^2 + (1-\omega)^2\sigma_2^2 + 2\omega(1-\omega)\sigma_{1,2}}} \]

Maximizing S is equivalent to maximizing S² (assuming the numerator is positive). Let N(ω) = ωμ1 + (1−ω)μ2 and V(ω) = σ²[Rp]. We want:

\[ \frac{d}{d\omega} \frac{N^2}{V} \;=\; \frac{2 N N' V - N^2 V'}{V^2} \;=\; 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 2 N' V \;=\; N V' \]

Compute the derivatives:

\[ N' \;=\; \mu_1 - \mu_2 \qquad V' \;=\; 2\omega\sigma_1^2 - 2(1-\omega)\sigma_2^2 + 2(1-2\omega)\sigma_{1,2} \]

Substitute into 2N'V = NV':

\[ 2(\mu_1 - \mu_2)\,V(\omega) \;=\; [\,\omega\mu_1 + (1-\omega)\mu_2\,] \,\bigl[2\omega\sigma_1^2 - 2(1-\omega)\sigma_2^2 + 2(1-2\omega)\sigma_{1,2}\bigr] \]

This is messy but linear in ω after expansion. After distributing and collecting like terms in ω, the ω² and higher terms cancel (this is the magic of the Sharpe optimization), leaving a linear equation:

\[ \omega\,[\mu_1\sigma_2^2 + \mu_2\sigma_1^2 - (\mu_1+\mu_2)\sigma_{1,2}] \;=\; \mu_1\sigma_2^2 - \mu_2\sigma_{1,2} \]

Solve for ω:

\[ \omega_{1,T} \;=\; \frac{\mu_1\sigma_2^2 - \mu_2\sigma_{1,2}}{\mu_1\sigma_2^2 + \mu_2\sigma_1^2 - (\mu_1+\mu_2)\sigma_{1,2}} \]

Alternative geometric argument: The tangency point is where the line from Rf is tangent to the portfolio possibility curve. At that point, the slope of the curve equals the slope of the CAL. Computing both slopes and equating yields the same answer. This is the clean way to remember it: "the steepest CAL touches the curve exactly once."

Total portfolio weights with two risky assets + riskless

Once you know T (the risky-asset portfolio) and you've decided how much to put in T vs. Rf, your weight in each individual risky asset is the product:

\[ \omega_{i,p} \;=\; \omega_{i,T} \cdot \omega_{T,p} \]

where ωT,p is the weight of T in your total portfolio (decided by your risk aversion), and ωi,T is the weight of asset i within T (the same for everyone).

You hold T as a single "bundle" — its composition is fixed by the universal tangency-portfolio formula. Then you decide how much of the bundle to hold (vs. T-bills) based on personal taste. Your final weight in any one asset is just "fraction of total that's in T" × "fraction of T that's in this asset."

Example: ωT,p = 0.6 (60% of your money in T), and ωDFQ,T = 0.277 (T is 27.7% DFQ). Then your overall position in DFQ is 0.6 × 0.277 ≈ 16.6% of your total wealth.

Lectures 4-5 - N risky assets

Generalize to N risky assets. Conceptually, almost nothing changes — same mean-variance framework, same idea of "find the best risky portfolio." What changes is the geometry:

  • With 2 risky assets, varying ω₁ traced out a single curve in (σ, E[R]) space.
  • With N risky assets, the achievable region becomes a 2D blob. There are infinitely many portfolios reachable, scattered all over the plane.
  • The minimum-variance frontier (MVF) is the leftmost edge of that blob — for each target expected return, the lowest σ you can build.
  • The efficient frontier is the upper half of the MVF (positive-sloped). Risk-averse investors only ever pick portfolios here.

The big new result is the diversification theorem: as N grows, idiosyncratic (firm-specific) risk gets washed out completely, leaving only systematic (average-covariance) risk. The leftover floor is what CAPM will call "the market" in the next lecture.

We do need matrix notation to express any of this cleanly — N-fold sums become unwieldy fast. The reference is below if you need it:

matrix notation refresher
  • \(\mathbf{w}\) is an N-tall column vector of weights: \(\mathbf{w} = (\omega_1, \omega_2, \ldots, \omega_N)^{\top}\). The superscript \(\top\) means "transpose" — turn the column into a row.
  • \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) (capital sigma) is the \(N \times N\) covariance matrix. The entry in row \(i\), column \(j\) is \(\Sigma_{i,j} = \sigma_{i,j}\) (covariance of asset \(i\) with asset \(j\)). Diagonal entries \(\Sigma_{i,i} = \sigma_i^2\) are variances. The matrix is symmetric (\(\sigma_{i,j} = \sigma_{j,i}\)).
  • \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\) is a single number (a "quadratic form") — multiply weights through both sides of the matrix and add up. It expands to \(\sum_{i,j} \omega_i\,\sigma_{i,j}\,\omega_j\), exactly the portfolio variance double-sum. Picture: each cell \((i, j)\) contributes \(\omega_i \cdot \sigma_{i,j} \cdot \omega_j\) to the total.
  • \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} = \omega_1\mu_1 + \omega_2\mu_2 + \cdots = E[R_p]\) (portfolio expected return = weighted average).
  • \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = \omega_1 + \omega_2 + \cdots = 1\) (weights sum to one).

Matrix notation is just shorthand — every step below can be rewritten with summation signs if you prefer.

Portfolio variance with N assets

Double-sum form:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \omega_i\,\omega_j\,\sigma_{i,j} \]

Matrix form:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\, \mathbf{w} \qquad \text{with } \mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1 \]

where \(\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^N\) is the weight vector, \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) is the \(N \times N\) covariance matrix (\(\Sigma_{i,j} = \sigma_{i,j}\), with diagonal entries \(\Sigma_{i,i} = \sigma_i^2\)), and \(\mathbf{1}\) is the vector of ones.

For N assets, portfolio variance has \(N^2\) terms total. \(N\) of them are diagonal (variance × weight²); the other \(N(N-1)\) are off-diagonal (covariance × cross weights). Picture an N×N grid: every cell contributes to the total.

The matrix form \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\) is the same expression written compactly. It's also the standard form used in mean-variance optimization libraries — once you can compute \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) and pick \(\mathbf{w}\), the variance just falls out as a quadratic form.

As N grows, the off-diagonal terms (covariances) overwhelm the diagonal terms (variances) — there are way more of them. This is what drives the diversification result below.

Start with the definition. Portfolio return is \(R_p = \sum_i \omega_i R_i\). Variance of a linear combination:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \mathrm{Var}\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^N \omega_i R_i\right) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \omega_i \omega_j \,\mathrm{Cov}(R_i, R_j) \;=\; \sum_{i,j} \omega_i \omega_j \sigma_{i,j} \]

using bilinearity of covariance, with \(\sigma_{i,i} = \sigma_i^2\).

Matrix form: stack weights and covariances. Let \(\mathbf{w} = (\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_N)^{\top}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = [\sigma_{i,j}]\). Then:

\[ \mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\,\mathbf{w} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \omega_i\, \Sigma_{i,j}\, \omega_j \;=\; \sigma^2[R_p] \]

Both forms are equivalent. The matrix form is more compact and makes optimization easier (the Lagrangian below uses it directly). One useful identity — split \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) into diagonal (variances) and off-diagonal (covariances) pieces:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_i \omega_i^2 \sigma_i^2}_{\text{diagonal (own-variance) terms}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_{i \ne j} \omega_i \omega_j \sigma_{i,j}}_{\text{off-diagonal (covariance) terms}} \]

Equal-weighted portfolio: the diversification result

The cleanest way to see how diversification works is to consider an equal-weighted portfolio of N assets — each ωi = 1/N — and study how its variance behaves as N grows.

Equal-weighted portfolio variance:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \frac{1}{N} \cdot \overline{\sigma^2} \;+\; \left(1 - \frac{1}{N}\right) \cdot \overline{\sigma_{i,j}} \]

where \(\overline{\sigma^2}\) is the average variance of the N assets and \(\overline{\sigma_{i,j}}\) is the average pairwise covariance.

Equal-weighting splits portfolio variance into two pieces:

  • \((1/N) \cdot \overline{\sigma^2}\) — the "own-asset" piece. Shrinks toward zero as you add more assets. Represents idiosyncratic / firm-specific / diversifiable risk.
  • \((1 - 1/N) \cdot \overline{\sigma_{i,j}}\) — the "co-movement" piece. As N grows, this approaches the average covariance itself. Represents systematic / market / undiversifiable risk.

The first term goes to 0 as \(N \to \infty\). The second term goes to \(\overline{\sigma_{i,j}}\):

\[ \lim_{N \to \infty} \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \overline{\sigma_{i,j}} \]

Portfolio variance can NEVER fall below the average pairwise covariance, no matter how many assets you add. That floor is the systematic risk — the part you can't diversify away. CAPM is built on this distinction.

Set \(\omega_i = 1/N\) for all \(i\). Substitute into the double sum:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \frac{1}{N} \cdot \frac{1}{N} \cdot \sigma_{i,j} \;=\; \frac{1}{N^2} \sum_{i,j} \sigma_{i,j} \]

Split the double sum into diagonal (\(i = j\)) and off-diagonal (\(i \ne j\)) parts:

\[ \sum_{i,j} \sigma_{i,j} \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^N \sigma_{i,i}}_{\text{diagonal}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_{i \ne j} \sigma_{i,j}}_{\text{off-diagonal}} \;=\; N\,\overline{\sigma^2} + N(N-1)\,\overline{\sigma_{i,j}} \]

There are \(N\) diagonal entries averaging to \(\overline{\sigma^2}\) and \(N(N-1)\) off-diagonal entries averaging to \(\overline{\sigma_{i,j}}\). Divide by \(N^2\):

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \frac{N\,\overline{\sigma^2}}{N^2} + \frac{N(N-1)\,\overline{\sigma_{i,j}}}{N^2} \;=\; \frac{\overline{\sigma^2}}{N} + \frac{N-1}{N}\,\overline{\sigma_{i,j}} \]

which is the same as \((1/N)\,\overline{\sigma^2} + (1 - 1/N)\,\overline{\sigma_{i,j}}\). ∎

Special cases that make the result tangible

Let every asset have the same variance \(\sigma^2\). Then \(\overline{\sigma^2} = \sigma^2\), and the average covariance simplifies to \(\overline{\sigma_{i,j}} = \bar{\rho}\,\sigma^2\), where \(\bar{\rho}\) is the average pairwise correlation.

\(\bar{\rho}\)\(\sigma^2[R_p]\)\(\sigma[R_p]\) as \(N \to \infty\)Meaning
\(0\)\(\sigma^2 / N\)\(0\)Full diversification: vol vanishes.
\(0.5\)\(\sigma^2 \bigl(\tfrac{1}{N} + (1 - \tfrac{1}{N}) \cdot 0.5\bigr)\)\(\sigma\sqrt{0.5} \approx 0.71\,\sigma\)Half the firm vol stays as systematic risk.
\(1\)\(\sigma^2\)\(\sigma\)No diversification: assets all move identically.

So \(\bar{\rho}\) sets the floor. With \(\bar{\rho} = 0.5\) and 100 assets, the equal-weighted portfolio still has roughly 70% of an individual asset's σ. Even infinite diversification cannot reduce risk below \(\sqrt{\bar{\rho}}\,\sigma\).

Minimum-variance frontier (MVF) for N assets

What the MVF is. Pick a target expected return μ* (say, "I want a portfolio with E[R] = 8%"). Among all the infinitely many portfolios that achieve that target, find the one with the lowest standard deviation. That's one point on the MVF. Repeat for every possible target, and the locus of points traces out a curve — the MVF itself.

What it looks like. A hyperbola opening to the right in (σ, E[R]) space (see the picture below). The tip of the hyperbola is the global minimum-variance portfolio — the lowest-σ portfolio achievable from these N assets. The upper branch (going up-right from the tip) is the efficient frontier; every risk-averse investor picks a point here. The lower branch is inefficient — same σ as the upper branch, lower E[R], so dominated.

What changes when you add assets. Adding another risky asset to the menu can only shift the MVF leftward (better risk-return trade-off). Old portfolios are still possible; new mixtures are also possible; you can only do better. This is the diversification argument made formal.

Formal setup. The MVF is constructed by solving, for each target μ*, a constrained quadratic optimization:

\[ \min_{\mathbf{w}} \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\, \mathbf{w} \quad \text{subject to} \quad \mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\mu} = \mu^* \;,\; \mathbf{w}^{\top} \mathbf{1} = 1 \]

The solution traces out a parabola in (σ², μ*) space — equivalently a hyperbola in (σ, μ*) space — which is the MVF.

Step 1: set up the Lagrangian. We're minimizing \(\tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\) (the \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) is just for clean algebra — doesn't change the optimum). Subtract a multiplier \(\lambda_k\) times each constraint:

\[ \mathcal{L} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w} - \lambda_1 (\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} - \mu^*) - \lambda_2 (\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} - 1) \]

Step 2: take the gradient with respect to w and set to zero. Two matrix-calculus facts you need:

  • \(\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\bigl(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{w}\bigr) = 2\mathbf{A}\mathbf{w}\) when \(\mathbf{A}\) is symmetric (which \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) always is). So \(\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\bigr) = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\).
  • \(\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\bigl(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{v}\bigr) = \mathbf{v}\) for any constant vector \(\mathbf{v}\).
\[ \nabla_{\mathbf{w}} \mathcal{L} \;=\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w} - \lambda_1 \boldsymbol{\mu} - \lambda_2 \mathbf{1} \;=\; \mathbf{0} \]

Solve for \(\mathbf{w}\) by left-multiplying with \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\):

\[ \mathbf{w}^{\,*} \;=\; \lambda_1 \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} + \lambda_2 \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \]

Note what this means: the optimal portfolio is a linear combination of two fixed vectors — \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}\) (a portfolio biased toward high-expected-return assets after adjusting for correlations) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}\) (the minimum-variance portfolio direction). The Lagrange multipliers \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\) just control the mix. This is already the two-fund spanning property: any MVF portfolio is a mixture of just two reference portfolios.

Step 3: pin down \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\) using the two constraints. Plug \(\mathbf{w}^{*}\) back into \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} = \mu^*\) and \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1\):

\[ \lambda_1\,\boldsymbol{\mu}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} + \lambda_2\,\boldsymbol{\mu}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \;=\; \mu^* \] \[ \lambda_1\,\mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} + \lambda_2\,\mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \;=\; 1 \]

Step 4: name the constants that keep appearing. Three scalars depend only on \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) (NOT on \(\mu^*\)):

\[ A \;\equiv\; \boldsymbol{\mu}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} \qquad B \;\equiv\; \boldsymbol{\mu}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \qquad C \;\equiv\; \mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \qquad D \;\equiv\; AC - B^2 \]

What they mean physically:

  • \(A\) = "weighted size of the expected-return vector" after \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\) adjusts for correlations. Large \(A\) → big return premia available.
  • \(B\) = the "alignment" between \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) and \(\mathbf{1}\) through \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\). Related to the global mean return.
  • \(C\) = "ease of constructing portfolios" — inverse of the global minimum variance (since \(\sigma^2_{\min} = 1/C\), as we'll show).
  • \(D = AC - B^2\). Always positive (by Cauchy-Schwarz). Sets the "curvature" of the MVF hyperbola.

The two constraint equations become a 2×2 linear system in \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\):

\[ \begin{cases} A\,\lambda_1 + B\,\lambda_2 = \mu^* \\ B\,\lambda_1 + C\,\lambda_2 = 1 \end{cases} \]

Solve (standard 2×2):

\[ \lambda_1 \;=\; \frac{C\,\mu^* - B}{D} \qquad \lambda_2 \;=\; \frac{A - B\,\mu^*}{D} \]

Step 5: write down the optimal portfolio.

\[ \mathbf{w}^{\,*}(\mu^*) \;=\; \frac{C\mu^* - B}{D}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} \;+\; \frac{A - B\mu^*}{D}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \]

The MVF as a whole is parameterized by \(\mu^*\): pick any target expected return, plug in, get the weights.

Step 6: variance at the optimum. Use the FOC: \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}^{*} = \lambda_1\boldsymbol{\mu} + \lambda_2\mathbf{1}\). So:

\[ \sigma^2[\mathbf{w}^{\,*}] \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{*\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}^{*} \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{*\top}(\lambda_1\boldsymbol{\mu} + \lambda_2\mathbf{1}) \;=\; \lambda_1\,\mu^* + \lambda_2 \]

Plug in the expressions for \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\):

\[ \sigma^2[\mathbf{w}^{\,*}] \;=\; \frac{C\,(\mu^*)^2 - 2B\,\mu^* + A}{D} \]

This is a parabola in \((\sigma^2, \mu^*)\) coordinates — equivalently a hyperbola in \((\sigma, \mu^*)\) coordinates. That hyperbola is the MVF.

Step 7: the global minimum-variance portfolio. Minimize variance over \(\mu^*\):

\[ \frac{d\sigma^2}{d\mu^*} \;=\; \frac{2C\mu^* - 2B}{D} \;=\; 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \mu^*_{\min} \;=\; \frac{B}{C} \]

At this target, \(\lambda_1 = 0\), so the portfolio is purely in the \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}\) direction:

\[ \mathbf{w}^{\,*}_{\min} \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}} \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}}{C} \]

And its variance: \(\sigma^2_{\min} = 1/C\). So \(C\) really does measure "ease of constructing low-variance portfolios" — bigger \(C\) → smaller global minimum variance.

Schematic plot. Individual asset points are scattered; the MVF curves leftward of them. Upper half is the efficient frontier (positive-sloped); lower half is inefficient.

Each individual asset sits inside the achievable region (to the right of the MVF). The red curve is the efficient frontier — the upper half of the hyperbola. The dashed gray curve is the inefficient region — same σ as the efficient counterpart, lower E[R].

Two facts about the MVF:

  • Adding risky assets only helps. Adding asset N+1 to a set of N assets cannot move the MVF to the right (a worse achievable set), and usually moves it to the left. More assets → more diversification possibilities → more efficient choices.
  • Each individual asset lies strictly inside the MVF (except in pathological cases). The whole point of constructing the MVF is that nobody holds a single asset as their portfolio — there's always a multi-asset combination with the same E[R] and lower σ.

Lectures 4-5 - N risky assets + a riskless asset

Add T-bills. The same logic from the 2-risky-+-riskless case scales up: among all portfolios on the MVF, find the one whose CAL has the steepest slope (highest Sharpe ratio). That's the tangency portfolio T. The new efficient frontier is the straight CAL through T — every risk-averse investor wants to be on it.

Tangency portfolio T — N-asset formula

Same problem as the 2-asset case, just bigger. We're still asking: "What's the single risky-asset portfolio that gives the steepest CAL when combined with Rf?" The answer was \(\mathbf{w}_T\) for 2 assets — a specific pair of weights given by a closed form. For N assets, the closed form generalizes to one matrix expression.

\[ \mathbf{w}_T \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{\mathbf{1}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}} \]

where \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} = \boldsymbol{\mu} - R_f \cdot \mathbf{1}\) is the vector of excess expected returns (each entry is \(E[R_i] - R_f\)).

Step 1: state the problem. Choose the risky-asset weights \(\mathbf{w}\) to maximize the Sharpe ratio:

\[ \max_{\mathbf{w}} \;\; \frac{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} - R_f}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}} \quad \text{subject to } \mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1 \]

The constraint forces the weights to sum to 1 — i.e., 100% of the risky-asset bucket goes into these N assets.

Step 2: simplify the numerator using the constraint. Because \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1\), you can write \(R_f = R_f \,\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{w}^{\top}(R_f \mathbf{1})\). Then:

\[ \mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} - R_f \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} - \mathbf{w}^{\top}(R_f\mathbf{1}) \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{\top}(\boldsymbol{\mu} - R_f\mathbf{1}) \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \]

So we're maximizing:

\[ S(\mathbf{w}) \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}} \]

Step 3: use the scale-invariance trick. If you rescale \(\mathbf{w} \to c\mathbf{w}\) for any positive constant \(c\), both the numerator (linear in \(\mathbf{w}\)) and the denominator (linear after the square root) scale by \(c\). So \(S\) is unchanged. This means we can drop the constraint \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1\) entirely — solve the unconstrained problem to find the right DIRECTION of \(\mathbf{w}\), then rescale at the end so the weights sum to 1.

Step 4: take the gradient. Use the quotient rule and the matrix identities:

  • \(\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\bigl(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\bigr) = \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\)
  • \(\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w} \,/\, \sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}\)
\[ \nabla_{\mathbf{w}} S \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}}\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;-\; \frac{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w})^{3/2}}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w} \;=\; \mathbf{0} \]

Step 5: rearrange to isolate \(\mathbf{w}\). Multiply through by \((\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w})^{3/2}\) and collect terms:

\[ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}}_{\text{positive scalar }k}\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \]

The scalar \(k\) on the right depends on \(\mathbf{w}\), but by scale-invariance we only care about direction. Multiply both sides by \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\):

\[ \mathbf{w} \;=\; k \,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \mathbf{w} \;\propto\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \]

Step 6: normalize so weights sum to 1. Pick the constant \(k\) so that \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1\). If \(\mathbf{w} = k\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\), then \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = k \cdot \mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\); setting this equal to 1 gives \(k = 1 \,/\, \mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\). So:

\[ \mathbf{w}_T \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{\mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}} \]

This is the tangency portfolio formula. Conceptually the answer is just "\(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\), normalized."

What's actually going on in that formula

Strip away the matrix dressing and the formula is saying three intuitive things:

  • Weight each asset by its excess return — assets with higher \(E[R_i] - R_f\) get more weight. That's the \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\) piece.
  • Then "scrub through" \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\) to avoid double-counting correlated assets. If two assets are highly correlated, \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\) partially cancels them — you don't want to bet twice on the same underlying risk. If one asset has a high Sharpe ratio but covaries strongly with everything else in the universe, \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\) shrinks its weight (it's redundant exposure).
  • Normalize by \(\mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\) so the weights sum to 1.

It's the same intuition that drove the 2-asset case: high excess return ⇒ more weight, high covariance with the rest of the portfolio ⇒ less weight. The matrix just generalizes the bookkeeping.

Two things worth knowing:

  • \(\mathbf{w}_T\) can have negative entries — some assets get shorted in the tangency portfolio if they have negative excess return, or if they correlate the wrong way. That's not a bug; it's the optimizer telling you the asset is dominated.
  • The formula reduces exactly to the 2-asset closed form we derived earlier. (If you want to see the matrix algebra reduce in a few lines, expand the proof below.)
show: how the matrix formula reduces to the 2-asset closed form

For 2 assets:

\[ \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \mu_1 \\ \mu_2 \end{pmatrix} \qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_1^2 & \sigma_{1,2} \\ \sigma_{1,2} & \sigma_2^2 \end{pmatrix} \qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 - \sigma_{1,2}^2} \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_2^2 & -\sigma_{1,2} \\ -\sigma_{1,2} & \sigma_1^2 \end{pmatrix} \]

(Standard 2×2 inverse: swap diagonal, negate off-diagonal, divide by determinant.)

Multiply \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\) by \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\):

\[ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 - \sigma_{1,2}^2} \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_2^2 \mu_1 - \sigma_{1,2}\mu_2 \\ -\sigma_{1,2}\mu_1 + \sigma_1^2 \mu_2 \end{pmatrix} \]

Sum the two components (that's \(\mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\)):

\[ \mathbf{1}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;=\; \frac{\mu_1 \sigma_2^2 + \mu_2 \sigma_1^2 - (\mu_1 + \mu_2)\,\sigma_{1,2}}{\sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 - \sigma_{1,2}^2} \]

Divide the first component by this sum — the determinant cancels — and you get the first weight:

\[ \omega_{1,T} \;=\; \frac{\mu_1\,\sigma_2^2 - \mu_2\,\sigma_{1,2}}{\mu_1\,\sigma_2^2 + \mu_2\,\sigma_1^2 - (\mu_1 + \mu_2)\,\sigma_{1,2}} \]

This matches the closed-form 2-asset tangency formula exactly. ✓

The new efficient frontier is the CAL through T

With \(R_f\) available, an investor can hold ANY combination of \(T\) and \(R_f\). The set of achievable portfolios is the entire straight line from \(R_f\) through \(T\) (and beyond, via margin). Its slope is the maximum Sharpe ratio:

\[ \text{slope}_{\text{CAL through T}} \;=\; \frac{E[R_T] - R_f}{\sigma_T} \;=\; \sqrt{\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}^{\top}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}} \]

This is the highest Sharpe ratio achievable from any portfolio of the \(N\) risky assets and \(R_f\).

The CAL slope is just the Sharpe ratio at the tangency portfolio. Write \(\mathbf{w}_T = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\,/\,B\) where \(B = \mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\).

Excess return at \(T\):

\[ \mathbf{w}_T^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;=\; \frac{(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}})^{\top}}{B}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{B} \;=\; \frac{A}{B} \]

where \(A = \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\). Variance at \(T\):

\[ \mathbf{w}_T^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}_T \;=\; \frac{(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}})^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{B^2} \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{B^2} \;=\; \frac{A}{B^2} \]

So \(\sigma_T = \sqrt{A}\,/\,|B|\). Sharpe ratio:

\[ \text{slope} \;=\; \frac{A/B}{\sqrt{A}/|B|} \;=\; \mathrm{sgn}(B) \cdot \sqrt{A} \;=\; \sqrt{\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}^{\top}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}} \]

(assuming \(B > 0\), the standard case where the average excess return is positive).

Two-fund separation theorem

Two-fund separation: with N risky assets and a riskless asset, every risk-averse mean-variance investor holds the same tangency portfolio T, combined with Rf in a proportion that depends on their personal risk aversion.

This is one of the most beautiful results in finance. It says that no matter how risk-averse you are, the composition of your risky-asset holdings should be identical to everyone else's — the only thing that varies is how much risky vs. riskless you hold. Marginal investors with different preferences agree completely on which risky portfolio to hold.

Why it matters:

  • It massively simplifies portfolio choice. The investor doesn't need to think about which assets to hold — they just hold T (plus T-bills). The asset-selection problem and the risk-level problem decouple.
  • It's the seed of CAPM. In the next lecture: under additional assumptions, T must equal the market portfolio M (every asset value-weighted). Two-fund separation + market clearing = CAPM.

Picture: MVF with and without a riskless asset

Without Rf, the efficient frontier is the upper half of the MVF (gray curve). With Rf, the new efficient frontier is the straight CAL through T (red line), which dominates almost the entire MVF — same risk, higher expected return.

Note: the CAL touches the MVF curve at exactly one point (T) and lies above it everywhere else. That's why T is called the tangency portfolio. Past T, you can't go up the MVF; you can only ride the straight line by leveraging T (buying T on margin, i.e., ωT,p > 1).

Adding more assets shifts the MVF left

If you expand the universe of available risky assets (e.g., add international stocks to a US-only portfolio, or add bonds to a stock portfolio), three things happen:

  1. The MVF shifts to the LEFT — every achievable expected return now has a lower-σ portfolio backing it.
  2. The new tangency portfolio T' has a STEEPER CAL (higher Sharpe ratio) — diversification gives you a better risk-return menu.
  3. EVERY risk-averse investor benefits (assuming no transaction costs). Even if they don't want to hold the new asset directly, the new tangency portfolio is better than the old one.

This is the formal answer to "why bother diversifying internationally?" or "why add bonds to a stock portfolio?" — the menu of (σ, E[R]) combinations strictly improves.

✦ remember this — Lectures 4-5 (N risky ± Rf)
  • Portfolio variance in matrix form: σ²[Rp] = wTΣw. The covariance matrix Σ is N × N; portfolio weights are an N-vector.
  • Diversification theorem (equal-weighted): σ²[Rp] = σ̄²/N + (1 − 1/N) σ̄i,j. As N → ∞, the first term (idiosyncratic risk) vanishes; the second (systematic risk, the average covariance) remains. You can't diversify below the floor of average covariance.
  • Minimum-variance frontier (MVF) is the parabola in (σ², μ) space (hyperbola in (σ, μ) space) traced out by minw wTΣw subject to wTμ = μ* and wT1 = 1. Adding risky assets always shifts the MVF leftward.
  • Tangency portfolio T for N assets: wT = Σ⁻¹μexcess / (1TΣ⁻¹μexcess). Maximum Sharpe ratio = √(μexcessTΣ⁻¹μexcess).
  • With a riskless asset, the efficient frontier is the CAL through T — a straight line, not a curve. It dominates the MVF everywhere except at the tangency point.
  • Two-fund separation theorem: every risk-averse mean-variance investor holds the SAME tangency portfolio T, combined with Rf in their personal preferred mix. Asset selection decouples from risk-level choice. This is what makes CAPM work.

Lectures 5–6 - CAPM

The whole semester until now has been about portfolio choice from one investor's seat. CAPM is the leap: if every investor solves the same mean-variance problem with the same inputs, what does the equilibrium look like? The answer is the cleanest result in modern finance — and the workhorse model the industry still uses for cost-of-capital calculations 60 years later.

The leap: from one investor to equilibrium

Two-fund separation said every risk-averse investor holds the same tangency portfolio T (just in different mix with Rf). CAPM adds one more leap: if everyone holds T, then in equilibrium T must equal the market portfolio M.

  1. The market portfolio M is just the sum of every risky asset in the economy, value-weighted. Equivalently: sum up every investor's risky portfolio.
  2. If every investor holds T as their risky-asset portfolio, then summing across investors gives M = T.
  3. So the tangency portfolio's weights aren't an exotic optimization output — they're just market caps. AAPL's weight in T = AAPL's market cap ÷ total market cap.

This is what makes CAPM tractable. You don't need to invert a 5,000 × 5,000 covariance matrix to find T — you can just look up market caps on Bloomberg.

The 4 CAPM assumptions (and what they buy you)
  1. Mean-variance investors — everyone only cares about E[Rp] and σ[Rp]. (Buys you: two-fund separation.)
  2. Homogeneous expectations — all investors agree on E[Ri], σ[Ri], σ[Ri,Rj] for every asset. (Buys you: same MVF, same T for everyone.)
  3. Borrow and lend freely at Rf — one common riskless rate, no constraints. (Buys you: same CAL through T for everyone.)
  4. Perfect capital markets — costless trading, costless short-sales, no taxes, infinitely divisible assets, price-taking. (Buys you: the equilibrium argument works.)

Several of these can be relaxed and CAPM still goes through. The empirical evidence (later) is what really gives CAPM trouble, not the assumptions.

CAPM result #1 — the Capital Market Line (CML)

Since every investor holds combinations of Rf and M, every investor's portfolio lies on the line from Rf through M in (σ, E[R]) space. That line is the CML.

Capital Market Line (CML)

\[ E[R_{ef}] = R_f + \frac{E[R_M] - R_f}{\sigma[R_M]} \cdot \sigma[R_{ef}] \]

The CML is just the CAL through the market portfolio. Same equation, special meaning: this is the menu of risk/return combinations available to every investor in a CAPM world. The slope (E[RM] − Rf) / σ[RM] is the market price of risk — how many extra expected return points you get per unit of σ when you walk up the line by borrowing more and putting it into M.

"ef" stands for efficient portfolio. Anything on the CML is efficient — it's the cheapest way to get that level of expected return. Anything below the CML is dominated. Anything above is impossible (in CAPM world).

From the CAL formula with the riskless asset and any risky portfolio P: E[Rp] = Rf + (E[RP] − Rf) / σ[RP] · σ[Rp]. Substitute P = M (the market portfolio, which equals the tangency T in CAPM) and you get the CML.

Properties of points on the CML:

  • Only assets that are combinations of Rf and M lie on the CML.
  • Every individual investor's total portfolio lies on the CML.
  • Any portfolio on the CML has correlation +1 with M — that's the math definition of being a combination of M and Rf.

CAPM result #2 — the Security Market Line (SML)

The CML only prices efficient portfolios. But there are millions of individual stocks, and most of them are not efficient (they have idiosyncratic risk you could diversify away). What's the expected return on an individual stock? That's the SML.

Security Market Line (SML) — the CAPM equation

\[ E[R_i] = R_f + \beta_{i,M} \cdot (E[R_M] - R_f) \] \[ \text{where} \quad \beta_{i,M} = \frac{\sigma[R_i, R_M]}{\sigma^2[R_M]} \]

The SML says expected return is linear in beta. Three pieces:

  • Rf: the baseline — what you'd get for taking no risk.
  • (E[RM] − Rf): the market risk premium — the extra return for taking on one unit of beta (since βM,M = 1).
  • βi,M: how much of that market risk premium asset i earns. β = 1 means same expected excess return as the market. β = 2 means twice the excess return (and twice the systematic risk). β = 0 means Rf (no systematic risk at all).

Why beta, not σ? Because everyone already holds M. The risk that matters for an extra asset isn't its standalone σ — it's how much it adds to the variance of M. The piece of σ[Ri] that's uncorrelated with M gets diversified away in M. Only the correlated piece — captured by beta — gets compensated.

Real-world betas you'll see: utilities/gold ~0.4–0.6 (defensive); Coca-Cola, P&G ~0.9–1.0 (market-like); tech, banks, luxury ~1.3–1.8 (cyclical); levered/distressed ~2+ (high beta).

Derivation sketch from "M is on the MVF":

1. Since M is the tangency portfolio, M lies on the MVF. The Lagrangian FOC for any asset i on the MVF gives:

\[ E[R_i] - R_f = \lambda \cdot \sigma[R_i, R_M] \]

2. Apply that same equation to M itself (i = M):

\[ E[R_M] - R_f = \lambda \cdot \sigma^2[R_M] \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda = \frac{E[R_M] - R_f}{\sigma^2[R_M]} \]

3. Substitute λ back into the first equation:

\[ E[R_i] - R_f = \frac{\sigma[R_i, R_M]}{\sigma^2[R_M]} (E[R_M] - R_f) = \beta_{i,M} (E[R_M] - R_f) \]

Rearrange to get the SML. The whole derivation hinges on M being on the MVF — which is what two-fund separation guarantees.

Picture: CML and SML side by side

These are the two diagrams Lynch draws on every CAPM whiteboard page. Different axes, same world.

All efficient portfolios lie on the CML (left). All individual assets — efficient or not — lie on the SML (right). G and H can have the same β but different σ: G is on the CML (efficient), H is to the right of the CML (some of its σ is diversifiable). On the SML, they sit at the same point.

CML vs SML — when does an asset lie on which?

This is the single most-tested CAPM concept. Three rules:

  1. Every asset lies on the SML (that's just the CAPM equation, which holds for all i).
  2. Only efficient portfolios lie on the CML. Efficient = a combination of Rf and M = perfectly correlated with M (ρ = 1).
  3. An asset lies on both the SML and the CML iff ρ[Ri, RM] = 1. Otherwise (ρ < 1), it lies on the SML but to the right of the CML — same expected return, higher σ.
Why ρ = 1 is the bridge between the two lines

The SML can be rewritten by substituting βi,M = ρ[Ri, RM] · σ[Ri] / σ[RM]:

\[ E[R_i] = R_f + \frac{E[R_M] - R_f}{\sigma[R_M]} \cdot \rho[R_i, R_M] \cdot \sigma[R_i] \]

Compare to the CML:

\[ E[R_{ef}] = R_f + \frac{E[R_M] - R_f}{\sigma[R_M]} \cdot \sigma[R_{ef}] \]

The two are identical iff ρ = 1. When ρ < 1, you need more σ to get the same expected return — which is why inefficient assets plot to the right of the CML.

Properties of beta

Beta is the central object in CAPM. Six properties worth keeping in your head:

  • βf,M = 0 — riskless asset has no covariance with anything.
  • βM,M = 1 — market has unit beta with itself (σ[RM, RM] / σ²[RM] = 1).
  • Average β in the economy = 1 (value-weighted). So β > 1 means above-average systematic risk, β < 1 means below.
  • Portfolio beta = weighted average of asset betas: βp,M = Σ ωi,p βi,M. (Direct from linearity of covariance.)
  • Beta of any asset uncorrelated with M = 0, regardless of how volatile the asset is.
  • What makes beta big or small: βi,M = ρ[Ri, RM] · σ[Ri] / σ[RM]. Increasing in correlation with M AND in the asset's own volatility.

How to estimate beta — regression

Beta is unobservable; you estimate it from data. The standard recipe: regress the asset's return on the market return, the slope is β.

Excess-return market model regression

\[ r_i = \alpha_{i,M} + \beta_{i,M} \, r_M + e_{i,M} \] \[ \text{where } r_i = R_i - R_f, \quad r_M = R_M - R_f \]

You're decomposing the asset's return into three pieces:

  • αi,M (Jensen's alpha) — the intercept. Average excess return not explained by market exposure. CAPM says this should be 0 for every asset. We'll use this for performance measurement next lecture.
  • βi,M rM — the part of the asset's return that moves with the market. This is the systematic, undiversifiable, priced-for piece.
  • ei,M — the noise / idiosyncratic / diversifiable piece. By construction uncorrelated with rM, and has expected value zero.

In practice: use 60 monthly returns, regress in Excel (Data → Data Analysis → Regression), the slope is your β estimate. Lynch's whiteboard example uses ADM (Archer Daniels Midland) regressed on the S&P 500 over 60 months ending 12/04, yielding β̂ ≈ 0.45 (raw) or 0.63 (Bloomberg adjusted).

Why S&P 500 as the market proxy? You can't observe the true global market portfolio (every risky asset in the world). S&P 500 is value-weighted, broad, and easy to obtain. But it's a proxy — Roll's critique (later) is that you can't really test CAPM without the true M.

From regression theory (Lecture 3): if you minimize E[e²] where e = Y − a − bX, the FOC gives:

\[ b = \frac{\sigma[Y, X]}{\sigma^2[X]}, \quad a = E[Y] - b \, E[X] \]

Plug Y = ri, X = rM:

\[ \beta_{i,M} = \frac{\sigma[r_i, r_M]}{\sigma^2[r_M]} = \frac{\sigma[R_i, R_M]}{\sigma^2[R_M]} \]

(The Rf drops out because it's a constant: σ[Ri − Rf, RM − Rf] = σ[Ri, RM], and similarly for the variance.) So the regression slope is CAPM beta. The intercept αi,M = E[ri] − βi,M E[rM] is Jensen's alpha.

Bloomberg's "adjusted beta" = 0.67 × raw β + 0.33 × 1, pulling estimates toward 1 because empirically beta estimates mean-revert toward 1 over time (Blume adjustment).

Variance decomposition — systematic vs diversifiable risk

From the regression ri = α + β rM + e:

\[ \sigma^2[R_i] = \beta_{i,M}^2 \, \sigma^2[R_M] + \sigma^2[e_{i,M}] \]

The first piece is systematic risk (priced — it shows up in E[Ri] via beta). The second is idiosyncratic / diversifiable risk (not priced — diversifies away in M). For an efficient portfolio, σ²[e] = 0 — all its variance is systematic. For an inefficient stock, the idiosyncratic piece can dominate.

Whiteboard example: G and H with the same beta

This is Lynch's signature CAPM problem. It tests whether you really get the CML/SML distinction.

Setup. Rf = 5%, market E[RM] = 13%, σ[RM] = 10%. Two assets:

AssetE[R]σ[R]βi,M
M (market)13%10%1
G?5%0.5
H?8%0.5

Q1: Expected returns? Both have β = 0.5, so both lie on the SML at β = 0.5:

E[R_G] = E[R_H] = R_f + β · (E[R_M] − R_f) = 0.05 + 0.5 · (0.13 − 0.05) = 0.05 + 0.04 = 0.09 = 9%

Same beta → same expected return. The SML doesn't care about σ.

Q2: Does G lie on the CML? Check the CML at σ = 5%:

E[R_ef at σ = 5%] = R_f + (E[R_M] − R_f) / σ[R_M] · σ[R_ef] = 0.05 + (0.08 / 0.10) · 0.05 = 0.05 + 0.04 = 9% ✓

Yes, G is on the CML. So G is efficient: it must be a combination of Rf and M. (Specifically, 50% in M and 50% in Rf: weights wM = σ[RG] / σ[RM] = 0.5.)

Q3: Does H lie on the CML? Check CML at σ = 8%:

E[R_ef at σ = 8%] = 0.05 + 0.8 · 0.08 = 0.05 + 0.064 = 11.4%

But E[RH] = 9% < 11.4%. H lies below the CML, not on it. H is inefficient — same beta as G but higher σ, because H has idiosyncratic risk that doesn't get rewarded.

Q4: Correlations with M? Using βi,M = ρ[Ri, RM] · σ[Ri] / σ[RM]:

ρ[R_G, R_M] = β_G,M · σ[R_M] / σ[R_G] = 0.5 · 0.10 / 0.05 = 1.0 (perfectly correlated → on CML) ρ[R_H, R_M] = β_H,M · σ[R_M] / σ[R_H] = 0.5 · 0.10 / 0.08 = 0.625 (not perfectly correlated → off CML)

The punchline: two assets with the same β get the same expected return (SML rules). But one has all its risk priced (G, ρ = 1) and one has unpriced idiosyncratic risk on top (H, ρ < 1). No rational investor would hold H by itself — they'd hold G or, more likely, some mix of M and Rf.

✦ remember this — Lecture 5 (CAPM)
  • The big leap: two-fund separation said everyone holds the same T. CAPM adds that in equilibrium T = M, the value-weighted market portfolio. Now T's weights aren't an optimization output — they're market caps.
  • CML: E[Ref] = Rf + (E[RM] − Rf)/σ[RM] · σ[Ref]. Prices efficient portfolios. Slope = market price of risk.
  • SML: E[Ri] = Rf + βi,M · (E[RM] − Rf). Prices every asset. Slope = market risk premium.
  • Beta βi,M = σ[Ri, RM] / σ²[RM] = ρ[Ri, RM] · σ[Ri] / σ[RM]. The slope of regressing Ri on RM. βM,M = 1, βf,M = 0, value-weighted average β across the economy = 1.
  • CML vs SML: Every asset is on the SML. Only assets with ρ[Ri, RM] = 1 are also on the CML. Same β = same E[R], but inefficient assets (ρ < 1) plot to the right of the CML.
  • Why beta and not σ? Everyone holds M. The piece of an asset's σ that's uncorrelated with M gets diversified away. Only the part that contributes to σ[RM] gets priced — that's β.

Lecture 5-6 - Performance measures & empirical evidence

Now that CAPM gives us the equilibrium pricing equation, we can ask: did this fund manager beat the market on a risk-adjusted basis? The answer is one of two ratios, depending on how the investor uses the fund.

Jensen's alpha — when the asset is one of many

Jensen's alpha — the SML intercept

\[ \alpha_{i,M} = E[r_i] - \beta_{i,M} \cdot E[r_M] \] \[ \text{where } r_i = R_i - R_f, \quad r_M = R_M - R_f \]

Jensen's alpha is "extra return above what CAPM says you should earn given your beta."

  • α > 0: asset lies above the SML → underpriced → buy. (Higher expected return than its beta would justify.)
  • α = 0: asset lies on the SML → correctly priced (CAPM equilibrium).
  • α < 0: asset lies below the SML → overpriced → short or avoid.

Why this is the right yardstick for a "marginal" asset. If you already hold M and Rf, the question of whether to add asset i to your portfolio is whether adding it improves your Sharpe ratio. The math says yes ⟺ αi > 0. So Jensen's alpha is the right performance measure when the asset is one of many in a CAPM-optimal portfolio.

Real-world use: mutual fund evaluation. Regress 60 months of the fund's excess returns on market excess returns; if the intercept (alpha) is reliably positive after fees, the manager is generating value.

Two equivalent definitions:

(1) Run the regression ri = αi + βi rM + ei. The intercept αi is Jensen's alpha.

(2) Take expectations of both sides:

\[ E[r_i] = \alpha_i + \beta_i \, E[r_M] \quad \Rightarrow \quad \alpha_i = E[r_i] - \beta_i \, E[r_M] \]

If CAPM holds, then E[Ri] − Rf = βi(E[RM] − Rf), i.e., E[ri] = βi E[rM], which means αi = 0. So under CAPM, alpha is zero for every asset. Nonzero alpha = mispricing.

Sharpe ratio — when the asset is your only risky position

Sharpe ratio — slope of the CAL through asset i

\[ \text{Sharpe}_i = \frac{E[r_i]}{\sigma[R_i]} = \frac{E[R_i] - R_f}{\sigma[R_i]} \]

Sharpe is "excess return per unit of standalone risk." It's literally the slope of the CAL you'd draw by combining asset i with Rf.

When to use Sharpe vs Jensen's alpha:

  • If asset i is being held as part of a well-diversified portfolio that already contains M → use Jensen's alpha. The marginal question is "does adding this improve my CAPM portfolio's Sharpe?" Answer: yes iff α > 0.
  • If asset i is the investor's only risky holding (combined with Rf only) → use Sharpe ratio. The marginal question is "does this asset give me a steeper CAL than alternatives?" Answer: pick the one with the highest Sharpe.

Why this distinction matters. An asset can have high Sharpe but zero alpha (it's just a combination of Rf and M). Or high alpha but low Sharpe (it's a high-α, high-σ asset that you'd only want in small quantity alongside M). Misusing the ratios → wrong call about whether a fund adds value.

From CAL slope formula: if you mix asset i with Rf at weight ω, your portfolio has:

\[ E[R_p] = R_f + \omega(E[R_i] - R_f), \quad \sigma[R_p] = |\omega| \sigma[R_i] \]

Eliminating ω gives a line with slope (E[Ri] − Rf) / σ[Ri] in (σ, E[R]) space. That's the Sharpe ratio. The market portfolio's Sharpe is the maximum achievable: SharpeM = (E[RM] − Rf) / σ[RM], i.e., the slope of the CML.

  • If you're holding M as your core risky portfolio and choosing which fund to add a small slice of → pick A (higher α, more bang for the marginal exposure).
  • If the fund will be your only risky position → pick B (steeper CAL, lower σ for similar excess return).

Empirical evidence — does CAPM actually work?

Lynch's empirical section is short: CAPM is a beautiful theory but the data doesn't love it.

  • Average returns are still increasing in beta — this part holds up. Higher-β stocks do earn higher returns on average.
  • The relationship is too flat. The empirical SML has a positive intercept and a slope below the theoretical (E[RM] − Rf). Low-β stocks earn more than CAPM predicts; high-β stocks earn less. "Low-beta anomaly."
  • Size effect (Banz, 1981): small-cap stocks earn significantly more than their CAPM beta would justify. Persistent positive Jensen's alpha for small firms.
  • Value effect (Fama-French, 1992): high book-to-market ("value") stocks earn significantly more than their beta would justify. Persistent positive alpha for value firms.
  • Conclusion: beta is part of the story, but not the whole story. Other risk factors — size, value, and others — also command premia. This motivates the multifactor models in Lecture 7.
Roll's critique — why CAPM is hard to test cleanly

Richard Roll (1977): you can't actually test CAPM because you can't observe the true market portfolio M. The S&P 500 is a proxy. If M is mismeasured, the test is biased in unknown ways. So a rejection of CAPM might really be a rejection of "S&P 500 = M," not CAPM itself. Most empirical work proceeds anyway, treating S&P 500 (or CRSP value-weighted index) as M.

✦ remember this — Lecture 5-6 (Performance Measures)
  • Jensen's alpha αi,M = E[ri] − βi,M·E[rM]. CAPM says α = 0 for every asset. α > 0 → underpriced (above SML), α < 0 → overpriced. Use when the asset is one of many in a CAPM-optimal portfolio.
  • Sharpe ratio = (E[Ri] − Rf) / σ[Ri] = slope of the CAL. Use when the asset is the investor's only risky holding. Max Sharpe = Sharpe of M = slope of CML.
  • Sharpe and alpha can disagree on rankings. An asset can have high α but low Sharpe (and vice versa). Know which question you're answering before picking a metric.
  • CAPM fails empirically. Small-cap stocks and value stocks earn persistent positive alpha. The SML is too flat. Beta alone doesn't explain the cross-section. Motivates Fama-French.

Lecture 7 - Multifactor models (ICAPM & Fama-French)

CAPM assumes investors care only about E[Rp] and σ[Rp]. But long-horizon investors with labor income also care about how their portfolio covaries with the state of the economy. When the economy tanks, you'd love your portfolio to hold up (you might be losing your job). This is the gateway to multifactor pricing.

ICAPM — when investors care about more than (E[R], σ)

Merton's Intertemporal CAPM (ICAPM) generalizes CAPM by adding one or more state variables — macroeconomic indicators that summarize "the state of the economy." Examples: GDP growth, building permits, help-wanted index.

The intuition: a multi-period investor with labor income (i.e., almost everyone) is worried not just about portfolio volatility but about what state of the world they'll be in when they need the money. Specifically:

  1. Human capital (the present value of future wages) drops when the economy weakens — risk of layoffs, wage cuts, lower bonuses.
  2. So the investor dislikes portfolios whose returns are positively correlated with the economic indicator (EI) — those portfolios lose money exactly when human capital is also hurting.
  3. Equivalently, investors want portfolios that hedge their human capital risk — portfolios whose returns are negatively correlated with EI.

Consequence: M no longer needs to be the tangency portfolio. Investors will accept lower expected return for a portfolio that hedges their EI exposure. So the optimal risky-asset holding mixes M with a hedging portfolio REI (the portfolio whose return is most correlated with EI).

ICAPM expected-return equation — the 2-factor model

ICAPM with 1 state variable

\[ E[r_i] = \beta^*_{i,M} \, \lambda^*_M + \beta^*_{i,EI} \, \lambda^*_{EI} \]

where ri = Ri − Rf, and the betas come from the multivariate regression

\[ r_i = a_{i,EI} + \beta^*_{i,M} \, r_M + \beta^*_{i,EI} \, r_{EI} + e_{i,EI} \]

and the risk premia are λ*M = E[rM], λ*EI = E[rEI].

Generalization of the SML: instead of one source of risk (market beta) priced at one risk premium (E[rM]), there are now two. β*i,M is the market risk loading; β*i,EI is the state-variable risk loading. Each has its own risk premium.

The asterisk on the betas is important. β*i,M is not the same as the CAPM βi,M from a single-variable regression. It's the partial slope on rM when you also control for rEI. (Stat people: think Frisch-Waugh-Lovell theorem.)

Why two risk premia? Investors care about two things now: portfolio volatility (priced via β*i,M) and covariance with EI (priced via β*i,EI). An asset that hedges EI well will have negative β*i,EI, which lowers its required return — investors will pay up (accept lower E[R]) for the hedging benefit.

Punchline for the SML: assets can fail to lie on the SML and still satisfy ICAPM. The CAPM equation E[ri] = βi,M·E[rM] is replaced by the 2-factor version.

The full derivation comes from solving Merton's continuous-time consumption-portfolio choice problem with stochastic state variables, then taking the FOC for portfolio weights. Out of scope for this course. The key result: in any economy where investors care about K state variables in addition to (E[Rp], σ[Rp]), expected returns satisfy a (K+1)-factor model.

Generalized Jensen's alpha — performance in a 2-factor world

If ICAPM is the right model, then the right alpha to measure performance against is the ICAPM alpha:

a_{i,EI} = E[r_i] − (β*_{i,M} · E[r_M] + β*_{i,EI} · E[r_EI])

Same interpretation as Jensen's alpha — but now relative to the 2-factor expected return plane, not the SML.

  • ai,EI > 0: asset is above the ICAPM plane → underpriced.
  • ai,EI = 0: asset is correctly priced under ICAPM.
  • ai,EI < 0: overpriced.

Worked example: GIP as the state variable

Let GIP = growth rate of industrial production. RGIP = return on the portfolio most correlated with GIP. Given:

  • Rf = 0.7%
  • E[RM] = 1.3% → λ*M = 0.6%
  • E[RGIP] = 1.7% → λ*GIP = 1.0%
Assetβ*i,Mβ*i,GIPE[Ri]

What the example shows. Grey and Black have the same market beta (0.9), so CAPM would assign them the same expected return. ICAPM does not — because Black hedges GIP (β*GIP < 0): when industrial production tanks, Black's return is higher. Investors will accept a lower expected return on Black to get that hedging benefit.

This is exactly the mechanism by which assets can lie off the SML and still be correctly priced — they're trading off market exposure for state-variable hedging.

Fama-French 3-factor model

ICAPM tells us multifactor pricing should hold but doesn't tell us which factors. Fama and French (1993) gave the empirically-winning answer: market, size (SMB), and value (HML).

Fama-French 3-factor model

\[ E[r_i] = \beta^*_{i,M} E[r_M] + \beta^*_{i,SMB} E[r_{SMB}] + \beta^*_{i,HML} E[r_{HML}] \]

where the betas come from the regression

\[ r_i = a_{i,FF} + \beta^*_{i,M} \, r_M + \beta^*_{i,SMB} \, r_{SMB} + \beta^*_{i,HML} \, r_{HML} + e_{i,FF} \]

Three risk factors instead of one:

  • rM = market excess return — same as CAPM.
  • rSMB (Small Minus Big) = return on a portfolio that goes long small-cap stocks and short large-cap stocks. Captures the size premium. E[rSMB] > 0 historically.
  • rHML (High Minus Low) = return on a portfolio that goes long high book-to-market ("value") stocks and short low book-to-market ("growth") stocks. Captures the value premium. E[rHML] > 0 historically.

SMB and HML are zero-investment portfolios (long/short with $0 net cost). They are constructed to be neutral to the other factor: SMB is book-to-market neutral, HML is size neutral.

Why this works: empirically, small-cap and value stocks earn returns that CAPM can't explain. FF says: those aren't anomalies, they're separate risk factors. Stocks that load positively on SMB and HML earn higher expected returns to compensate.

Fama-French alpha ai,FF = E[ri] − (β*i,M·E[rM] + β*i,SMB·E[rSMB] + β*i,HML·E[rHML]) is the modern standard for measuring mutual fund performance. Most active US equity funds have aFF ≈ 0 after fees.

FF is empirically motivated, not derived. The theoretical justification is ICAPM-style: SMB and HML are hedging portfolios for unidentified state variables (possibly distress risk, possibly behavioral mispricing). The model is judged on fit, not first principles.

Why FF explains the size and value premia

Lynch's empirical demo (US 1963–91 monthly data, E[rM] = 0.43%/mo):

  • Small firm portfolio S: β*S,SMB = +1.19, β*S,HML = +0.26. FF predicts higher expected return than CAPM by ≈ 1.19·0.27 + 0.26·0.40 ≈ +0.43%/mo. That extra return is what CAPM was missing.
  • Value firm portfolio V: β*V,SMB = +0.66, β*V,HML = +0.68. FF predicts an extra ≈ 0.66·0.27 + 0.68·0.40 ≈ +0.45%/mo over CAPM.
  • Empirical fit: on 25 size×B/M sorted portfolios, the largest deviation from FF's expected return equation is 0.13% per annum — vs. 0.57% per annum from CAPM. FF explains roughly 4× more of the cross-section.

Lecture 7-8 - Debt instruments & the money market

Module pivot. Portfolio theory and asset pricing are done. The rest of the course is about the other two asset classes: debt (now) and derivatives (later). Fixed income flips the question from "what return should I expect from a stock?" to "what's the price of a security that promises a fixed cash flow stream?" The whole topic reduces to: price = PV of remaining cash flows, discounted at the right yield. The hard part is the right yield.

The money market — short-dated debt

The money market trades instruments with original maturity ≤ 1 year. Lowest-default-risk piece of the U.S. financial system. The flagship instrument is the T-bill. Other money-market instruments (mostly optional in this course): commercial paper (short-term corporate debt), CDs (bank-issued), Fed Funds (overnight interbank), LIBOR (being phased out for SOFR).

U.S. Treasury bills (T-bills)

T-bills are obligations of the U.S. government — among the safest dollar-denominated assets in existence. They're discount instruments: no coupon, just bought below par and redeemed at par. Maturities issued at 4, 8, 13, 26, 52 weeks.

T-bill holding-period return (held to maturity)

\[ R = \frac{\text{Par} - \text{Price}}{\text{Price}} = \frac{\text{Par}}{\text{Price}} - 1 \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \text{Price} = \frac{\text{Par}}{1 + R} \]

You pay Price today, you get Par at maturity. Your return is the gap, as a fraction of what you paid. No coupons, no compounding — just one cash flow in, one cash flow out. Think of a T-bill as a coupon you redeem at the post office: you bought it for $99.92, you'll cash it for $100.00 in 37 days, you make 8 cents per $100.

The math is trivial; what makes T-bills interesting is the yield convention used to compare them (next formula).

Bond-equivalent yield (BEY) — the "Asked Yield" quoted by the WSJ

\[ y = \frac{365}{n} \cdot R \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad R = y \cdot \frac{n}{365} \]

where n = days from settlement to maturity.

BEY is the hold-to-maturity return expressed as an APR with n-day compounding. Recall the APR convention: APR = per-period rate × periods/year. Here the "period" is n days, there are 365/n such periods in a year, and the per-period rate is R. So y = R × (365/n) — exactly the APR.

BEY lets you compare T-bills of different maturities on a common annualized scale. A 37-day bill yielding R = 0.0813% is not "obviously" cheaper or more expensive than a 91-day bill yielding R = 0.20% — until you annualize both via BEY.

APR definition: inom = m × r1/m, where m = number of compound periods in a year and r1/m = the per-period rate. For an n-day T-bill, the period is n days, m = 365/n periods in a year, and r1/m = R. Substitute: y = (365/n) × R.

Treasury notes & bonds — coupon-paying instruments

  • T-notes: original maturity 1–10 years.
  • T-bonds: original maturity > 10 years.

Both pay semi-annual coupons and return par at maturity. A plain-vanilla note/bond is characterized by:

  1. Maturity — when par is repaid.
  2. Par (face value) — amount repaid at maturity (typically $100).
  3. Coupon rate — annual rate; semi-annual coupon = 0.5 × coupon rate × par.
  4. Coupon dates — paid on the 15th or end of the month, every 6 months.

Quoting convention: the WSJ "clean price" does NOT include accrued interest. The actual transaction price ("dirty" / invoice price) = clean + accrued. If a coupon has just been paid, clean = dirty.

Treasury strips — zero coupon bonds via STRIPS

A zero coupon bond ("zero") pays no intermediate cash flows — just par at maturity. In that respect it's a T-bill, but for longer maturities (up to 30 years).

How they're created: by stripping a coupon bond. The U.S. Treasury STRIPS program (Separate Trading of Registered Interest and Principal Securities) lets investors register coupons and principal payments as separately tradable securities since 1982.

The key insight that powers all of L8

A coupon bond is a portfolio of zero-coupon bonds, each maturing on one of the coupon dates.

A 1½-year 6% T-note paying $3 every 6 months and $103 at maturity is, in effect:

  • One ½-year zero with face $3,
  • One 1-year zero with face $3,
  • One 1½-year zero with face $103.

You can own the coupon bond as one security OR own the three replicating zeros. By no-arbitrage, the coupon bond's price must equal the sum of the zero prices. This is the engine of all bond pricing in L8 — and the route into the conversion triangle below.

Bond pricing via the law of one price

Bond price = sum of cash-flow PVs at strip yields / discount factors

\[ P^i(0) = d_{1/2}(0)\,C^i(1/2) + d_1(0)\,C^i(1) + \cdots + d_N(0)\,C^i(N) \] \[ = \frac{C^i(1/2)}{(1 + y_{1/2}(0)/2)^1} + \frac{C^i(1)}{(1 + y_1(0)/2)^2} + \cdots + \frac{C^i(N)}{(1 + y_N(0)/2)^{2N}} \]

The bond is a portfolio of zeros — so its price is the sum of the zero prices that replicate its cash flows. Each $1 of coupon at time t costs dt(0) today; each $100 of par at time T costs dT(0) × 100.

Notice: each cash flow is discounted at a different rate. Coupons in 6 months use y½; coupons in 1 year use y1; the par at maturity T uses yT. No single discount rate. That single-rate object (the YTM) is what you back out after pricing the bond — it's a summary, not the engine.

No-arbitrage proof. Build a replicating portfolio of strips: for each coupon date t in the bond, buy Ci(t) face value worth of t-year strips, costing dt(0)·Ci(t) today. At maturity, also buy par face value of T-year strips.

This portfolio delivers exactly the bond's cash flows. So if it costs less than the bond, buy the portfolio and sell the bond — arbitrage. If it costs more, do the reverse. Equilibrium: portfolio cost = bond price.

Slope rule — when does YTM(coupon) sit above or below yT(0)?
  • Upward-sloping yield curve (y½(0) < y1(0) < y1.5(0)): YTM(coupon bond, maturity 1.5) < y1.5(0). The coupon bond looks "less yieldy" than the equivalent-maturity zero.
  • Flat curve: YTM(coupon) = yT(0). All discounting at one rate.
  • Downward-sloping curve: YTM(coupon) > yT(0).
  • Higher coupon rate → more PV in earlier cash flows → YTM pulled further toward short-end yields.

This is why Lynch keeps reminding you: it is NOT correct to use the YTM of an n-year coupon bond as the yield on an n-year zero. They differ whenever the curve isn't flat.

The yield curve

Definition (Lynch): the yield curve is the yield to maturity on an n-year zero coupon bond graphed as a function of n. Equivalently, the spot rate at each maturity.

Spot rate = YTM on the shortest-maturity zero of interest (here the ½-year strip). y½(0) is today's spot rate; y½(t) is the spot rate at some future time t.

Bootstrapping the yield curve from coupon notes

If you only see coupon-bond prices (no strips), you can still recover the spot rates by solving one maturity at a time. Given the 2/15/24 note prices above:

  1. From 4% Aug 24 note (one cash flow left at 8/15/24): 98.94 = 102 · d½(0) → d½(0) = 0.97 (and y½(0) = 6.186%).
  2. From 4% Feb 25 note using d½(0): 97.82 = 2 · 0.97 + 102 · d1(0) → d1(0) = 0.94 (and y1(0) = 6.284%).
  3. From 6% Aug 25 note using d½(0) and d1(0): 98.43 = 3 · 0.97 + 3 · 0.94 + 103 · d1.5(0) → d1.5(0) = 0.90 (and y1.5(0) = 7.149%).

One maturity at a time, each step using the spot rates already pinned down. This is bootstrapping the zero curve. In practice it's how dealers build the curve, because clean strip quotes don't exist for every maturity but coupon Treasuries cover the full range.

Other bond pricing issues

Apparent violations of the law of one price usually have a reason:

  • Taxes. Two instruments with the same pretax cash flows but different tax treatment can have different prices (e.g., municipal vs. Treasury).
  • Liquidity. Less liquid bonds trade at a price discount (yield premium). Treasury strips are less liquid than on-the-run notes.
  • Transaction costs. Bid-ask spreads create a no-arbitrage band, not a single price. Arbitrage opportunities have to clear the spread before they're real.

Corporate bonds promise the same cash flows as Treasuries, but with some probability of default. This drives a wedge between expected return E[R] and yield to maturity (YTM) — concepts that coincided for Treasuries.

Holding period return on a bond

½-year holding period return on bond i

\[ h^i(t + 1/2) = \frac{p^i(t + 1/2) + C^i(t + 1/2) - p^i(t)}{p^i(t)} \quad \text{(effective ½-year)} \]

Expressed as an APR with ½-year compounding: multiply by 2.

HPR is the return you actually earned over the holding period: (capital gain + coupon income) / price paid. Same shape as an equity return, just with a coupon term added.

Key relationship to YTM:

  • For a ½-year strip (matures at end of holding period): HPR = YTM at start. The two coincide because there's no reinvestment uncertainty.
  • For longer-maturity bonds: HPR ≠ YTM at start.
    • If yield rises during the period → end price falls → HPR < YTMstart.
    • If yield falls during the period → end price rises → HPR > YTMstart.

This is the seed of duration: how much does the price change when yields move? (Coming in L9.)

Forward rates — definition

A forward rate is the interest rate you lock in today for a borrowing/lending period that starts in the future. The contract is called a Forward Rate Agreement (FRA).

Lynch's notation: ft, t+α(0) is the α-period forward rate available at time 0 for the period starting at t. APR with semi-annual compounding.

FRA naming convention (in months): "t × (t+α) FRA" where both numbers are in months from today.

  • f½, 1(0) = "6 × 12 FRA" — locks a 6-month rate for the period 6 months out.
  • f½, 1.5(0) = "6 × 18 FRA" — locks a 1-year rate for the period 6 months out.
  • f5, 7(0) = "60 × 84 FRA" — locks a 2-year rate for the period 5 years out.

Example (Lynch). 2/15/24. You agree to invest $1 at 8/15/24 for 6 months at 6.38% (APR, semi-annual compounding). So f½, 1(0) = 6.38%, and you'll receive $1 × (1 + 0.0638/2) = $1.0319 on 2/15/25.

The fundamental no-arbitrage relationship

Spot-forward no-arbitrage (the conversion identity)

\[ \left(1 + \frac{y_{t+\alpha}(0)}{2}\right)^{2(t+\alpha)} = \left(1 + \frac{y_t(0)}{2}\right)^{2t} \cdot \left(1 + \frac{f_{t,t+\alpha}(0)}{2}\right)^{2\alpha} \]

Equivalently, in terms of discount factors:

\[ d_{t+\alpha}(0) = d_t(0) \cdot d_{t,t+\alpha}(0) \]

where dt, t+α(0) = 1 / (1 + ft, t+α(0)/2)2α is the forward discount factor.

Two ways to invest $1 from today to t+α must give the same payoff. If they don't, free money.

Strategy A: buy a (t+α)-year zero. Locked-in growth: (1 + yt+α(0)/2)2(t+α).

Strategy B: buy a t-year zero AND enter today a forward contract for the α years after t. Locked-in growth: (1 + yt(0)/2)2t × (1 + ft, t+α(0)/2)2α.

Both are riskless. By no-arbitrage their payoffs must match. The discount-factor form is just the reciprocal: today's price of $1 at t+α = today's price of $1 at t × today's price of $1 at t+α conditional on being at t.

The key takeaway: forward rates are NOT predictions; they're mechanical no-arbitrage extracts from today's spot curve.

Build two strategies, both starting with $1 today.

Strategy A: buy a (t+α)-year strip.

  • Spend $1 at time 0.
  • Receive (1 + yt+α(0)/2)2(t+α) at time t+α.

Strategy B: buy t-year strips today, plus enter a forward to roll the proceeds at the locked-in forward rate.

  • Spend $1 at time 0.
  • Receive (1 + yt(0)/2)2t at time t.
  • Roll into the forward: at time t+α get (1 + yt(0)/2)2t × (1 + ft,t+α(0)/2)2α.

Both strategies are riskless and use the same capital. By no-arbitrage, payoffs must match. Done.

Lecture 8-9 - Theories of the yield curve

Forward rates fall out of no-arbitrage. But why does the market price them where it does? Three competing stories, each making a different claim about what forward rates equal.

Expectations Hypothesis (EH)

Setting: individuals don't care about interest rate risk. Expected return over any future period is the same for all strips and forward contracts available for that period.

Three approximately-equivalent statements (all exactly equivalent under continuous compounding):

EH1 — n-year horizon (long yield = blend of shorter yields and expected future spot)

\[ \left(1 + \frac{y_n(0)}{2}\right)^{2n} = \left(1 + \frac{y_{n-1/2}(0)}{2}\right)^{2n-1} \cdot \left(1 + \frac{E_0[y_{1/2}(n-1/2)]}{2}\right) \]

If you don't care about interest rate risk, two strategies for an n-year horizon must have the same expected payoff:

  • Buy an n-year strip and hold to maturity (certain payoff).
  • Buy an (n−½)-year strip, then roll the proceeds into a ½-year strip at the prevailing rate at that time (random — depends on future spot).

Expected payoffs equal ⟹ the equation above. The n-year yield is the geometric blend of the (n−½)-year yield and the expected ½-year spot rate at time n−½.

EH2 — ½-year horizon (all bonds have the same expected ½-year HPR = today's spot)

\[ y_{1/2}(0) = E_0[h_n(1/2)] \quad \text{for any } n > 0 \]

If investors don't care about interest rate risk, the expected ½-year return on a 30-year strip equals the expected ½-year return on a 1-year strip equals today's ½-year spot rate. No bond offers an expected-return premium for taking on more rate risk. Under EH, the term-premium is exactly zero.

EH3 — forward rate = expected future spot rate

\[ f_{n-1/2,\, n}(0) = E_0\!\left[y_{1/2}(n-1/2)\right] \quad \text{for any } n > 1/2 \]

The forward rate locked in today is the market's expected ½-year spot rate at that future date. This is the cleanest statement of EH — and the one Lynch leans on for the yield curve implications below.

EH implications — yield curve slope ↔ expected future spot rates

So under EH, the yield curve slope is a clean signal about market expectations of future short rates. But — important — this is only true if EH holds. The next two theories say it doesn't, and the signal gets muddier.

Yield curve slope and future GDP growth

Lynch's macro link: positive yield curve slope predicts strong future GDP growth.

  1. Firm investment decision: accept projects with NPV0 > 0, where NPV uses E[R] from the CAPM SML: E[Ri] = Rf + βi,M(E[RM] − Rf).
  2. Lower Rf (short-term rates) → lower hurdle rate → more positive-NPV projects → more investment.
  3. Positive yield curve slope = low short-term rates relative to long → high investment today → high GDP growth ahead.
  4. Empirically: the slope of the yield curve is one of the best available predictors of future GDP growth. Average U.S. slope 1997–2007 ≈ 90 bp with σ ≈ 90 bp.

June 2026 example (Lynch's class): yield curve slope = +119 bp. Close to long-term average → market expecting future GDP growth close to average. Macro context: Fed hiked from 0.25%/0.5% to 5.25%/5.5% (March 2022 → July 2023) on inflation concerns, then started cutting in Sept 2024, pause at 3.5%/3.75% by April 2026. Yield curve hump-shaped at the long end suggests rates expected to stay near current levels.

Reasons EH fails — why slope signals get noisy

Reason 1: Illiquidity

Investors require a liquidity premium to hold less-liquid strips. Higher illiquidity → lower strip prices → higher yields than EH would imply.

Implication: the EH1 equation doesn't hold with equality — it can go either direction. Slope of the yield curve tells you little about whether the market expects higher or lower future spots.

Reason 2: Liquidity Preference Theory

Refinement of the illiquidity story: strip illiquidity increases with maturity. Longer strips need a bigger liquidity premium.

Liquidity preference: y_n(0)^{2n} > y_{n-½}(0)^{2n-1} × (1 + E[y_½(n-½)]/2) (the EH equation, but with strict >) So forward rates upward-biased: f_{n,n+½}(0) > E[y_½(n)]

Implications:

  • You'll see upward-sloping yield curves more often than EH would predict.
  • Forward rates are upward-biased predictors of future spots — they over-predict on average.
  • An upward-sloping curve is consistent with EITHER higher or lower expected future spots. Slope no longer cleanly signals expectations.
  • A downward-sloping curve still implies the market expects lower future spots (the term premium can't flip the sign).

This is the modern empirical consensus. Term premia are positive on average, which is why "the yield curve normally slopes up" doesn't mean "rates are normally expected to rise."

Reason 3: Investors care about interest rate risk (preferred habitat)

Two positions with the same investment period but different exposures to interest rate risk have different expected returns. Sometimes called the preferred habitat hypothesis — pension funds prefer long bonds, banks prefer short bonds, and they don't substitute freely.

Implications: EH doesn't hold; the EH1 equation can go either way. Yield curve slope says little about expected future spots.

Lecture 9 - Duration & bond portfolio management

Once bond prices are pinned down by the term structure, the next question is risk: how much does a bond's price move when yields shift, and how do you build a bond portfolio that survives those shifts? An investor with a horizon T faces two opposing risks:

  • Reinvestment risk: if cash flows arrive before T, you don't know what yield you'll get when you reinvest them. Falling yields hurt.
  • Liquidation risk: if cash flows arrive after T, you must sell at T at an unknown price. Rising yields hurt.

Duration is the tool that quantifies this sensitivity — and immunization is the trick that balances the two risks.

Macaulay duration — the cash-flow-weighted average maturity

Macaulay duration of a cash-flow stream with time-0 price P and YTM y (APR, semi-annual compounding)

\[ D = \tfrac12\,k(\tfrac12) + 1\cdot k(1) + \tfrac32\,k(\tfrac32) + \cdots + N\,k(N), \qquad k(t) = \frac{1}{P}\cdot\frac{C(t)}{(1+y/2)^{2t}} \]

The weight k(t) is the fraction of the bond's price contributed by the cash flow at time t. The weights sum to 1, so D is a weighted average of the payment times.

Duration is the bond's "center of mass" in time — the average date you get your money back, where each date is weighted by how much PV lands on it.

For a zero coupon bond, all the money arrives at maturity, so duration = maturity exactly. For a coupon bond, some cash arrives early (the coupons), pulling the center of mass forward: duration < maturity.

This is more than a curiosity — duration turns out to be exactly the number that governs how sensitive the price is to yield changes (next box). The longer the duration, the more rate-sensitive the bond.

Price is P = Σ C(t)/(1+y/2)2t. Differentiate with respect to y:

\[ \frac{dP}{dy} = \sum_t -2t\cdot\frac{C(t)}{2(1+y/2)^{2t+1}} = -\frac{1}{1+y/2}\sum_t t\cdot\frac{C(t)}{(1+y/2)^{2t}} \]

Divide by P and pull out the weights k(t) = [C(t)/(1+y/2)2t]/P:

\[ \frac{1}{P}\frac{dP}{dy} = -\frac{1}{1+y/2}\sum_t t\,k(t) = -\frac{D}{1+y/2} \]

So the weighted-average-time object D is precisely what controls price sensitivity. That's why duration is defined this way.

Modified duration — the price-sensitivity number

Modified duration and the first-order price approximation

\[ D^* = \frac{D}{1 + y/2}, \qquad \frac{dP}{dy} = -P\,\frac{D}{1+y/2} = -P\,D^*, \qquad \Delta P \approx -P\,\frac{D}{1+y/2}\,\Delta y \]

Modified duration is "percent price drop per unit yield increase." If D* = 6 and yields rise 10 bp (Δy = 0.001), the bond loses about 6 × 0.001 = 0.6%.

Why the minus sign: yields up → price down. Always — the single most fundamental fact in fixed income.

Why it's an approximation: it's the first-order (tangent-line) term. The true price-yield curve is convex — it bends above the tangent line — so duration slightly overstates the loss when yields rise and understates the gain when they fall. For small Δy the error is tiny; the second-order correction is convexity.

Convexity note: for coupon bonds and strips, price is always a decreasing, convex function of yield. Duration is the slope of that curve (scaled); convexity is its curvature.

Duration of a portfolio

Portfolio duration (when the components share the same YTM)

\[ D^C = \omega^{A,C}\,D^A + \omega^{B,C}\,D^B \]

where ωA,C, ωB,C are the value-weights of assets A and B in portfolio C.

Duration is linear in value-weights — the portfolio's center of mass is the weighted average of its holdings' centers of mass. This is what makes immunization tractable: you can dial a portfolio's duration to any target by mixing a short-duration and a long-duration bond.

Immunization — matching duration to a horizon

Two ways to fund a stream of fixed liabilities with bonds:

  • Dedication (cash-flow matching): hold bonds whose cash flows exactly match the outflows. No rebalancing ever needed — but often impractical to construct exactly.
  • Target-date immunization: hold a portfolio with (1) the same present value as the liabilities and (2) the same duration. Then a small parallel shift in the (flat) yield curve changes the asset and liability values by the same amount — the funding stays intact. Requires re-immunizing over time as durations drift.

Target-date immunization conditions

\[ L = A \quad\text{(match value)}, \qquad D^L = D^A = \omega^{1,A}D^1 + (1-\omega^{1,A})D^2 \quad\text{(match duration)} \]

Matching value alone isn't enough — if the portfolio's duration differs from the liability's, a yield shift moves the two sides by different amounts and a funding gap opens. Matching both value and duration makes the asset and liability move in lock-step to first order, neutralizing reinvestment risk against liquidation risk.

Whiteboard example — immunizing Firm GF's liabilities

Firm GF owes $5M in 1 year and $4M in 3 years. Yield curve is flat at 10% APR semi-annual. Fund it with 1-year and 4-year strips.

Value of liabilities: L = 5M/(1.05)^2 + 4M/(1.05)^6 = 4.5351M + 2.9849M = 7.5200M Duration of liabilities (strips, so each cash flow's "duration" = its maturity): D^L = 1yr × (4.5351/7.5200) + 3yr × (2.9849/7.5200) = 1.7938 years Match value: A^1 + A^4 = 7.5200M Match duration: 1.7938 = ω^1·(1) + (1−ω^1)·(4) → ω^1 = 0.7354 So A^1 = 0.7354 × 7.5200M = 5.5302M in 1-year strips A^4 = 7.5200M − 5.5302M = 1.9898M in 4-year strips

The portfolio holds $5.53M of 1-year strips and $1.99M of 4-year strips: same value (7.52M) and same duration (1.79 yr) as the liabilities, so a parallel yield shift leaves GF still able to make both payments.

✦ remember this — Lecture 9 (Duration & bond portfolio management)
  • Two horizon risks: reinvestment risk (cash flows before T, hurt by falling yields) vs liquidation risk (cash flows after T, hurt by rising yields). Duration balances them.
  • Macaulay duration D = Σ t·k(t), the PV-weighted average payment time. Zero: D = maturity. Coupon bond: D < maturity. Higher coupon → shorter D; longer maturity → longer D.
  • Modified duration D* = D/(1+y/2) is the price sensitivity: ΔP/P ≈ −D*·Δy. Yields up → price down. Duration is the tangent; convexity is the curvature (price is a decreasing convex function of yield).
  • Portfolio duration is the value-weighted average: DC = ωADA + ωBDB. This lets you dial duration to a target.
  • Immunization: dedication = exact cash-flow match (no rebalancing). Target-date = match both value (L = A) and duration (DL = DA); re-immunize as durations drift.

Lectures 9-10 - Derivatives: payoffs & option valuation

Notation note. This section follows Lynch's conventions: X for the strike/exercise price (not K), S(T) for the underlying at expiry, and the discount factor dT(0) (price today of $1 at T) rather than (1+r)−T. Capital CX,T(0), PX,T(0) denote European call/put prices; lowercase cX,T(0), pX,T(0) denote American. Results assume a non-dividend-paying underlying unless stated.

Payoff diagrams — the building blocks

A payoff diagram plots the value of a position at time T as a function of S(T). Everything is built from four primitives:

  • Long 1 unit of the asset: a 45° line through the origin (value = S(T)).
  • Short 1 unit: a −45° line (value = −S(T)).
  • Long a discount bond, face $F: a horizontal line at $F (pays F regardless of S(T)).
  • Short a discount bond, face $F: a horizontal line at −$F.

Calls & puts — definitions and payoffs

A call gives its holder the right (not obligation) to buy the underlying at strike X; a put, the right to sell at X. European options exercise only at expiry T; American, any time up to T. The price paid for the option is a sunk cost and irrelevant to the exercise decision.

Payoffs at expiration T (X = strike, S(T) = underlying at T)

\[ \text{Call holder: } \max\{S(T) - X,\, 0\} \qquad \text{Put holder: } \max\{X - S(T),\, 0\} \]

The writer's payoff is the negative of the holder's (options are zero-sum between the two parties).

Call: if S(T) > X you exercise — buy at X an asset worth S(T), netting S(T) − X. If S(T) ≤ X you walk away (payoff 0). You'd never pay X for something worth less.

Put: if S(T) < X you exercise — sell at X an asset worth S(T), netting X − S(T). If S(T) ≥ X you walk away.

Each payoff is piecewise-linear with a single kink at S(T) = X. Note holding a put is not the same as writing a call — they have different payoff shapes.

The two long positions. Writer (short) payoffs are these reflected through the horizontal axis. Profit diagrams shift each line by the premium so it crosses zero at the breakeven.

Forward contract payoffs

A forward is an agreement to exchange cash for the asset at a preset forward price FT(0) on settlement date T. No money changes hands at initiation. Unlike an option, both sides are obligated.

Forward payoffs at settlement T (FT(0) = forward price set at time 0)

\[ \text{Buyer (long): } S(T) - F_T(0) \qquad \text{Seller (short): } F_T(0) - S(T) \]

The buyer locked in price FT(0); at T the asset is worth S(T), so the buyer gains S(T) − FT(0) (positive if the asset rose above the locked price, negative if it fell). The seller has the mirror payoff. The two sum to zero — forwards transfer risk, they don't create value.

The payoff diagram is a full 45° line (buyer) or −45° line (seller) crossing zero at S(T) = FT(0) — like a stock position but shifted, and with no premium paid up front.

Zero net supply & why people hold derivatives

  • Zero net supply. Stocks and bonds are in positive net supply (long + short positions sum to a positive number). Derivatives — forwards, futures, options — are in zero net supply: every long is matched by a short, so they sum to zero.
  • Speculation (leverage). Options give leveraged exposure: a small premium controls a large notional. (Worked example below.)
  • Hedging. Lock in a price to remove risk — e.g. a U.S. firm owing £10,000 in October buys £ forward today to kill its exchange-rate exposure.
  • Risk management / portfolio insurance. Hold the asset + a long put to floor the position value at the strike. (See protective put below.)

XYZ trades at S(0) = $50. A 6-month European call with X = $50 costs C(0) = $5. The 6-month T-bill BEY is 10%. You have $5,000 and consider three strategies:

Moneyness, intrinsic value, time value

  • In / at / out of the money: a call is ITM if S > X, a put if S < X; ATM if S = X.
  • Intrinsic value = the no-arbitrage floor (see below): max{S(0) − X·dT(0), 0} for a call.
  • Time value = option price − intrinsic value. Always ≥ 0 before expiry because the holder isn't forced to decide on exercise until T. Decays to 0 at expiration.

Put-call parity — the link between calls, puts, the stock, and a bond

Put-call parity (European, same X and T, no dividends — Lynch's form)

\[ S(0) = C_{X,T}(0) - P_{X,T}(0) + X\,d_T(0) \]

Holding the stock is equivalent to: long a call, short a put (both struck at X), plus lending X·dT(0) (a discount bond with face X). Rearrange for whatever you need: C = S(0) − X·dT(0) + P, or P = C − S(0) + X·dT(0).

The one slip people make is forgetting the discount factor — always use X·dT(0), the present value of the strike, not X itself.

Compare two portfolios' payoffs at T:

Portfolio A: long the stock. Payoff = S(T).

Portfolio B: long a call, short a put, long a discount bond with face X. Payoff = max{S(T)−X,0} − max{X−S(T),0} + X.

  • If S(T) > X: (S(T)−X) − 0 + X = S(T). ✓
  • If S(T) ≤ X: 0 − (X−S(T)) + X = S(T). ✓

Same payoff in every state ⇒ by the law of one price, same cost today: S(0) = CX,T(0) − PX,T(0) + X·dT(0). ∎

No-arbitrage bounds — the intrinsic-value floors

European call & put floors

\[ C_{X,T}(0) \ge \max\{S(0) - X\,d_T(0),\, 0\} \qquad P_{X,T}(0) \ge \max\{X\,d_T(0) - S(0),\, 0\} \]

Two floors combine. First, an option's payoff is never negative, so its value is ≥ 0. Second, no-arbitrage forces the call above S(0) − X·dT(0): a call lets you acquire the stock for X paid later, which beats buying it outright for the PV of that cost. The max of these two is the intrinsic value.

The gap between the option's actual price and its intrinsic value is the time value — positive because the holder retains the choice not to exercise until T.

Whiteboard example (Dell, 4/13/05). Dell pays no dividend before the 5/21/05 expiry (38 days out). Riskfree rate 2.76% continuously compounded → dT(0) = e−0.0276·38/365 = 0.9971. Dell stock S(0) = 36.91. For the X = 35 call:

Intrinsic value = max{S(0) − X·d_T(0), 0} = max{36.91 − 35 × 0.9971, 0} = max{2.01, 0} = 2.01 The call's ask price on the screen was 2.45 ≥ 2.01. ✓ (the 0.44 gap is time value)

Protective put / portfolio insurance

Hold the asset + a long put struck at X: payoff = max{S(T), X} — a floor at X with unlimited upside. By put-call parity this is identical to holding a long call (strike X) plus a discount bond with face X. This is the standard way to insure a portfolio's downside.

Black-Scholes model

The no-arbitrage bounds say where an option price can't be; Black-Scholes gives the exact price under stronger assumptions. Assumptions: (1) the yield curve is flat and constant (no interest-rate risk); (2) the underlying's return is lognormal with constant volatility and pays no dividends; (3) continuous trading is possible; (4) no transaction costs, taxes, or other frictions.

New notation: r′ is the continuously compounded annual riskfree rate ($1 grows to $er′t in t years, so dT(0) = e−r′T); σ is the volatility of the continuously compounded annual return on the underlying.

Black-Scholes — European call on a non-dividend stock

\[ C_{X,T}(0) = S(0)\,N(d_1) - X\,e^{-r'T}\,N(d_2), \quad d_1 = \frac{\ln(S(0)/X) + (r' + \tfrac12\sigma^2)T}{\sigma\sqrt T}, \quad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt T \]

N(·) is the cumulative standard normal. The European put follows from put-call parity. Note e−r′T = dT(0), tying this to Lynch's discount-factor notation.

You won't reproduce d₁/d₂ under exam pressure, but you must know the five inputs and how each moves the price. The deep point, shared with all no-arbitrage option pricing: the stock's expected return never enters. Only volatility matters, because the option can be hedged with the underlying — so two people who disagree about the stock's expected return still agree on the option's price.

N(d₂) is (roughly) the risk-neutral probability the call finishes in the money; N(d₁) is the call's delta — the hedge ratio, how many shares replicate the call.

Why each input moves the price the way it does

Comparative statics (the signs Lynch wants)

\[ C_{X,T}(0) = C\big(\underset{+}{S(0)},\ \underset{-}{X},\ \underset{+}{\sigma},\ \underset{+}{r'},\ \underset{+}{T}\big) \qquad P_{X,T}(0) = P\big(\underset{-}{S(0)},\ \underset{+}{X},\ \underset{+}{\sigma},\ \underset{-}{r'},\ \underset{?}{T}\big) \]

Why σ raises BOTH calls and puts — the truncation argument. More σ means more extreme S(T). The option payoff is truncated at zero on the bad side, so the holder gets the bigger favorable tail without bearing the bigger unfavorable tail. See the two-state table below: doubling the spread of S(T) (same E[S(T)] = 50) doubles both the call and put values from 5 to 15.

Why r′ helps the call, hurts the put: higher r′ shrinks dT(0), so the PV of the strike X·dT(0) falls. The call pays X later (good when its PV is smaller); the put receives X later (bad).

Why T is ambiguous for the put: longer T shrinks PV(X) received (hurts the put) but raises the volatility of S(T) (helps it). The two fight. For the call both point up (lower PV of X to pay, and more volatility), so calls unambiguously rise in T.

The σ effect, shown with two equally-likely states (same E[S(T)] = 50, strike X = 50):

VolatilityS(T) statesCall C(T) statesE[call payoff]Put P(T) statesE[put payoff]
σ[S(T)] = 1040 or 600 or 10510 or 05

Same expected stock price, triple the volatility → triple the option value, for both the call and the put. The zero-truncation means only the favorable tail is kept.

Why the expected return on the underlying does NOT affect the option price

Take a stock that pays 0 or 30 for the call (states S(T) = 20 or 80, E[S(T)] = 50, price 40, so E[return] = 25%). Now imagine a more bullish stock with states 25 or 85 (E[S(T)] = 55, same σ = 30, same price 40, so a higher E[return] = 37.5%). The call now pays 0 or 35, E[payoff] = 17.5, E[call return] = 75% vs 50% before.

The call's expected return rose — but its price today (C(0) = 10 in both) did not, because the underlying's price S(0) = 40 is the same despite the higher expected return. Higher expected return on the stock ⟹ higher expected return on the call, but the same call price. Price is set by no-arbitrage replication, not by expectations.

Implied volatility. Four of the five inputs (S, X, r′, T) are directly observable; σ is not. The σ that makes the Black-Scholes price equal the market's actual option price is the implied volatility — the market's forecast of the underlying's volatility, backed out of the option price.

Lectures 10-11 - Forwards, futures & swaps: valuation by replication

The spine of the whole second half. Every valuation here uses one move: build a synthetic copy of the contract out of stuff you can already price (the underlying + strips), lay out the cash flows in a Time-0 / Time-T table, and invoke the law of one price — the contract must cost the same as its synthetic. Put-call parity, the option floors, forward prices, covered interest parity, and swap rates are all the same trick.

A forward obligates the buyer to pay the preset forward price FT(0) at settlement T and receive the asset. Payoff: buyer S(T) − FT(0), seller FT(0) − S(T). No money changes hands at initiation — which immediately pins down FT(0) by no-arbitrage.

Forward-spot parity (no carrying costs)

Replicate "buy the forward" with "buy the asset now + borrow." The two strategies have identical cash flows at T, so they must cost the same at time 0. Lynch's gold example (7/23, spot S(0) = 400, 1-yr strip price 80 per $100 face so d1(0) = 0.8):

Forward-spot parity, no carry

\[ F_T(0) = \frac{S(0)}{d_T(0)} = S(0)\,[1 + y^*_T(0)]^T \]

The synthetic forward costs nothing at T-matching, so its time-0 cost must be zero too (the real forward costs nothing today): FT(0)·d1(0) − 400 = 0 ⟹ FT(0) = 400/0.8 = 500.

The forward price is just the future value of today's spot price, grown at the strip yield to the settlement date. It is not a forecast of S(T). The more distant the settlement, the higher the forward price (more compounding).

With carrying costs

Forward-spot parity, general (carrying cost C(τ) paid at time τ)

\[ F_T(0) = \frac{S(0) + C(\tau)\,d_\tau(0)}{d_T(0)} \]

Carrying the asset yourself may cost extra (storage for a commodity) or pay you (dividends on a stock index). Add the PV of any storage cost to the spot before growing it to T.

  • Positive carry (storage) → higher forward price. Cotton example: S(0) = 71, storage 10 paid at ½-yr (d½ = 0.9), d1 = 0.8 → F = (71 + 10·0.9)/0.8 = 100.
  • Negative carry (a dividend-paying index) → lower forward price. The dividends you'd collect by holding the asset make the synthetic cheaper, so the forward is cheaper.

Covered interest parity (forwards on a currency)

A forward to deliver £ in T years: the underlying is the foreign currency, and the "negative carry" is the foreign interest you'd earn holding £. Build a synthetic $-strip by buying £ today, investing at the £ rate, and locking in the forward exchange rate. Lynch's example (7/23: spot S$/£(0) = 1.60, 1-yr $ yield 25%, 1-yr £ yield 10%):

Covered interest parity

\[ [1 + y^{*\$}_T(0)]^T = F^{\$/£}_T(0)\cdot\frac{1}{S^{\$/£}(0)}\cdot[1 + y^{*£}_T(0)]^T \quad\Leftrightarrow\quad \frac{[1+y^{*\$}_T(0)]^T}{[1+y^{*£}_T(0)]^T} = \frac{F^{\$/£}_T(0)}{S^{\$/£}(0)} \]

Two ways to turn $1 into dollars in a year: (A) buy a 1-yr $-strip → 1.25; (B) convert to £ now, earn the £ rate, convert back at the locked forward rate. No-arbitrage forces them equal: F = 1.60·(1.25/1.10) = $1.8181/£.

The higher-yielding currency trades at a forward discount. Here $ yields (25%) > £ yields (10%), so you get more $ per £ in the future than today — the £ forward price (in $) is above spot. If the foreign currency yielded more than the dollar, its forward price would be below spot.

Futures vs forwards

  • Futures are marked-to-market daily (gains/losses settle every day); forwards settle once, at expiry.
  • Because of daily settlement, the futures price need not exactly equal the forward price — but spot-forward parity holds approximately for futures.

Interest rate swaps (introduced in L7-8) — a synthetic bond swap

An interest rate swap exchanges a stream of fixed-rate payments for a stream of floating-rate (LIBOR) payments on a notional principal. The notional itself never changes hands — only the interest streams.

  • Fixed-rate payer ("bought the swap"): pays fixed, receives floating. Synthetically = short a fixed-rate bond + long a floating-rate bond.
  • Floating-rate payer ("sold the swap"): pays floating, receives fixed. The mirror image.
  • The fixed rate is the swap rate.

The swap rate by no-arbitrage

At initiation the swap rate is set so that PV(fixed leg) = PV(floating leg). The value of the swap is then zero to both sides — which is why no money changes hands to enter it.

Same recipe as everything else in this section: a swap is a portfolio of two bonds. The fixed-rate payer is long a floating bond and short a fixed bond, both at the notional. Set the fixed coupon (the swap rate) so the two bonds have equal PV today → the swap is worth 0 at inception.

After inception the swap gains or loses value as rates move. If rates rise, the fixed-rate payer wins (they locked in paying the old, lower fixed rate while receiving the higher floating). If rates fall, the floating-rate payer wins.

Credit risk is small because only the net difference between the two legs changes hands each period — never the notional. If your counterparty defaults you lose the difference, not the whole principal.

Example. Company A (an S&L) agrees to pay Company B 10% fixed (semi-annual) on a $50M notional for 5 years; B pays A six-month LIBOR on the same notional. The floating rate resets every 6 months off the prevailing LIBOR. If LIBOR is 7% at a reset, A pays B 0.5·10%·50M = $2.5M and B pays A 0.5·7%·50M = $1.75M — only the $0.75M net actually moves. A (the fixed-rate payer) profits if rates rise above 10%.

Lecture 11 - Valuation models & capital budgeting

Valuation = figuring out what an asset is worth. Three approaches: discounted cash flow (PV of expected cash flows), relative valuation (comparable multiples like P/E), and contingent-claim valuation (option-pricing tools for assets with option features). The course's whole engine — discount expected cash flows at a risk-adjusted rate — is the DCF approach, and the rate comes straight from the CAPM SML.

General DCF / discounted dividend model

\[ P_0 = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{E[CF_t]}{(1 + E[R])^t}, \qquad E[R] = R_f + \beta\,(E[R_M] - R_f) \]

For equity, the cash flows are dividends (DDM): P0 = Σ E[Dt]/(1+E[R])t.

Price today = PV of expected future cash flows, discounted at the required return for the asset's risk. The discount rate is the SML return for the asset's β — not Rf. The formula also shows why an asset-pricing model matters for valuation: a higher required return (riskier asset) means a lower price for the same cash flows.

Constant-growth DDM (Gordon)

\[ P_0 = \frac{D_0(1+g)}{E[R] - g} = \frac{E[D_1]}{E[R] - g} \quad (\text{valid if } E[R] > g), \qquad E[R] = \frac{E[D_1]}{P_0} + g \]

A growing perpetuity of dividends. Rearranged, required return = dividend yield + growth — the g term is the expected capital gain (E[P₁] = P₀(1+g)).

IBM example (end of 2004). D₀ = $0.70, β = 1.01 → CAPM E[R] = 2.74% + 1.01·(11.88 − 2.74)% = 11.97%; analyst earnings growth g = 10.68%. Then P₀ = 0.70·(1.1068)/(0.1197 − 0.1068) = $59.99 — versus IBM's actual price of $98.58. Taken at face value, the model calls IBM overvalued (a sell). In practice the result is hugely sensitive to g, which is the model's weakness.

Capital budgeting — NPV with a CAPM discount rate

Net present value (accept if NPV > 0)

\[ NPV_0 = CF_0 + \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{E[CF_t]}{(1+E[R])^t}, \qquad E[R] = R_f + \beta_{\text{project}}(E[R_M] - R_f) \]

Discount the project's incremental expected cash flows at the required return for the project's risk — use the β of the business line, not the firm's overall β. Include only incremental cash flows; ignore sunk costs and already-committed overhead.

ZDF example. A new widget line costs $20M today and returns $5M/yr for 10 years. The required return on assets for widget-makers is 15% (use this, not ZDF's 18% or its 20% equity return). Headquarters overhead is a sunk cost — ignore it. NPV = −20 + 5·PVAF(15%, 10) = −20 + 5·5.0187 = +$5.09M > 0 → accept.

Investment opportunities: where growth g comes from (g = b·ROE)

If a firm retains a fraction b of earnings (the plowback ratio) and earns ROE on book equity, then expected earnings, dividends, and book value all grow at:

\[ g = b \cdot \text{ROE} \]

Valuation becomes P₀ = E[E₁](1 − b)/(E[R] − b·ROE). Optimal plowback: if E[R] > ROE, set b = 0 (don't reinvest — the firm destroys value reinvesting below its cost of capital); if E[R] < ROE, more plowback raises the price; if E[R] = ROE, b doesn't matter. IBM: g = 10.68%, payout (1−b) = D₀/E₀ = 0.70/4.94 = 14.2% so b = 85.8%, ROE = g/b = 12.45% > E[R] = 11.88% → P₀ rises in b.

Relative valuation — the P/E multiple

P/E valuation

\[ \frac{P_0}{E[E_1]} = \frac{1 - b}{E[R] - g}, \qquad P_0 = E_0 \cdot (P/E)_{\text{comparables}} \]

Value a firm by applying the average P/E of comparable firms to its earnings. Strength: simple and quick; works for non-traded firms. Weaknesses: "comparable" is subjective, and accounting earnings are distorted by inconsistent accounting across firms. To be truly comparable, firms need similar required return, payout, and growth.

  • DCF / DDM: P₀ = Σ E[CFt]/(1+E[R])t, discount rate from the SML. Higher required return → lower price.
  • Gordon: P₀ = E[D₁]/(E[R] − g) (need E[R] > g). Equivalently E[R] = dividend yield + g. Very sensitive to g.
  • NPV > 0 → accept. Discount incremental cash flows at the project's required return (business-line β, not firm β). Ignore sunk costs. (ZDF: NPV = +5.09M.)
  • Growth: g = b·ROE. Reinvest (b > 0) only when ROE > E[R]; otherwise pay it out.
  • P/E: P₀/E[E₁] = (1−b)/(E[R]−g); apply comparable multiples. Quick but comparables are subjective and earnings are noisy.

Lecture 11 - Market efficiency

The course's closing warning. In an efficient market the price is an unbiased estimate of value — it already reflects available information, so you can't systematically earn abnormal risk-adjusted returns. Efficiency has two dimensions: how much information is in the price, and how fast new information gets incorporated.

The efficiency identity

\[ \text{Realized return} = \text{Expected return} + \text{Unpredictable mean-zero surprise} \]

In an efficient market the only thing that moves price away from its expected path is genuinely new information — a surprise that is, by definition, unforecastable and averages to zero. Even if news itself is correlated over time, the surprises aren't: the market already priced in the predictable part.

Autocorrelation and efficiency — the subtle part

  • If expected returns are constant through time: efficiency ⟹ returns are not autocorrelated (you can't predict future returns from past returns). Autocorrelation would then signal inefficiency.
  • If expected returns vary through time (with the business cycle): returns can be autocorrelated and the market still efficient — the predictability is in the time-varying expected return, not in mispricing. So autocorrelated returns do NOT by themselves imply inefficiency.

Skill vs luck

In an efficient market, a manager's expected abnormal performance (Jensen's α in a CAPM world) is zero. In any given period some managers post great returns purely by chance, so be skeptical of a short track record. The longer the run of good performance, the more likely it's skill and not luck.

How efficient are U.S. markets?

  • Probably semi-strong efficient but not strong-form efficient. Rare semi-strong anomalies exist (e.g. the drift around S&P 500 index additions/deletions), but it's generally very hard to beat the market using only public information.
  • Mutual funds confirm it. Net of fees and trading costs, the average fund's α is negative (~−1.8%/yr; Carhart/Carpenter/Lynch/Musto 2002) — worse for high-turnover and high-expense funds. Bad performance persists (driven by costs); good performance does not persist (consistent with luck, not skill).
  • Implication: to add value, a manager needs genuine non-public "smarts," not public-data screens.

Why testing efficiency is hard

  • Joint-hypothesis problem. Any test of efficiency is simultaneously a test of the assumed asset-pricing model. Value stocks plotting above the SML implies semi-strong inefficiency if CAPM is right — but in a multifactor world the extra return could just be compensation for a priced state-variable risk. You can never cleanly separate the two. Ties straight back to CAPM's empirical failures and Fama-French.
  • Data-mining. "Seek and thou shalt find" — even truly random data show patterns if you look hard enough. In-sample predictability need not survive out-of-sample.
