Key ideas so far (through Class 5)

Refreshed after each lecture. Each lecture section also has its own "✦ remember this" callout at the end.

  1. A dollar today is worth more than a dollar later. Every valuation question reduces to: discount future cash flows by the right rate and add them up. PV, FV, annuity, perpetuity — different shapes of the same idea. Compounding is the snowball; discounting is the snowball in reverse.
  2. APR ≠ EAR. APR is a quoting convention (the per-period rate times the number of compounding periods per year). EAR is the actual annual rate you earn or pay. Always convert to EAR before comparing rates with different compounding frequencies.
  3. Continuous compounding is the limit as you compound more and more often. Continuous rates add over time instead of compounding multiplicatively, which is why all of derivatives pricing uses them.
  4. Annuities and perpetuities collapse infinite sums into one formula via the geometric series. Annuity PVAF = (1 − (1+r)−N)/r; perpetuity = C/r; growing perpetuity = C/(r−g) when r > g.
  5. Investors prefer more to less, and almost all are risk averse. Risk averse means: for a given expected return, prefer lower volatility; for a given volatility, prefer higher expected return. When one portfolio has higher E[R] AND higher σ, the call depends on the investor's risk-aversion level.
  6. Markets have structure. Limit orders provide liquidity (you sit on the book at a price); market orders demand immediacy (you cross the spread and pay the cost). Bid-ask spread is the price of immediacy and the profit of liquidity providers. Dealers manage inventory toward zero.
  7. The three ways to hold a stock — long, on margin, short — map to portfolio weights ω: in (0,1], >1 with negative riskless weight, <0 respectively. Percentage margin = Net Worth / Value of Stock; if it falls below maintenance margin, you get a margin call.
  8. The portfolio return formula R_p = Σ ωi Ri handles long, levered, and short positions with the same equation — only the signs and magnitudes of weights change. Weights sum to 1.
  9. Risk = standard deviation (for now). A return distribution is summarized by E[R] (its center) and σ (its spread). Covariance and correlation describe how two assets move together — and that joint behavior is the whole point of diversification.
  10. Regression slope = covariance / variance. b = σ[Y,X] / σ²[X]. When we run R_stock on R_market in Lecture 5, this slope becomes beta, the central risk measure in CAPM.
  11. One risky + one riskless = a Capital Allocation Line in (σ, E[R]) space. Slope = (E[Ri] − Rf) / σ[Ri] = reward per unit of risk. Between two risky assets, every risk-averse investor picks the one with the steeper CAL, regardless of their personal risk aversion.
  12. Two-fund separation (preview). Risk aversion determines where on the CAL you sit (more averse = closer to Rf, less averse = further out / on margin). It does NOT determine which CAL — that's a universal choice. This is the seed of CAPM.
  13. With two risky assets, the portfolio possibility curve bows leftward as correlation drops. Lower ρ → more diversification benefit. At ρ = −1 you can construct a riskless portfolio. Min-variance portfolio: ω₁* = (σ₂² − σ1,2) / (σ₁² + σ₂² − 2σ1,2).
  14. Adding a riskless asset changes the efficient frontier from a curve to a straight line. The CAL through the tangency portfolio T dominates every point on the curved frontier except T itself.
  15. Tangency portfolio T: for 2 assets, closed form using excess returns and covariances. For N assets, wT = Σ⁻¹μexcess / (1TΣ⁻¹μexcess). Max Sharpe ratio: √(μexcessTΣ⁻¹μexcess).
  16. For N assets, equal-weighted portfolio variance decomposes as σ²/N + (1 − 1/N) × avg covariance. First term → 0 (diversifiable / idiosyncratic risk). Second term → average covariance (systematic / undiversifiable risk). Even infinite diversification can't get below √ρ̄ × σ.
  17. Minimum variance frontier (MVF) is the leftmost boundary of achievable (σ, E[R]) — a hyperbola in (σ, μ) space, parabola in (σ², μ) space. Efficient frontier = upper half. Adding risky assets always shifts MVF left.
  18. Two-fund separation theorem: with N risky assets + Rf, every risk-averse mean-variance investor holds the same tangency portfolio T, just in different mix with Rf. The asset-selection problem and the risk-level problem fully decouple. This is the engine of CAPM.
  19. No arbitrage / law of one price. Two assets with identical cash flows must have the same price. Any price difference (after taxes, liquidity, transaction costs) is an arbitrage opportunity, and well-functioning markets don't let those last.
  20. Market efficiency is a warning. If U.S. equity prices are efficient w.r.t. public information, you can't beat the market on average using public info, risk-adjusted. This is the big warning the course returns to at the end.
  21. Option value is non-negative. A right (without obligation) to do something in the future must be worth ≥ 0 today. (Comes back in Lecture 10.)

Overview - what finance is about

Asset classes

  • Real assets: physical things - land, buildings, machines.
  • Financial assets (securities): claims on real or financial assets. Four flavors:
    • Money - medium of exchange.
    • Debt - claim to a predetermined payment stream.
    • Equity - residual claim on a firm's assets, usually with corporate control rights.
    • Derivatives - payoff depends on the value of another asset.

What the financial system does

  1. Allocates capital from savers to capital users; disciplines firm investment decisions.
  2. Lets investors smooth consumption over time.
  3. Repackages risk so investors bear less of it.
  4. Disseminates information (prices aggregate beliefs).
  5. Lets investors exploit economies of scale in trading.

Markets

  • Primary vs secondary: primary = new issues sold to initial buyers; secondary = previously issued securities trade among investors.
  • Money vs capital: money = short-term debt (< 1 yr maturity); capital = long-term debt and equity.
  • Mutual funds & index funds: low-cost vehicles for individuals to hold diversified portfolios. The S&P 500 is value-weighted (more $ in big firms), 500 stocks chosen to mirror the U.S. market.

Core concepts the course returns to

  • Investor preferences: all prefer more to less; almost all are risk averse.
  • Time value of money: a dollar today > a dollar later.
  • Diversification: portfolio volatility falls as assets are added; average returns are unchanged.
  • Risk adjustment: riskier assets must offer higher expected returns.
  • No arbitrage: two assets with the same cash flows must have the same price (law of one price). Differences only arise from taxes, liquidity, or transaction costs.
  • Option value: a right without an obligation has non-negative value today.
  • Market efficiency: in an efficient market, price is an unbiased estimate of value. The U.S. stock market is probably efficient w.r.t. publicly available info → you can't beat it on average using public info, risk-adjusted.
✦ remember this — Lecture 1
  • Four asset types: money, debt, equity, derivatives. Three things the financial system does that matter: allocate capital, smooth consumption, repackage risk.
  • Seven core concepts the course returns to: risk aversion, time value of money, diversification, risk adjustment, no arbitrage, option value, market efficiency. Everything that follows is just formal machinery for one of these.
  • Mutual funds (especially index funds) exist because of market efficiency. If you can't beat the market on average using public info, the rational play is to own the market cheaply.

Lectures 1–2 - Time Value of Money

The atomic operation of finance: take a cash flow at one date and figure out its equivalent value at another date, given an interest rate. Everything else stacks on top of this.

Single cash flows (discrete compounding)

Future value

\[ V_{t+n} \;=\; V_t \, (1 + r)^n \]

Money in a bank earns interest. Put $100 in a 5% account: one year later it's $105. The next year, that whole $105 earns 5%, so you get $110.25 - slightly more than $110, because you also earned interest on the interest. The (1+r)n just stacks that little bit of "interest on interest" n times.

Think of it like a snowball rolling downhill: each period it picks up new snow proportional to how big it already is, so it grows faster the longer it rolls. That's compounding - and it's why finance people obsess over starting to invest early.

Start with one period. By definition of "effective per-period rate r," money at t grows to:

\[ V_{t+1} \;=\; V_t (1 + r) \]

Apply the same rule again to Vt+1:

\[ V_{t+2} \;=\; V_{t+1}(1+r) \;=\; V_t (1+r)^2 \]

Iterating n times stacks the factor:

\[ V_{t+n} \;=\; V_t (1+r)^n \]

This is just "interest on interest" written compactly. The exponent counts how many compoundings happen between the two dates.

Present value

\[ V_t \;=\; V_{t+n} \, (1 + r)^{-n} \]

If someone promises you $110.25 in two years, how much would you pay for that promise today? You'd pay less than $110.25 - because if you had the money now, you could invest it and grow it yourself. At a 5% rate, you'd pay exactly $100.

"Discounting" is just compounding run in reverse. The further in the future the cash, the less it's worth today. This is the single most important idea in finance: every valuation - stocks, bonds, businesses - is some version of "what's this future cash worth today?"

Solve the future-value formula for Vt. Divide both sides by (1+r)n:

\[ V_t \;=\; \frac{V_{t+n}}{(1+r)^n} \;=\; V_{t+n} (1+r)^{-n} \]

So present-valuing is just future-valuing in reverse - discount instead of compound.

Effective n-period rate from the per-period rate

\[ r_n \;=\; (1 + r)^n - 1 \]

If you earn 5% per year for two years, your total return isn't 10% - it's slightly more (10.25%) because of compounding. This formula converts a "per-period" rate into the actual cumulative growth rate over n periods.

Useful when you need to compare returns over different timeframes: "is 8% over 5 years better than 7% over 6 years?" Convert both to the same horizon using this formula.

By definition, the effective n-period rate rn satisfies:

\[ V_{t+n} \;=\; V_t (1 + r_n) \]

But the future-value formula says Vt+n = Vt (1+r)n. Equating the two:

\[ V_t (1+r_n) \;=\; V_t (1+r)^n \quad\Rightarrow\quad 1 + r_n \;=\; (1+r)^n \]

Subtract 1 to get the formula. The "n-period rate" is just the cumulative growth factor minus one.

r is the effective rate per period, expressed as a decimal. The period must match the interval between cash flows - if cash flows are monthly, r is the monthly rate.

APR (nominal rate) vs EAR (effective annual rate)

The APR inom with m compounding periods per year is the per-period rate times m. It's a quoting convention, not a real rate. The EAR is the actual annual rate you earn.

Definition: per-period rate from APR

\[ r_{1/m} \;=\; \frac{i_{\text{nom}}}{m} \]

APR ("annual percentage rate") is just the banks' favorite quoting convention. When your credit card says "18% APR with monthly compounding," they really charge you 18% ÷ 12 = 1.5% per month. APR is the headline number; the per-period rate is what actually compounds.

So APR alone is meaningless without knowing how often it compounds. Two different lenders quoting the same APR can be charging very different amounts.

By convention, the APR is the per-period rate times the number of periods per year - a quoting rule, nothing more.

APR → EAR

\[ \text{EAR} \;=\; \left( 1 + \frac{i_{\text{nom}}}{m} \right)^{\!m} - 1 \]

EAR is the real annual cost - what you'd compare across loans. APR is the quoted number; EAR is what it actually costs you over a year after compounding.

An 18% APR compounded monthly is really 19.56% per year. The extra 1.56% is interest charged on interest you've already accrued during the year. The more frequently a loan compounds (daily > monthly > annually), the bigger the gap between APR and EAR.

The rule of thumb: if you're comparing two loans with the same APR, the one that compounds more frequently is the worse deal.

The per-period rate is r1/m = inom/m. A year contains m compounding periods, so applying the effective-n-period-rate formula with n = m:

\[ \text{EAR} \;=\; (1 + r_{1/m})^m - 1 \;=\; \left(1 + \tfrac{i_{\text{nom}}}{m}\right)^m - 1 \]

Two APRs with the same headline number compound differently if m differs - e.g., 18% APR compounded monthly (EAR ≈ 19.56%) vs daily (EAR ≈ 19.72%).

EAR → APR

\[ i_{\text{nom}} \;=\; \left[ (1 + r)^{1/m} - 1 \right] m \]

The reverse direction: you know what an account actually earns per year (EAR), and you want to back out the per-period rate so you can compare against a bank's APR quote, or use it in a monthly cash-flow calculation.

This is the operation a mortgage calculator performs internally - given an EAR, figure out the monthly rate to apply to the loan balance each month.

If the effective annual rate is r, the effective (1/m)-year rate satisfies:

\[ (1 + r_{1/m})^m \;=\; 1 + r \quad\Rightarrow\quad r_{1/m} \;=\; (1+r)^{1/m} - 1 \]

By definition, the APR is m × r1/m. Multiplying through gives the formula.

Watch out: APR with monthly compounding ≠ APR with annual compounding even if they're the same number. Always convert to EAR before comparing rates with different compounding frequencies.

Continuous compounding

Continuous rate from / to effective rate

\[ r' \;=\; \ln(1 + r) \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad r \;=\; e^{r'} - 1 \]

Imagine compounding interest more and more often: yearly, then monthly, daily, hourly, every second… each increase in frequency gives you a little more. You might guess "infinitely often = infinite money," but it doesn't - the gains taper off and approach a hard limit. That limit is the natural exponential function ex.

Continuous compounding is just the idealization of "compounded as often as possible." If a bank quotes an effective annual rate of 12%, the continuously compounded equivalent is about 11.33% - slightly lower, because continuous compounding makes each tiny bit of interest work harder.

Why bother? Because the math is way cleaner. Continuous rates add over time (5% for 2 years = 10% total), while discrete rates multiply ((1.05)2). Almost everything in derivatives pricing is in continuous time for this reason.

Start with an APR of inom compounded m times per year. The EAR is:

\[ \text{EAR}_m \;=\; \left(1 + \tfrac{i_{\text{nom}}}{m}\right)^m - 1 \]

Let m → ∞ (compound infinitely many times per year). Use the standard limit:

\[ \lim_{m \to \infty} \left(1 + \tfrac{x}{m}\right)^m \;=\; e^x \]

(This is one definition of e; equivalently ex = Σ xk/k!.) So:

\[ \text{EAR}_\infty \;=\; e^{i_{\text{nom}}} - 1 \]

Calling the continuously compounded rate r' = inom and the effective annual rate r = EAR∞:

\[ r \;=\; e^{r'} - 1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad r' \;=\; \ln(1+r) \]

Intuition: continuous compounding pushes you from a discrete (1+r/m)m to the smooth exponential er'. For small rates, r' ≈ r (since ln(1+r) ≈ r).

FV and PV with continuous compounding

\[ V_{t+n} \;=\; V_t \, e^{n \, r'} \qquad V_t \;=\; V_{t+n} \, e^{-n \, r'} \]

Same idea as discrete FV/PV - money grows or shrinks at a rate - but with the smooth exponential ex in place of (1+r)n. Time enters the formula as a smooth continuous variable instead of in discrete chunks.

The key magic: the exponent is just (rate × time). So a 5% continuous rate for 3 years grows your money by e0.15. To run it backwards (PV), you put a minus sign in the exponent. Nothing fancier than that.

Plug 1 + r = er' into the discrete FV formula:

\[ V_{t+n} \;=\; V_t (1+r)^n \;=\; V_t \left(e^{r'}\right)^n \;=\; V_t \, e^{n r'} \]

PV is the algebraic inverse. The exponent scales linearly with time, which is the property that makes continuous rates so clean.

Continuously compounded n-period rate

\[ r'_n \;=\; n \, r' \]

Discrete rates compound: 5% per year for 2 years is (1.05)2 − 1 = 10.25%, not 10%. Continuous rates just add: 5% per year for 2 years is exactly 10% total continuous.

This is the entire reason continuous compounding exists in finance theory - addition is simpler than multiplication, especially when you're stacking many different time periods, like in options pricing where you compound from "today" through a moving expiration date.

By definition the n-period continuous rate satisfies Vt+n/Vt = er'n. From the FV formula above, that ratio is en r'. So:

\[ e^{r'_n} \;=\; e^{n r'} \quad\Rightarrow\quad r'_n \;=\; n \, r' \]

Continuous rates simply add over time, instead of compounding multiplicatively like discrete rates do. This is why Black-Scholes is written in continuous time - the algebra is dramatically cleaner.

Used heavily in derivatives pricing (Black-Scholes) because continuous rates add instead of compounding multiplicatively - much cleaner algebra.

Annuities - N equal cash flows at equal intervals

First cash flow at t+1, last at t+N. r = effective per-period rate.

PV annuity factor - value one period BEFORE the first cash flow

\[ \text{PVAF}(r, N) \;=\; \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{r} \qquad V_t \;=\; C \cdot \text{PVAF}(r, N) \]

An annuity is just a stream of equal payments - like a salary, a mortgage payment, or a fixed pension. To value the whole stream today, you'd discount each future payment back to today and add them all up.

Doing that summation by hand for 360 mortgage payments would be miserable. The PVAF is a shortcut: a single number you multiply by the per-period payment C to get the present value. Think of it as "how many years of cash you're effectively buying" - a 30-year mortgage at 6% has PVAF ≈ 167 monthly periods, meaning $400K today buys $400K/167 ≈ $2,398/month.

The factor is always less than N because future dollars are worth less than today's.

The annuity pays C at t+1, t+2, …, t+N. The PV is the sum of PVs of each cash flow:

\[ V_t \;=\; \frac{C}{(1+r)} + \frac{C}{(1+r)^2} + \cdots + \frac{C}{(1+r)^N} \]

Factor out C and recognize a finite geometric series with first term a = 1/(1+r) and common ratio q = 1/(1+r):

\[ V_t \;=\; C \sum_{k=1}^{N} q^k \;=\; C \cdot a \cdot \frac{1 - q^N}{1 - q} \]

Plug in a and q:

\[ V_t \;=\; C \cdot \frac{1}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{1 - \tfrac{1}{1+r}} \]

The denominator simplifies: 1 − 1/(1+r) = r/(1+r). So:

\[ V_t \;=\; C \cdot \frac{1}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{r/(1+r)} \;=\; C \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{r} \]

The "magic" is just the geometric series sum formula. Sanity checks:

  • If N = 1: PVAF = (1 − (1+r)−1)/r = 1/(1+r). ✓ (one cash flow, one period away)
  • If N → ∞: PVAF → 1/r - that's the perpetuity formula. ✓

FV annuity factor - value AT the last cash flow

\[ \text{FVAF}(r, N) \;=\; \frac{(1+r)^N - 1}{r} \qquad V_{t+N} \;=\; C \cdot \text{FVAF}(r, N) \]

This is the retirement-savings formula. You save the same amount C every period (say, $500/month into a 401(k)) for N periods at rate r. How much do you have at the end?

The first contribution gets to compound for the longest, the last one barely compounds at all. The FVAF accounts for all those overlapping compounding windows in one closed-form number.

For 30 years of monthly savings at 7% APR, FVAF ≈ 1,220. So $500/month becomes 500 × 1,220 ≈ $610K - way more than 30 × 12 × $500 = $180K of contributions. The other ~$430K is pure compounding magic.

The cash flow received at t+k grows for N−k more periods to time t+N:

\[ V_{t+N} \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{N} C \, (1+r)^{N-k} \;=\; C \sum_{j=0}^{N-1} (1+r)^j \]

This is a geometric series with first term 1 and ratio q = (1+r):

\[ V_{t+N} \;=\; C \cdot \frac{(1+r)^N - 1}{(1+r) - 1} \;=\; C \cdot \frac{(1+r)^N - 1}{r} \]

Equivalently, take the PV annuity value Vt and compound it forward N periods - same answer:

\[ V_{t+N} \;=\; V_t (1+r)^N \;=\; C \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{r} \cdot (1+r)^N \;=\; C \cdot \frac{(1+r)^N - 1}{r} \]

Whiteboard example: Tom's retirement — two chained annuities

Tom is 35 today and wants to retire at 65. After retirement, he wants to withdraw $10,000 each year for 10 years (so 10 birthday withdrawals on his 66th through 75th birthdays). His savings earn 8% per year (EAR). Two scenarios:

(A) Tom deposits an equal amount each year from age 36 to 65 (30 annual deposits) to fund the withdrawals.
(B) Tom inherits a lump sum and deposits it once, on his 36th birthday.

Step 1 (both scenarios): How much money does he need at age 65?

On his 65th birthday (just after the last deposit), the balance must be enough to fund 10 future withdrawals of $10,000. That's a PV annuity at age 65, with first cash flow at age 66, last at age 75, r = 8%, N = 10.

PVAF(0.08, 10) = [1 − (1.08)^(−10)] / 0.08 = [1 − 0.46319] / 0.08 ≈ 6.7101 Balance needed at 65 = $10,000 × 6.7101 ≈ $67,101

This intermediate number is the bridge between the two halves of the problem.

Scenario A: equal annual deposits, 30 of them

30 deposits at ages 36 through 65 must accumulate to $67,101 at age 65. That's an FV annuity at age 65 with the last cash flow on the same day as the target — exactly the setup for FVAF.

FVAF(0.08, 30) = [(1.08)^30 − 1] / 0.08 = [10.0627 − 1] / 0.08 ≈ 113.28 C = 67,101 / 113.28 ≈ $592 per year

So if Tom puts away ~$592 every birthday for 30 years, he can withdraw $10K every birthday for 10 years after retirement. Total deposited: 30 × $592 = $17,760. Total withdrawn: 10 × $10K = $100,000. Compounding does the other ~$82K of work.

Scenario B: one lump-sum deposit at age 36

If Tom deposits a single sum at age 36, it grows for 29 years (from age 36 to age 65) at 8%. It needs to reach $67,101 by age 65.

Required lump sum × (1.08)^29 = 67,101 (1.08)^29 ≈ 9.317 Lump sum = 67,101 / 9.317 ≈ $7,201

About $7,200 today funds the entire retirement income stream — because it has 29 years to compound at 8% before withdrawals begin, multiplying it by ~9.3×. Then the balance fights off the withdrawals because it keeps earning interest during the payout phase too.

Comparison: 30 deposits × $592 = $17,760 total cash out of pocket (Scenario A), vs $7,201 lump sum today (Scenario B). The lump sum is <50% as much cash but achieves the same retirement income — because every dollar deposited at 36 has 29 years to compound, while the average deposit in Scenario A has only ~15 years.

Amortization: a level-payment loan is an annuity from the lender's side. Outstanding balance after any payment = PV of remaining payments. Interest accrued in a period = balance × per-period rate; the rest of the payment goes to principal.

Whiteboard example: 30-year mortgage

You take a $400,000 mortgage. 30 years (360 monthly payments). APR = 6% compounded monthly, so the per-month rate is r = 0.06 / 12 = 0.005.

(a) Monthly payment

The bank lends you $400,000 today; you make 360 equal payments. So $400,000 = C × PVAF(0.005, 360). Solve for C:

PVAF(0.005, 360) = [1 − (1.005)^(−360)] / 0.005 = [1 − 0.16604] / 0.005 = 0.83396 / 0.005 ≈ 166.79 C = 400,000 / 166.79 ≈ $2,398.20 per month

(b) How the first payment breaks down

Interest accrued in month 1: $400,000 × 0.005 = $2,000.00 Principal paid in month 1: $2,398.20 − $2,000 = $398.20 Loan balance after payment 1: $400,000 − $398.20 = $399,601.80

The first month, almost the entire payment goes to interest. Welcome to a mortgage.

(c) How the very last payment breaks down

By month 360, the balance is down to almost zero. Payment is still $2,398.20, but now it's almost all principal and almost no interest. The interest portion shrinks each month as the balance shrinks.

(d) Balance after 10 years (just after payment 120)

The outstanding balance equals the PV of the remaining 240 payments at the per-period rate:

PVAF(0.005, 240) = [1 − (1.005)^(−240)] / 0.005 = [1 − 0.30207] / 0.005 ≈ 139.58 Balance = $2,398.20 × 139.58 ≈ $334,750

After 10 years of payments ($287,784 total paid in), you've only knocked the balance down from $400K to ~$335K. The other ~$222K of your payments went to interest. Mortgages are front-loaded with interest — a key reason early extra principal payments save so much money.

(e) Refinance comparison

Suppose 10 years in, rates have dropped to 4% APR. Should you refinance? Your remaining balance is $334,750. Pay it off as a new 20-year loan at 4%/12 ≈ 0.333%/month:

New PVAF(0.00333, 240) = [1 − (1.00333)^(−240)] / 0.00333 ≈ 165.02 New monthly payment = 334,750 / 165.02 ≈ $2,028 per month Savings: $2,398 − $2,028 = $370 per month × 240 months = $88,800 over the life of the loan (ignoring closing costs)

If closing costs are, say, $5,000, you'd break even in ~14 months and save $80K+ over the remaining loan life. Refinancing math is literally just the annuity formula run twice.

Perpetuities - equal cash flows forever

PV perpetuity (value one period BEFORE the first cash flow)

\[ V_t \;=\; \frac{C}{r} \]

A perpetuity is a magic asset that pays you the same amount, forever. You might think it would be worth infinite - but it isn't, because each future payment is worth less than the one before. At a 10% discount rate, getting $1 forever is worth exactly $10 today.

Here's the most intuitive way to see why V = C/r: if you had X dollars in a 10% bank account, you'd earn X × 10% in interest each year without touching the principal. For that interest to be exactly C, you need X = C / r. So a perpetuity paying C per year is equivalent to a pile of money C/r earning interest.

This formula is the foundation of stock valuation, real estate cap rates, and "what's a business worth" rules of thumb.

Path 1: limit of the annuity formula. Take N → ∞ in PVAF:

\[ V_t \;=\; \lim_{N \to \infty} C \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-N}}{r} \]

Since r > 0, (1+r)−N → 0. So Vt = C/r.

Path 2: infinite geometric series directly. Sum PVs of all future cash flows:

\[ V_t \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{C}{(1+r)^k} \;=\; \frac{C}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - \tfrac{1}{1+r}} \;=\; \frac{C}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1+r}{r} \;=\; \frac{C}{r} \]

Same answer. Note this requires r > 0 - otherwise the geometric series diverges.

Growing perpetuity (cash flow grows at g per period; requires r > g)

\[ V_t \;=\; \frac{C_1}{r - g} \]

Like a regular perpetuity, but the cash flow grows over time - e.g., a dividend that increases 3% per year forever. This is the famous Gordon growth model used to value stocks.

The intuition: growth acts like a partial refund on the discount rate. If your discount rate is 9% and cash flows grow 3% per year, it's as if you're only really discounting at 9% − 3% = 6%. The effective discount is the gap between the two.

The "r > g" requirement is real, not a technicality. If cash flows grow faster than you discount them, the math says the asset is infinitely valuable - which is finance's way of telling you the assumption is unrealistic. No real cash flow grows faster than the discount rate forever.

Cash flow at t+k is C1 (1+g)k−1. PV sum:

\[ V_t \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{C_1 (1+g)^{k-1}}{(1+r)^k} \;=\; \frac{C_1}{1+r} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1+g}{1+r}\right)^{\!k} \]

Infinite geometric series with ratio q = (1+g)/(1+r). Converges iff |q| < 1, i.e., r > g. Sum is 1/(1−q):

\[ V_t \;=\; \frac{C_1}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - \tfrac{1+g}{1+r}} \;=\; \frac{C_1}{1+r} \cdot \frac{1+r}{r-g} \;=\; \frac{C_1}{r-g} \]

Special case g = 0 recovers the flat perpetuity Vt = C/r. If g ≥ r the value is infinite (or negative) - the series diverges. In finance "r > g" is a real constraint, not a technicality - perpetual growth at a rate above the discount rate would make any company infinitely valuable.

Lecture 2 - Equity markets

Primary market

  • Firm picks a lead underwriter (an investment bank) who contacts potential buyers.
  • Firm-commitment underwriting (the common case): the bank buys the shares from the firm and resells to the public at a markup. Best-efforts: bank doesn't take inventory, just helps place.
  • Types of issues: IPO (first public offering), seasoned issue, private placement (sold to a handful of institutions).

IPO stylized facts

  • Most IPOs are oversubscribed at the offer price.
  • Issue price is on average below the first-day market price → "IPO underpricing." Reason: informed investors only subscribe to underpriced IPOs, uninformed get full allocation of overpriced (bad) deals and only a fraction of underpriced (good) ones. To break even on average, deals must be underpriced.
  • Long-run: IPOs underperform comparable stocks.

Secondary market mechanics

  • Call auction: all orders aggregated at a fixed time, trade clears at the equilibrium price.
  • Continuous auction: trades happen throughout the day at prices that bounce around equilibrium. Solves the immediacy problem.
  • Two continuous auction structures:
    • Limit order ("order-driven") markets - used for stocks, options, futures.
    • Dealer markets - used for bonds, currencies, swaps, OTC derivatives. Dealers post bid/ask quotes and are always counterparty to customer trades.

Limit order book

  • Market order: execute now at best available price. Limit order: execute only at your stated price or better (provides liquidity).
  • Order book = collection of unexecuted limit orders, sorted in price-time priority (higher bids first; if tied, earlier order first).
  • Bid = highest unfilled limit buy = price a market seller receives. Ask = lowest unfilled limit sell = price a market buyer pays.
  • Spread = Ask − Bid > 0. Profit earned by limit orders for providing immediacy. Spread is the cost of demanding immediacy via a market order.
  • Marketable limit order: a newly-arrived limit order that can execute immediately against the book. Buy priced ≥ lowest ask, or sell priced ≤ highest bid.

How dealers set quotes

Dealers don't want directional exposure → they manage inventory toward zero.

  • Inventory too high (more sells than buys hitting them) → lower bid (discourage selling) and lower ask (encourage buying).
  • Inventory too low (more buys than sells) → raise bid (encourage selling to them) and raise ask (discourage buying from them).

Determinants of bid-ask spread

  • Volume: higher volume → lower spread (less inventory risk per trade).
  • Volatility: higher volatility → higher spread (more inventory risk).
  • Competition between dealers: more competition → tighter spreads.
  • Adverse selection: if market orders are coming from better-informed counterparties, dealers widen spreads to compensate.

Trading costs

  1. Commission - broker's fee.
  2. Bid-ask spread - cost of immediacy (market orders).
  3. Market impact - for large orders, you move the price against yourself.

U.S. secondary market structure

  • Most stocks trade in a fragmented, hybrid market - many venues (NYSE, NASDAQ, BATS, Direct Edge, ECNs), many mechanisms.
  • Exchanges linked by the consolidated quotation system. NBBO = National Best Bid and Offer = highest bid and lowest ask across all linked venues. Reg NMS forces routing to the venue with the better quote.
  • Why people trade: information advantage, liquidity needs (raise cash / deploy cash), or rebalancing.

  • Limit orders provide liquidity; market orders take it. Limit orders sit on the book at a price; market orders cross the spread. Spread = Ask − Bid > 0 is the price of immediacy.

Lectures 2-3 - Stock positions & portfolio return

Return formula

Single-period return on an asset from t to t+1:

\[ R_{t+1} \;=\; \frac{p_{t+1} + c_{t+1} - p_t}{p_t} \;=\; \frac{p_{t+1} + c_{t+1}}{p_t} - 1 \]

Return is just the percentage change in your wealth over the period. Buy a stock at $100, sell at $110 → 10% return. If it also paid a $2 dividend, your total return is $12 / $100 = 12%.

The key idea: cash you got in your pocket (the dividend) and price appreciation count the same toward your return. The formula reflects that by putting price gain and cash flow in the numerator. This is sometimes called "total return."

where p is price and c is any cash flow paid (dividend, coupon). Convention: returns are calculated close-to-close.

Percentage margin

\[ \text{Margin} \;=\; \frac{\text{Net Worth}}{\text{Value of Stock}} \]

"Margin" tells you what fraction of your stock position is funded by your own money vs borrowed money. 50% margin means half your stock is yours, half is borrowed from the broker. 100% margin means you bought with all cash (no borrowing).

When the stock falls, your equity shrinks faster than the loan shrinks (the loan doesn't shrink - you still owe it). So your percentage margin falls. If it drops below the broker's maintenance margin (often 25–30%), you get a margin call: put up more cash or they liquidate.

Margin is your skin in the game. Lower margin = more leverage = bigger gains AND bigger losses.

  • U.S. initial margin minimum: 50%. If margin falls below the broker's maintenance margin, you must either post collateral or close out.
  • Long on margin: margin = % decline in stock price that wipes out your net worth.
  • Short selling: margin = % increase in stock price that wipes out your net worth.

Portfolio return formula

Return on a portfolio of N assets

\[ R_p \;=\; \omega_1 R_1 + \omega_2 R_2 + \cdots + \omega_N R_N \qquad \text{with} \quad \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i = 1 \]

Your portfolio's return is just a weighted average of the returns on each thing you own. If 60% of your money is in Stock A (which returned 10%) and 40% in Stock B (which returned 5%), your portfolio returned 0.6 × 10% + 0.4 × 5% = 8%. Simple as that.

The weights ωi are the fraction of your starting wealth in each asset. They must sum to 1, because the pieces add up to the whole. The interesting bit is that weights can be negative (when you short) or greater than 1 (when you buy on margin) — see the table below.

This formula is the foundation of everything in portfolio theory. Mean-variance, CAPM, the efficient frontier — they're all just questions about how to pick the weights ωi.

Let W0 be your starting wealth. By definition of "weight," the dollar amount in asset i at the start is ωi W0. Over one period, asset i's value grows to ωi W0 (1 + Ri).

End-of-period wealth is the sum across all assets:

\[ W_1 \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i W_0 (1 + R_i) \;=\; W_0 \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i (1 + R_i) \]

Portfolio return is end-over-start minus 1:

\[ R_p \;=\; \frac{W_1}{W_0} - 1 \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i (1 + R_i) - 1 \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i R_i - 1 \]

Since the weights sum to 1, the first sum is just 1 and cancels the trailing −1:

\[ R_p \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i R_i \]

So the return on a portfolio is exactly the weighted average of the asset returns, with portfolio weights as the weights. No surprise — but it relies on Σ ωi = 1 to work cleanly.

Lecture 3 - Characterizing the return distribution

So far we've talked about returns as if we already knew them. But the return on a risky asset is uncertain — we only know its distribution. To build a portfolio we need to summarize that distribution. Two moments do the job: expected return (the center) and variance (the spread). When we have multiple assets, we also need covariance to capture how they move together.

Notation

Suppose there are K possible "states of the world" at the end of the period (e.g., expansion, recession, normal). Each state s has probability prob(s) and a known return for each asset in that state. The probabilities sum to 1.

Expected return

\[ E[R_A] \;=\; \sum_{s=1}^{K} \text{prob}(s) \cdot R_A(s) \]

The expected return is just the probability-weighted average return across all the possible outcomes. If a stock returns 20% half the time and −10% the other half, its expected return is 0.5 × 20% + 0.5 × (−10%) = 5%.

"Expected" doesn't mean "what's most likely to happen" — it means the long-run average if you played the same gamble many times. In any single year you'll get one specific outcome, not the expected value.

This is also called the mean of the return distribution. It's the single best one-number summary of "what return should I bake into my plans?"

This is just the definition of expectation for a discrete random variable. If RA can take values RA(s1), RA(s2), … with probabilities prob(s1), prob(s2), …, then:

\[ E[R_A] \;=\; \sum_s \text{prob}(s) \cdot R_A(s) \]

The weights are probabilities; they sum to 1; we average. Nothing fancier.

Variance and standard deviation

\[ \sigma^2[R_A] \;=\; \sum_{s} \text{prob}(s) \cdot \left(R_A(s) - E[R_A]\right)^2 \qquad \sigma[R_A] \;=\; \sqrt{\sigma^2[R_A]} \]

Variance measures how spread-out the possible returns are around the mean. For each possible outcome, you take the gap between that outcome and the expected return, square it (so positive and negative gaps both count as "spread"), and average across all the states.

Two assets can have the same expected return but very different variance: one delivers 5% reliably every year, the other ranges from −30% to +40%. The second is way "riskier" in the everyday sense, and variance captures that.

Standard deviation (the square root of variance) is what people actually quote, because it has the same units as the return (percent). A stock with σ = 20% has typical year-to-year swings of roughly ±20% around its mean. Variance has units of "percent squared," which is unintuitive.

In finance, "risk" usually means standard deviation of returns — at least until we get to CAPM where the relevant risk measure changes.

Variance is the expectation of the squared deviation from the mean — the standard definition from statistics:

\[ \sigma^2[R_A] \;=\; E\!\left[(R_A - E[R_A])^2\right] \]

Expanding the expectation for a discrete random variable:

\[ \sigma^2[R_A] \;=\; \sum_s \text{prob}(s) \cdot (R_A(s) - E[R_A])^2 \]

Why square the deviation? Because if you just averaged the raw deviations (RA(s) − E[RA]), they'd cancel out and you'd always get zero — the mean is by construction the balancing point of the distribution. Squaring makes them all positive so they accumulate.

Standard deviation is simply √variance to recover the original units.

Covariance and correlation

Covariance between two assets' returns:

\[ \sigma[R_A, R_B] \;=\; \sum_{s} \text{prob}(s) \cdot (R_A(s) - E[R_A])(R_B(s) - E[R_B]) \]

Correlation (standardized version):

\[ \rho[R_A, R_B] \;=\; \frac{\sigma[R_A, R_B]}{\sigma[R_A]\, \sigma[R_B]} \qquad \rho \in [-1, +1] \]

Covariance asks: when asset A has a good year (above its mean), does asset B tend to also have a good year? If yes, covariance is positive. If A's good years coincide with B's bad years, covariance is negative. If they move independently, covariance is zero.

Think of two stocks: an airline and an oil producer. When oil prices spike, oil stocks soar while airlines suffer (jet fuel is a huge cost). They have negative covariance. Now imagine two banks — they move together because they're exposed to the same interest-rate environment. Strong positive covariance.

Correlation is just covariance rescaled to fit between −1 and +1, so you can compare across asset pairs that have wildly different volatilities. ρ = +1 = perfectly in sync. ρ = 0 = unrelated. ρ = −1 = perfectly opposite. Real assets are usually somewhere in between — most stocks are mildly positively correlated with each other (most things go up when the economy is good).

This is the magic ingredient in diversification: if you combine assets that aren't perfectly correlated, the portfolio's overall risk is less than the weighted average of the individual risks. That's the entire point of holding more than one thing.

Covariance is the expected product of the deviations of each asset from its own mean:

\[ \sigma[R_A, R_B] \;=\; E\!\left[(R_A - E[R_A])(R_B - E[R_B])\right] \]

For a discrete distribution this expands to the probability-weighted sum shown above. Two important special cases:

  • A and itself: σ[RA, RA] = E[(RA − E[RA])²] = σ²[RA]. Covariance with self = variance.
  • Correlation = ±1 means perfect linear dependence. If RB = a + b RA for constants a, b, then ρ = +1 (if b > 0) or −1 (if b < 0).

Covariance has awkward units (return × return) and depends on the scale of the assets. Correlation is the cleaned-up version: divide by both standard deviations to get a unit-free number that always lies in [−1, +1].

\[ \rho[R_A, R_B] \;=\; \frac{\sigma[R_A, R_B]}{\sigma[R_A] \, \sigma[R_B]} \]

By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, this ratio is always between −1 and 1, so correlation is a clean comparable measure across any pair of assets.

Linear regression

Regression of Y on X

\[ Y \;=\; a + b X + e \] \[ b \;=\; \frac{\sigma[Y, X]}{\sigma^2[X]} \qquad a \;=\; E[Y] - b \, E[X] \]

Regression draws the best straight line through a cloud of data points. Given two random variables X and Y, it answers: "if I know X, what's my best guess for Y?"

The slope b says "for each one-unit increase in X, Y goes up by b on average." The intercept a is the predicted Y when X = 0. The residual e is what the line missed — the gap between the actual Y and the predicted Y.

Why we care: in Lecture 5 we'll regress a stock's returns on the market's returns. The slope of that regression is beta — the central risk measure in CAPM. So this formula is the math behind every "beta" you've ever seen quoted on Yahoo Finance.

"Best" line means the one that minimizes the sum of squared residuals. Squared so positive and negative misses both count.

Pick a and b to minimize E[e²] = E[(Y − a − bX)²]. Take partial derivatives and set to zero.

Optimal intercept (first-order condition on a):

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial a} E[(Y - a - bX)^2] \;=\; -2 \, E[Y - a - bX] \;=\; 0 \]

So E[Y − a − bX] = 0, which means a = E[Y] − b E[X]. This forces the line to pass through the point of means (E[X], E[Y]) — and it means the residual e has zero expectation, E[e] = 0.

Optimal slope (first-order condition on b):

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial b} E[(Y - a - bX)^2] \;=\; -2 \, E[X(Y - a - bX)] \;=\; 0 \]

Substitute the optimal a back in. The condition becomes E[X·e] = 0, equivalent to σ[X, e] = 0. Expanding e = Y − a − bX and using a = E[Y] − bE[X]:

\[ E[X(Y - E[Y]) - b X (X - E[X])] \;=\; 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \sigma[Y, X] - b \, \sigma^2[X] \;=\; 0 \]

Solve for b:

\[ b \;=\; \frac{\sigma[Y, X]}{\sigma^2[X]} \]

Two properties that fall out for free: E[e] = 0 (residuals average to zero) and σ[X, e] = 0 (residuals are uncorrelated with the predictor). These are the two "orthogonality conditions" that define the OLS regression line.

Inputs to the portfolio choice problem

Given N risky assets plus a riskless asset, an investor needs three things to choose portfolio weights:

  1. E[Ri] for every asset (expected returns)
  2. σ[Ri] for every asset (standard deviations)
  3. σ[Ri, Rj] for every pair (covariances)

Under mean-variance assumptions, the investor cares only about the resulting portfolio's E[Rp] and σ[Rp]. Those two summary numbers are functions of the weights and the three inputs above. The whole rest of the course is variations on: "given these inputs and some assumption about preferences, how should I pick the weights?"

✦ remember this — Lecture 3 (Return Distribution)
  • Return distributions are summarized by E[R] (center) and σ (spread). Expected return = probability-weighted mean. Variance = probability-weighted squared deviation from the mean. Standard deviation = √variance, in the same units as returns.
  • Covariance measures joint movement. Same sign = move together; opposite = move opposite. Correlation = covariance / (σ1 × σ2), always in [−1, +1]. Correlation is the standardized, scale-free version of covariance.
  • Regression slope b = σ[Y, X] / σ²[X]. Intercept a = E[Y] − b × E[X]. The line passes through the point of means and minimizes the sum of squared residuals. When Y is a stock's return and X is the market's, b becomes beta in Lecture 5.
  • To build a portfolio you need three input sets: E[Ri] for each asset, σ[Ri] for each asset, and σ[Ri, Rj] for every pair. The rest of portfolio theory is "given these, pick the weights."

Lectures 3-4 - One risky + one riskless asset

The simplest portfolio problem: you have access to one risky asset (say, a stock) and the riskless asset (T-bills). How do you split your money?

Investor preferences, formalized

The mean-variance setup makes two assumptions about preferences:

  • All investors prefer more to less — for a given level of risk, higher expected return is always better.
  • Almost all investors are risk averse — for a given expected return, lower volatility is always better.

Stated more formally: for any risk-averse investor,

  • If two portfolios have the same E[R]: pick the one with lower σ.
  • If two portfolios have the same σ: pick the one with higher E[R].
  • If one has both higher E[R] AND higher σ: depends on how risk-averse the investor is.

Portfolio expected return — general formula

\[ E[R_p] \;=\; \omega_1 E[R_1] + \omega_2 E[R_2] + \cdots + \omega_N E[R_N] \]

The expected return on a portfolio is just the weighted average of the expected returns of the assets in it. Same logic as the portfolio return formula from Class 3 — but now we're averaging the expectations, not realized returns.

This works for any number of assets and any weight pattern (including margin and short selling). Clean.

From Lecture 2-3: Rp = Σ ωi Ri. Expectation is a linear operator, so it distributes over sums:

\[ E[R_p] \;=\; E\!\left[\sum_i \omega_i R_i\right] \;=\; \sum_i \omega_i \, E[R_i] \]

The weights ωi are known constants (decided at the start of the period), so they come out of the expectation. Clean and simple.

Standard deviation: one risky + one riskless

\[ \sigma[R_p] \;=\; |\omega_i| \cdot \sigma[R_i] \qquad \text{(one risky asset } i \text{ + riskless)} \]

The riskless asset has zero volatility by definition (it's "riskless"). So all the portfolio's volatility comes from the risky asset, scaled by how much you have invested in it.

If you put 60% in a stock with σ = 20%, the portfolio has σ = 0.6 × 20% = 12%. If you go on margin and have ω = 1.5 in the stock (financed by ω = −0.5 in the riskless), portfolio σ = 1.5 × 20% = 30%. Margin amplifies risk linearly.

Why the absolute value? Because risk is symmetric — going short a risky asset with ω = −0.5 gives you the same volatility as going long with ω = +0.5. Standard deviation doesn't care about direction; only magnitude.

For a two-asset portfolio (one risky + riskless), variance is:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \omega_i^2 \, \sigma^2[R_i] + \omega_f^2 \, \sigma^2[R_f] + 2 \omega_i \omega_f \, \sigma[R_i, R_f] \]

Now use two facts about the riskless asset:

  • σ²[Rf] = 0 (riskless = zero variance)
  • σ[Ri, Rf] = 0 (riskless return is constant, so it can't covary with anything)

The second and third terms vanish, leaving:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \omega_i^2 \, \sigma^2[R_i] \quad\Rightarrow\quad \sigma[R_p] \;=\; |\omega_i| \, \sigma[R_i] \]

The absolute value comes from taking the square root — variance is squared, so a negative weight squares to positive.

How (σ, E[R]) traces out as you vary the weight

Now we can plot it. As you change ω (your weight in the risky asset), the portfolio's (σ, E[R]) point moves along a specific path in mean-variance space. Using DFQ (E[R] = 11%, σ = 12.49%) and Rf = 6%:

Each weight ω on DFQ maps to one point in (σ, E[R]) space. Long positions trace out the upper red branch; short positions trace out the lower dashed branch. Both branches start at Rf (when ω = 0, you're 100% in T-bills).

Two things to notice:

  • The upper (red) branch has positive slope: more risk → more expected return. This is the "good" direction.
  • The lower (gray) branch has negative slope: more risk → less expected return. This is what shorting DFQ gets you, because DFQ has a positive expected risk premium (E[R] > Rf). Shorting it means you're betting against a winner — you bear the risk but lose the premium.

No risk-averse investor ever picks the lower branch. For any σ on the lower branch, there's a portfolio on the upper branch with the SAME σ and a HIGHER E[R]. That's Rule 1 in action (see next subsection).

Decision rules: how risk-averse investors choose

Lynch states two rules that any risk-averse investor follows when comparing two portfolios:

RuleIf you fix…… then preferWhy
Rule 1σ (volatility) constanthigher E[R]"More return for the same risk is always better."
Rule 2E[R] constantlower σ"Less risk for the same return is always better."

What happens when one portfolio has BOTH higher E[R] AND higher σ than the other? Then it depends — a more risk-averse investor might prefer the safer, lower-return one; a less risk-averse investor would take the higher-risk one. Risk aversion enters as a personal trade-off only in this case.

Rule 1, applied to the graph above: Take any σ value, say σ = 6.25%. The upper branch gives E[R] = 8.5% (ω = +0.5, long DFQ); the lower branch gives E[R] = 3.5% (ω = −0.5, short DFQ). Same volatility, but the upper-branch portfolio dominates. Every risk-averse investor picks it. The lower branch is eliminated entirely.

Rule 2 will become important in the next lecture when we have two risky assets — the portfolio possibility curve will bend, and you can find two portfolios with the same E[R] but different σ. Rule 2 then tells you to pick the leftward one (lower σ). On the current 1-risky-+-riskless setup, each E[R] uniquely maps to a single σ on each branch, so Rule 2 doesn't add anything new beyond Rule 1.

Where do risk-averse investors sit on the CAL?

After applying the rules, every risk-averse investor agrees: hold a portfolio on the upper (red) branch. But where on it? That depends on personal risk aversion.

Same CAL, different sitting positions. More risk averse → closer to Rf (more in T-bills, less in DFQ). Less risk averse → further along the line, possibly on margin (ω > 1).

A useful mental model:

  • Highly risk-averse (say, near retirement, can't afford big drawdowns): ω might be 0.2-0.4 in the risky asset, rest in T-bills. Low expected return but low volatility.
  • Moderately risk-averse: ω near 1.0 — basically all in the risky asset, no cash buffer.
  • Low risk aversion (young, long horizon, high tolerance for swings): ω > 1 — borrow at the riskless rate and lever into the risky asset for amplified expected return at the cost of amplified volatility.

The CAL is the menu; the investor's risk aversion picks the dish. Crucially, every risk-averse investor uses the SAME menu (same CAL), just orders different things from it. This is the seed of the two-fund separation theorem we'll see when we get to multiple risky assets.

Capital Allocation Line (CAL)

Slope of the CAL for risky asset i:

\[ \text{slope}[\text{CAL}_i] \;=\; \frac{|E[R_i] - R_f|}{\sigma[R_i]} \]

The Capital Allocation Line is the menu of all portfolios you can build by mixing one risky asset with the riskless asset. Plot it in (σ, E[R]) space and it's a straight line starting from the riskless asset (at σ = 0, E[R] = Rf) and passing through the risky asset (at σ = σ[Ri], E[R] = E[Ri]).

The slope of this line is excess return per unit of risk — how much extra expected return you earn for each unit of standard deviation you take on. This is sometimes called the reward-to-variability ratio. Higher slope = better deal.

Key insight: between two risky assets, a risk-averse investor will always prefer the one with the steeper CAL, regardless of how risk-averse they are. Risk preference only determines where on the CAL to sit, not which CAL to use.

If E[Ri] > Rf, you hold the risky asset with ω ≥ 0 (possibly on margin if ω > 1). If E[Ri] < Rf, you actually want to short the risky asset (ω < 0). Why hold something with expected return below the risk-free rate? You wouldn't — short it instead.

Take a portfolio of weight ω in risky asset i and (1 − ω) in the riskless asset. From the formulas above:

\[ E[R_p] \;=\; \omega E[R_i] + (1 - \omega) R_f \;=\; R_f + \omega (E[R_i] - R_f) \] \[ \sigma[R_p] \;=\; |\omega| \, \sigma[R_i] \]

Solve the second for |ω|: |ω| = σ[Rp] / σ[Ri]. Substituting:

\[ E[R_p] \;=\; R_f \pm \frac{\sigma[R_p]}{\sigma[R_i]} \cdot (E[R_i] - R_f) \]

(The ± reflects ω being positive or negative — long or short.) This is a straight line in (σ, E[R]) space passing through (0, Rf), with slope:

\[ \text{slope} \;=\; \frac{|E[R_i] - R_f|}{\sigma[R_i]} \]

Absolute value because by convention we plot σ on the positive horizontal axis even when ω is negative.

Choosing between two risky assets

If you have a choice of two risky assets A and B to combine with the riskless asset, which do you pick?

Answer: pick the one with the higher CAL slope. Doesn't matter how risk-averse you are. Why? Because any point on the lower-slope CAL is dominated by some point on the higher-slope CAL — the same σ, but more expected return.

Risk preference only affects the weight you choose on that CAL — i.e., how far up the line you sit. More risk-averse → lower weight on the risky asset, more in T-bills. Less risk-averse → higher weight, possibly on margin (ω > 1, levered into the risky asset).

Lecture 4 - Two risky assets

Now we add a second risky asset. With one risky + one riskless, the (σ, E[R]) menu was a straight line — easy. With two risky assets, the menu becomes a curve, and the curve's shape depends on the correlation between the two assets. This is where diversification benefit shows up as actual geometry.

Portfolio variance with two risky assets

Variance in terms of covariance

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \omega_1^2\,\sigma_1^2 + \omega_2^2\,\sigma_2^2 + 2\,\omega_1\omega_2\,\sigma_{1,2} \]

Variance in terms of correlation

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \omega_1^2\,\sigma_1^2 + \omega_2^2\,\sigma_2^2 + 2\,\omega_1\omega_2\,\rho_{1,2}\,\sigma_1\,\sigma_2 \]

The variance of a sum has three pieces: the variance of each asset weighted by ω², PLUS a "cross term" that depends on how the two assets co-move. If they move together (positive covariance), the cross term is positive and adds to portfolio variance. If they move opposite (negative covariance), the cross term subtracts — that's diversification doing its job.

The correlation form makes the role of ρ obvious: portfolio variance is highest when ρ = +1 (no diversification) and lowest when ρ = −1 (assets are perfect hedges). For any ρ < 1, you get some diversification benefit — portfolio σ falls below the weighted average of the individual σ's.

Start with the definition Rp = ω1R1 + ω2R2. Variance of a linear combination:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \mathrm{Var}(\omega_1 R_1 + \omega_2 R_2) \]

Use the general identity Var(aX + bY) = a²Var(X) + b²Var(Y) + 2ab\,Cov(X,Y):

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \omega_1^2\,\mathrm{Var}(R_1) + \omega_2^2\,\mathrm{Var}(R_2) + 2\omega_1\omega_2\,\mathrm{Cov}(R_1,R_2) \]

Rewrite with finance notation (σ² for variance, σi,j for covariance), giving the first form. For the second form, substitute σ1,2 = ρ1,2 σ1 σ2.

For a quick sanity check, take ρ = 1: σ²p = (ω1σ1 + ω2σ2)², so σp = ω1σ1 + ω2σ2 — exactly the weighted average of the individual σ's. Perfect positive correlation produces NO diversification. Any ρ < 1 produces some.

Portfolio possibility curve

As ω1 varies from 0 to 1 (no shorting), the pair (σ[Rp], E[Rp]) traces out a curve in mean-variance space. Its shape depends critically on the correlation between the two assets. Below: same DFQ and BML (E[R] = 11%, σ = 12.49% and E[R] = 10%, σ = 7.75%), but with the correlation varied. As ρ falls, the curve bows further to the left — that's diversification visible as geometry.

All curves connect the same two endpoints (BML and DFQ). The curve bows leftward as ρ decreases — that's diversification reducing risk for the same expected return. At ρ = −1, the curve hits σ = 0 (a perfect hedge can be built from two perfectly-negatively-correlated risky assets).

Minimum-variance portfolio (two risky)

Among all portfolios on the curve, the one with the smallest σ is the minimum-variance portfolio. Its weight in asset 1 is:

\[ \omega_1^{\,*} \;=\; \frac{\sigma_2^{\,2} - \sigma_{1,2}}{\sigma_1^{\,2} + \sigma_2^{\,2} - 2\sigma_{1,2}} \;=\; \frac{\sigma_2^{\,2} - \rho\,\sigma_1\,\sigma_2}{\sigma_1^{\,2} + \sigma_2^{\,2} - 2\rho\,\sigma_1\,\sigma_2} \]

The minimum-variance portfolio puts MORE weight in the asset with LOWER standard deviation, but the precise weight depends on covariance. When ρ < 0 (assets hedge each other), you sometimes hold a lot of the higher-vol asset because each "good" period for one offsets a "bad" period for the other.

When ρ = +1, the formula gives ω₁* = (σ₂² − σ₁σ₂)/(σ₁ − σ₂)² × σ₂/(σ₁−σ₂) — which can be negative (shorting the higher-vol asset to neutralize risk via the perfect linear relationship).

We want to choose ω ≡ ω1 (so ω2 = 1 − ω) to minimize:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p](\omega) \;=\; \omega^2 \sigma_1^2 + (1-\omega)^2 \sigma_2^2 + 2\omega(1-\omega) \sigma_{1,2} \]

Take the first-order condition (FOC) — differentiate with respect to ω and set to zero:

\[ \frac{d \sigma^2}{d\omega} \;=\; 2\omega \sigma_1^2 - 2(1-\omega)\sigma_2^2 + 2(1-2\omega)\sigma_{1,2} \;=\; 0 \]

Divide by 2 and expand:

\[ \omega \sigma_1^2 - \sigma_2^2 + \omega \sigma_2^2 + \sigma_{1,2} - 2\omega \sigma_{1,2} \;=\; 0 \]

Group terms in ω:

\[ \omega\,(\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\sigma_{1,2}) \;=\; \sigma_2^2 - \sigma_{1,2} \]

Solve:

\[ \omega_1^{\,*} \;=\; \frac{\sigma_2^2 - \sigma_{1,2}}{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\sigma_{1,2}} \]

Substitute σ1,2 = ρ σ1 σ2 for the correlation form. Second-order condition is automatically satisfied because σ²[Rp] is a convex parabola in ω: the coefficient of ω² is σ²1 + σ²2 − 2σ1,2 ≥ 0 (this is just Var(R1 − R2) ≥ 0).

Efficient vs inefficient region

The portfolio possibility curve has two halves:

  • Positive-sloped portion (above the minimum-variance portfolio): the efficient frontier. Higher E[R] for higher σ. Risk-averse investors only pick portfolios here.
  • Negative-sloped portion (below the minimum-variance portfolio): the inefficient region. For any point here, there's a point directly above it with the same σ but higher E[R] — so by Rule 1, nobody picks the lower point.

For the DFQ/BML curve at any ρ < 1, both halves exist. At ρ = +1 the curve is just a straight line so there's no minimum-variance portfolio interior to the segment (the lowest σ is just BML).

Lecture 4 - Two risky assets + a riskless asset

Now add T-bills to the mix. With two risky assets, the menu was a curve. Adding a riskless asset gives us back straight-line CALs, but now the CALs can be drawn from Rf to any portfolio on the curve. The question becomes: which CAL is steepest? That CAL passes through the so-called tangency portfolio T, and every risk-averse investor wants it.

The two-step decision

With two risky assets plus a riskless asset, the investor's choice splits cleanly into two steps:

  1. Find the best risky portfolio T — the mixture of the two risky assets whose CAL has the steepest slope.
  2. Pick how much of T vs. Rf to hold — this is the personal risk-aversion call, identical to the 1-risky-+-riskless problem from the previous lecture.

Crucially, Step 1's answer is the same for every risk-averse investor. The mix of the two risky assets is universal. Only Step 2 — how levered or conservative to be — depends on personal taste. This is the seed of two-fund separation that will later become CAPM.

Tangency portfolio T

The tangency portfolio is the risky-asset mixture that maximizes the Sharpe ratio (CAL slope). For two assets, the closed-form solution is:

\[ \omega_{1,T} \;=\; \frac{\mu_1\,\sigma_2^2 - \mu_2\,\sigma_{1,2}}{\mu_1\,\sigma_2^2 + \mu_2\,\sigma_1^2 - (\mu_1 + \mu_2)\,\sigma_{1,2}} \]

where μi = E[Ri] − Rf is the excess return of asset i.

The tangency portfolio is the unique risky portfolio whose CAL is steepest — the highest Sharpe ratio achievable in the (σ, E[R]) plane. Geometrically, the CAL through Rf just touches (is tangent to) the upper edge of the portfolio possibility curve at T.

The formula weights each asset by its excess return μ times the OTHER asset's variance, minus its excess return times the covariance. Intuitively: the more an asset contributes (high μ) and the less it covaries with the other (low σ1,2), the more weight it gets.

Note: tangency weights can be negative — when one asset has such a poor Sharpe ratio relative to the other that you'd rather short it to fund a bigger position in the other. That's not pathological; it's the math telling you the lower-Sharpe asset is just dominated.

The Sharpe ratio of a portfolio with weights (ω, 1−ω) is:

\[ S(\omega) \;=\; \frac{E[R_p] - R_f}{\sigma[R_p]} \;=\; \frac{\omega\,\mu_1 + (1-\omega)\,\mu_2}{\sqrt{\omega^2\sigma_1^2 + (1-\omega)^2\sigma_2^2 + 2\omega(1-\omega)\sigma_{1,2}}} \]

Maximizing S is equivalent to maximizing S² (assuming the numerator is positive). Let N(ω) = ωμ1 + (1−ω)μ2 and V(ω) = σ²[Rp]. We want:

\[ \frac{d}{d\omega} \frac{N^2}{V} \;=\; \frac{2 N N' V - N^2 V'}{V^2} \;=\; 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 2 N' V \;=\; N V' \]

Compute the derivatives:

\[ N' \;=\; \mu_1 - \mu_2 \qquad V' \;=\; 2\omega\sigma_1^2 - 2(1-\omega)\sigma_2^2 + 2(1-2\omega)\sigma_{1,2} \]

Substitute into 2N'V = NV':

\[ 2(\mu_1 - \mu_2)\,V(\omega) \;=\; [\,\omega\mu_1 + (1-\omega)\mu_2\,] \,\bigl[2\omega\sigma_1^2 - 2(1-\omega)\sigma_2^2 + 2(1-2\omega)\sigma_{1,2}\bigr] \]

This is messy but linear in ω after expansion. After distributing and collecting like terms in ω, the ω² and higher terms cancel (this is the magic of the Sharpe optimization), leaving a linear equation:

\[ \omega\,[\mu_1\sigma_2^2 + \mu_2\sigma_1^2 - (\mu_1+\mu_2)\sigma_{1,2}] \;=\; \mu_1\sigma_2^2 - \mu_2\sigma_{1,2} \]

Solve for ω:

\[ \omega_{1,T} \;=\; \frac{\mu_1\sigma_2^2 - \mu_2\sigma_{1,2}}{\mu_1\sigma_2^2 + \mu_2\sigma_1^2 - (\mu_1+\mu_2)\sigma_{1,2}} \]

Alternative geometric argument: The tangency point is where the line from Rf is tangent to the portfolio possibility curve. At that point, the slope of the curve equals the slope of the CAL. Computing both slopes and equating yields the same answer. This is the clean way to remember it: "the steepest CAL touches the curve exactly once."

Total portfolio weights with two risky assets + riskless

Once you know T (the risky-asset portfolio) and you've decided how much to put in T vs. Rf, your weight in each individual risky asset is the product:

\[ \omega_{i,p} \;=\; \omega_{i,T} \cdot \omega_{T,p} \]

where ωT,p is the weight of T in your total portfolio (decided by your risk aversion), and ωi,T is the weight of asset i within T (the same for everyone).

You hold T as a single "bundle" — its composition is fixed by the universal tangency-portfolio formula. Then you decide how much of the bundle to hold (vs. T-bills) based on personal taste. Your final weight in any one asset is just "fraction of total that's in T" × "fraction of T that's in this asset."

Example: ωT,p = 0.6 (60% of your money in T), and ωDFQ,T = 0.277 (T is 27.7% DFQ). Then your overall position in DFQ is 0.6 × 0.277 ≈ 16.6% of your total wealth.

Lectures 4-5 - N risky assets

Generalize to N risky assets. The key new objects are the N × N covariance matrix and the minimum-variance frontier. The diversification result becomes spectacular: as N grows, idiosyncratic risk gets washed out completely, leaving only systematic (average-covariance) risk. This is the engine of CAPM in the next lecture.

A matrix notation primer (so the rest is readable)

The next few formulas use matrix notation because writing out N-fold sums is unwieldy. Three quick reminders:

  • \(\mathbf{w}\) is an N-tall column vector of weights: \(\mathbf{w} = (\omega_1, \omega_2, \ldots, \omega_N)^{\top}\). The superscript \(\top\) means "transpose" — turn the column into a row.
  • \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) (capital sigma) is the \(N \times N\) covariance matrix. The entry in row \(i\), column \(j\) is \(\Sigma_{i,j} = \sigma_{i,j}\) (covariance of asset \(i\) with asset \(j\)). Diagonal entries \(\Sigma_{i,i} = \sigma_i^2\) are variances. The matrix is symmetric (\(\sigma_{i,j} = \sigma_{j,i}\)).
  • \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\) is a single number (a "quadratic form") — multiply weights through both sides of the matrix and add up. It expands to \(\sum_{i,j} \omega_i\,\sigma_{i,j}\,\omega_j\), which is exactly the portfolio variance double-sum. Picture: each cell \((i, j)\) contributes \(\omega_i \cdot \sigma_{i,j} \cdot \omega_j\) to the total.

Similarly, \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} = \omega_1\mu_1 + \omega_2\mu_2 + \cdots = E[R_p]\) (portfolio expected return = weighted average), and \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = \omega_1 + \omega_2 + \cdots = 1\) (weights sum to one). Matrix notation is just a shorthand — every step below can be rewritten with summation signs if you prefer.

Portfolio variance with N assets

Double-sum form:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \omega_i\,\omega_j\,\sigma_{i,j} \]

Matrix form:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\, \mathbf{w} \qquad \text{with } \mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1 \]

where \(\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^N\) is the weight vector, \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) is the \(N \times N\) covariance matrix (\(\Sigma_{i,j} = \sigma_{i,j}\), with diagonal entries \(\Sigma_{i,i} = \sigma_i^2\)), and \(\mathbf{1}\) is the vector of ones.

For N assets, portfolio variance has \(N^2\) terms total. \(N\) of them are diagonal (variance × weight²); the other \(N(N-1)\) are off-diagonal (covariance × cross weights). Picture an N×N grid: every cell contributes to the total.

The matrix form \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\) is the same expression written compactly. It's also the standard form used in mean-variance optimization libraries — once you can compute \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) and pick \(\mathbf{w}\), the variance just falls out as a quadratic form.

As N grows, the off-diagonal terms (covariances) overwhelm the diagonal terms (variances) — there are way more of them. This is what drives the diversification result below.

Start with the definition. Portfolio return is \(R_p = \sum_i \omega_i R_i\). Variance of a linear combination:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \mathrm{Var}\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^N \omega_i R_i\right) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \omega_i \omega_j \,\mathrm{Cov}(R_i, R_j) \;=\; \sum_{i,j} \omega_i \omega_j \sigma_{i,j} \]

using bilinearity of covariance, with \(\sigma_{i,i} = \sigma_i^2\).

Matrix form: stack weights and covariances. Let \(\mathbf{w} = (\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_N)^{\top}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = [\sigma_{i,j}]\). Then:

\[ \mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\,\mathbf{w} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \omega_i\, \Sigma_{i,j}\, \omega_j \;=\; \sigma^2[R_p] \]

Both forms are equivalent. The matrix form is more compact and makes optimization easier (the Lagrangian below uses it directly). One useful identity — split \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) into diagonal (variances) and off-diagonal (covariances) pieces:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_i \omega_i^2 \sigma_i^2}_{\text{diagonal (own-variance) terms}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_{i \ne j} \omega_i \omega_j \sigma_{i,j}}_{\text{off-diagonal (covariance) terms}} \]

Equal-weighted portfolio: the diversification result

The cleanest way to see how diversification works is to consider an equal-weighted portfolio of N assets — each ωi = 1/N — and study how its variance behaves as N grows.

Equal-weighted portfolio variance:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \frac{1}{N} \cdot \overline{\sigma^2} \;+\; \left(1 - \frac{1}{N}\right) \cdot \overline{\sigma_{i,j}} \]

where \(\overline{\sigma^2}\) is the average variance of the N assets and \(\overline{\sigma_{i,j}}\) is the average pairwise covariance.

Equal-weighting splits portfolio variance into two pieces:

  • \((1/N) \cdot \overline{\sigma^2}\) — the "own-asset" piece. Shrinks toward zero as you add more assets. Represents idiosyncratic / firm-specific / diversifiable risk.
  • \((1 - 1/N) \cdot \overline{\sigma_{i,j}}\) — the "co-movement" piece. As N grows, this approaches the average covariance itself. Represents systematic / market / undiversifiable risk.

The first term goes to 0 as \(N \to \infty\). The second term goes to \(\overline{\sigma_{i,j}}\):

\[ \lim_{N \to \infty} \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \overline{\sigma_{i,j}} \]

Portfolio variance can NEVER fall below the average pairwise covariance, no matter how many assets you add. That floor is the systematic risk — the part you can't diversify away. CAPM is built on this distinction.

Set \(\omega_i = 1/N\) for all \(i\). Substitute into the double sum:

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \frac{1}{N} \cdot \frac{1}{N} \cdot \sigma_{i,j} \;=\; \frac{1}{N^2} \sum_{i,j} \sigma_{i,j} \]

Split the double sum into diagonal (\(i = j\)) and off-diagonal (\(i \ne j\)) parts:

\[ \sum_{i,j} \sigma_{i,j} \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^N \sigma_{i,i}}_{\text{diagonal}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_{i \ne j} \sigma_{i,j}}_{\text{off-diagonal}} \;=\; N\,\overline{\sigma^2} + N(N-1)\,\overline{\sigma_{i,j}} \]

There are \(N\) diagonal entries averaging to \(\overline{\sigma^2}\) and \(N(N-1)\) off-diagonal entries averaging to \(\overline{\sigma_{i,j}}\). Divide by \(N^2\):

\[ \sigma^2[R_p] \;=\; \frac{N\,\overline{\sigma^2}}{N^2} + \frac{N(N-1)\,\overline{\sigma_{i,j}}}{N^2} \;=\; \frac{\overline{\sigma^2}}{N} + \frac{N-1}{N}\,\overline{\sigma_{i,j}} \]

which is the same as \((1/N)\,\overline{\sigma^2} + (1 - 1/N)\,\overline{\sigma_{i,j}}\). ∎

Special cases that make the result tangible

Let every asset have the same variance \(\sigma^2\). Then \(\overline{\sigma^2} = \sigma^2\), and the average covariance simplifies to \(\overline{\sigma_{i,j}} = \bar{\rho}\,\sigma^2\), where \(\bar{\rho}\) is the average pairwise correlation.

\(\bar{\rho}\)\(\sigma^2[R_p]\)\(\sigma[R_p]\) as \(N \to \infty\)Meaning
\(0\)\(\sigma^2 / N\)\(0\)Full diversification: vol vanishes.
\(0.5\)\(\sigma^2 \bigl(\tfrac{1}{N} + (1 - \tfrac{1}{N}) \cdot 0.5\bigr)\)\(\sigma\sqrt{0.5} \approx 0.71\,\sigma\)Half the firm vol stays as systematic risk.
\(1\)\(\sigma^2\)\(\sigma\)No diversification: assets all move identically.

So \(\bar{\rho}\) sets the floor. With \(\bar{\rho} = 0.5\) and 100 assets, the equal-weighted portfolio still has roughly 70% of an individual asset's σ. Even infinite diversification cannot reduce risk below \(\sqrt{\bar{\rho}}\,\sigma\).

Minimum-variance frontier (MVF) for N assets

For two assets, the portfolio possibility curve was a single curve. For N assets, the set of achievable (σ, E[R]) points is an entire region in the plane. The leftmost boundary of this region — the lowest σ at each E[R] — is the minimum-variance frontier.

MVF construction (for each target μ*):

\[ \min_{\mathbf{w}} \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\, \mathbf{w} \quad \text{subject to} \quad \mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\mu} = \mu^* \;,\; \mathbf{w}^{\top} \mathbf{1} = 1 \]

The solution traces out a parabola in (σ², μ*) space, equivalently a hyperbola in (σ, μ*) space. The efficient frontier is the upper half of the hyperbola — the lowest σ for each ACHIEVABLE expected return.

For each target expected return μ*, find the portfolio that achieves it with the lowest possible σ. Plot the result. The locus of these "minimum σ for given E[R]" points is the MVF.

Adding more risky assets always shifts the MVF LEFT (or, in the degenerate case, leaves it unchanged) — because old portfolios are still possible, plus new mixtures become available. More choices ⟹ never worse, often better.

The "efficient frontier" (upper half) is the only part risk-averse investors care about. The lower half (inefficient) is dominated by the upper half — same σ, lower E[R].

Step 1: set up the Lagrangian. We're minimizing \(\tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\) (the \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) is just for clean algebra — doesn't change the optimum). Subtract a multiplier \(\lambda_k\) times each constraint:

\[ \mathcal{L} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w} - \lambda_1 (\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} - \mu^*) - \lambda_2 (\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} - 1) \]

Step 2: take the gradient with respect to w and set to zero. Two matrix-calculus facts you need:

  • \(\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\bigl(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{w}\bigr) = 2\mathbf{A}\mathbf{w}\) when \(\mathbf{A}\) is symmetric (which \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) always is). So \(\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\bigr) = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}\).
  • \(\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\bigl(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{v}\bigr) = \mathbf{v}\) for any constant vector \(\mathbf{v}\).
\[ \nabla_{\mathbf{w}} \mathcal{L} \;=\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w} - \lambda_1 \boldsymbol{\mu} - \lambda_2 \mathbf{1} \;=\; \mathbf{0} \]

Solve for \(\mathbf{w}\) by left-multiplying with \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\):

\[ \mathbf{w}^{\,*} \;=\; \lambda_1 \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} + \lambda_2 \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \]

Note what this means: the optimal portfolio is a linear combination of two fixed vectors — \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}\) (a portfolio biased toward high-expected-return assets after adjusting for correlations) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}\) (the minimum-variance portfolio direction). The Lagrange multipliers \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\) just control the mix. This is already the two-fund spanning property: any MVF portfolio is a mixture of just two reference portfolios.

Step 3: pin down \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\) using the two constraints. Plug \(\mathbf{w}^{*}\) back into \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} = \mu^*\) and \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1\):

\[ \lambda_1\,\boldsymbol{\mu}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} + \lambda_2\,\boldsymbol{\mu}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \;=\; \mu^* \] \[ \lambda_1\,\mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} + \lambda_2\,\mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \;=\; 1 \]

Step 4: name the constants that keep appearing. Three scalars depend only on \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) (NOT on \(\mu^*\)):

\[ A \;\equiv\; \boldsymbol{\mu}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} \qquad B \;\equiv\; \boldsymbol{\mu}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \qquad C \;\equiv\; \mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \qquad D \;\equiv\; AC - B^2 \]

What they mean physically:

  • \(A\) = "weighted size of the expected-return vector" after \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\) adjusts for correlations. Large \(A\) → big return premia available.
  • \(B\) = the "alignment" between \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) and \(\mathbf{1}\) through \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\). Related to the global mean return.
  • \(C\) = "ease of constructing portfolios" — inverse of the global minimum variance (since \(\sigma^2_{\min} = 1/C\), as we'll show).
  • \(D = AC - B^2\). Always positive (by Cauchy-Schwarz). Sets the "curvature" of the MVF hyperbola.

The two constraint equations become a 2×2 linear system in \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\):

\[ \begin{cases} A\,\lambda_1 + B\,\lambda_2 = \mu^* \\ B\,\lambda_1 + C\,\lambda_2 = 1 \end{cases} \]

Solve (standard 2×2):

\[ \lambda_1 \;=\; \frac{C\,\mu^* - B}{D} \qquad \lambda_2 \;=\; \frac{A - B\,\mu^*}{D} \]

Step 5: write down the optimal portfolio.

\[ \mathbf{w}^{\,*}(\mu^*) \;=\; \frac{C\mu^* - B}{D}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} \;+\; \frac{A - B\mu^*}{D}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1} \]

The MVF as a whole is parameterized by \(\mu^*\): pick any target expected return, plug in, get the weights.

Step 6: variance at the optimum. Use the FOC: \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}^{*} = \lambda_1\boldsymbol{\mu} + \lambda_2\mathbf{1}\). So:

\[ \sigma^2[\mathbf{w}^{\,*}] \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{*\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}^{*} \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{*\top}(\lambda_1\boldsymbol{\mu} + \lambda_2\mathbf{1}) \;=\; \lambda_1\,\mu^* + \lambda_2 \]

Plug in the expressions for \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\):

\[ \sigma^2[\mathbf{w}^{\,*}] \;=\; \frac{C\,(\mu^*)^2 - 2B\,\mu^* + A}{D} \]

This is a parabola in \((\sigma^2, \mu^*)\) coordinates — equivalently a hyperbola in \((\sigma, \mu^*)\) coordinates. That hyperbola is the MVF.

Step 7: the global minimum-variance portfolio. Minimize variance over \(\mu^*\):

\[ \frac{d\sigma^2}{d\mu^*} \;=\; \frac{2C\mu^* - 2B}{D} \;=\; 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \mu^*_{\min} \;=\; \frac{B}{C} \]

At this target, \(\lambda_1 = 0\), so the portfolio is purely in the \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}\) direction:

\[ \mathbf{w}^{\,*}_{\min} \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}} \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}}{C} \]

And its variance: \(\sigma^2_{\min} = 1/C\). So \(C\) really does measure "ease of constructing low-variance portfolios" — bigger \(C\) → smaller global minimum variance.

Two facts about the MVF:

  • Adding risky assets only helps. Adding asset N+1 to a set of N assets cannot move the MVF to the right (a worse achievable set), and usually moves it to the left. More assets → more diversification possibilities → more efficient choices.
  • Each individual asset lies strictly inside the MVF (except in pathological cases). The whole point of constructing the MVF is that nobody holds a single asset as their portfolio — there's always a multi-asset combination with the same E[R] and lower σ.

Lectures 4-5 - N risky assets + a riskless asset

Add T-bills. The same logic from the 2-risky-+-riskless case scales up: among all portfolios on the MVF, find the one whose CAL has the steepest slope (highest Sharpe ratio). That's the tangency portfolio T. The new efficient frontier is the straight CAL through T — every risk-averse investor wants to be on it.

Tangency portfolio T — N-asset formula

\[ \mathbf{w}_T \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{\mathbf{1}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}} \]

where \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} = \boldsymbol{\mu} - R_f \cdot \mathbf{1}\) is the vector of excess expected returns (each entry is \(E[R_i] - R_f\)).

The numerator \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\) is the "raw" weighting: each asset is weighted by its excess return, then "scrubbed" through the inverse covariance matrix so that highly-correlated assets share weight rather than double-counting. The denominator is the sum of those raw weights — it normalizes so the final weights sum to 1.

Same intuition as the 2-asset case: high excess return ⇒ more weight; high covariance with the rest of the portfolio ⇒ less weight (because that's redundant exposure).

\(\mathbf{w}_T\) can have negative entries — some assets are shorted in the tangency portfolio if they have negative excess return or correlate the wrong way. That's normal.

Step 1: state the problem. Choose the risky-asset weights \(\mathbf{w}\) to maximize the Sharpe ratio:

\[ \max_{\mathbf{w}} \;\; \frac{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} - R_f}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}} \quad \text{subject to } \mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1 \]

The constraint forces the weights to sum to 1 — i.e., 100% of the risky-asset bucket goes into these N assets.

Step 2: simplify the numerator using the constraint. Because \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1\), you can write \(R_f = R_f \,\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{w}^{\top}(R_f \mathbf{1})\). Then:

\[ \mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} - R_f \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu} - \mathbf{w}^{\top}(R_f\mathbf{1}) \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{\top}(\boldsymbol{\mu} - R_f\mathbf{1}) \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \]

So we're maximizing:

\[ S(\mathbf{w}) \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}} \]

Step 3: use the scale-invariance trick. If you rescale \(\mathbf{w} \to c\mathbf{w}\) for any positive constant \(c\), both the numerator (linear in \(\mathbf{w}\)) and the denominator (linear after the square root) scale by \(c\). So \(S\) is unchanged. This means we can drop the constraint \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1\) entirely — solve the unconstrained problem to find the right DIRECTION of \(\mathbf{w}\), then rescale at the end so the weights sum to 1.

Step 4: take the gradient. Use the quotient rule and the matrix identities:

  • \(\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\bigl(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\bigr) = \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\)
  • \(\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w} \,/\, \sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}\)
\[ \nabla_{\mathbf{w}} S \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}}\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;-\; \frac{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w})^{3/2}}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w} \;=\; \mathbf{0} \]

Step 5: rearrange to isolate \(\mathbf{w}\). Multiply through by \((\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w})^{3/2}\) and collect terms:

\[ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}}{\mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}}_{\text{positive scalar }k}\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \]

The scalar \(k\) on the right depends on \(\mathbf{w}\), but by scale-invariance we only care about direction. Multiply both sides by \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\):

\[ \mathbf{w} \;=\; k \,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \mathbf{w} \;\propto\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \]

Step 6: normalize so weights sum to 1. Pick the constant \(k\) so that \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = 1\). If \(\mathbf{w} = k\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\), then \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{1} = k \cdot \mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\); setting this equal to 1 gives \(k = 1 \,/\, \mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\). So:

\[ \mathbf{w}_T \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{\mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}} \]

This is the tangency portfolio formula. Conceptually the answer is just "\(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\), normalized."

What does \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\) actually do? Think of \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\) as "excess return per unit of risk exposure, adjusted for correlations." If two assets are highly correlated, \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\) partially cancels them out — you don't want to double-count similar risks. If one asset has a high Sharpe but covaries with everything else, \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\) shrinks its weight. The result is the unique direction in N-asset space that maximizes the reward-per-risk trade-off.

Sanity check: 2 assets, by matrix vs by closed form

The matrix formula has to reduce to the 2-asset closed form we derived earlier. Let's verify. For 2 assets:

\[ \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \mu_1 \\ \mu_2 \end{pmatrix} \qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_1^2 & \sigma_{1,2} \\ \sigma_{1,2} & \sigma_2^2 \end{pmatrix} \qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 - \sigma_{1,2}^2} \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_2^2 & -\sigma_{1,2} \\ -\sigma_{1,2} & \sigma_1^2 \end{pmatrix} \]

(Standard 2×2 inverse: swap diagonal, negate off-diagonal, divide by determinant.)

Multiply Σ⁻¹ by μexcess:

\[ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 - \sigma_{1,2}^2} \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_2^2 \mu_1 - \sigma_{1,2}\mu_2 \\ -\sigma_{1,2}\mu_1 + \sigma_1^2 \mu_2 \end{pmatrix} \]

Sum the two components (that's 1TΣ⁻¹μexcess):

\[ \mathbf{1}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;=\; \frac{\mu_1 \sigma_2^2 + \mu_2 \sigma_1^2 - (\mu_1 + \mu_2)\,\sigma_{1,2}}{\sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 - \sigma_{1,2}^2} \]

Divide the first component of Σ⁻¹μexcess by this sum — the determinant cancels — and you get the first weight:

\[ \omega_{1,T} \;=\; \frac{\mu_1\,\sigma_2^2 - \mu_2\,\sigma_{1,2}}{\mu_1\,\sigma_2^2 + \mu_2\,\sigma_1^2 - (\mu_1 + \mu_2)\,\sigma_{1,2}} \]

This matches exactly the closed-form 2-asset tangency formula derived earlier. ✓ The matrix machinery generalizes the same logic to N assets, but the answer for N = 2 is recoverable in a few lines of algebra.

The new efficient frontier is the CAL through T

With \(R_f\) available, an investor can hold ANY combination of \(T\) and \(R_f\). The set of achievable portfolios is the entire straight line from \(R_f\) through \(T\) (and beyond, via margin). Its slope is the maximum Sharpe ratio:

\[ \text{slope}_{\text{CAL through T}} \;=\; \frac{E[R_T] - R_f}{\sigma_T} \;=\; \sqrt{\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}^{\top}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}} \]

This is the highest Sharpe ratio achievable from any portfolio of the \(N\) risky assets and \(R_f\).

The CAL slope is just the Sharpe ratio at the tangency portfolio. Write \(\mathbf{w}_T = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\,/\,B\) where \(B = \mathbf{1}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\).

Excess return at \(T\):

\[ \mathbf{w}_T^{\top}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;=\; \frac{(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}})^{\top}}{B}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}} \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{B} \;=\; \frac{A}{B} \]

where \(A = \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}\). Variance at \(T\):

\[ \mathbf{w}_T^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}_T \;=\; \frac{(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}})^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{B^2} \;=\; \frac{\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}}{B^2} \;=\; \frac{A}{B^2} \]

So \(\sigma_T = \sqrt{A}\,/\,|B|\). Sharpe ratio:

\[ \text{slope} \;=\; \frac{A/B}{\sqrt{A}/|B|} \;=\; \mathrm{sgn}(B) \cdot \sqrt{A} \;=\; \sqrt{\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}^{\top}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{excess}}} \]

(assuming \(B > 0\), the standard case where the average excess return is positive).

Two-fund separation theorem

Two-fund separation: with N risky assets and a riskless asset, every risk-averse mean-variance investor holds the same tangency portfolio T, combined with Rf in a proportion that depends on their personal risk aversion.

This is one of the most beautiful results in finance. It says that no matter how risk-averse you are, the composition of your risky-asset holdings should be identical to everyone else's — the only thing that varies is how much risky vs. riskless you hold. Marginal investors with different preferences agree completely on which risky portfolio to hold.

Why it matters:

  • It massively simplifies portfolio choice. The investor doesn't need to think about which assets to hold — they just hold T (plus T-bills). The asset-selection problem and the risk-level problem decouple.
  • It's the seed of CAPM. In the next lecture: under additional assumptions, T must equal the market portfolio M (every asset value-weighted). Two-fund separation + market clearing = CAPM.

Picture: MVF with and without a riskless asset

Without Rf, the efficient frontier is the upper half of the MVF (gray curve). With Rf, the new efficient frontier is the straight CAL through T (red line), which dominates almost the entire MVF — same risk, higher expected return.

Note: the CAL touches the MVF curve at exactly one point (T) and lies above it everywhere else. That's why T is called the tangency portfolio. Past T, you can't go up the MVF; you can only ride the straight line by leveraging T (buying T on margin, i.e., ωT,p > 1).

Adding more assets shifts the MVF left

If you expand the universe of available risky assets (e.g., add international stocks to a US-only portfolio, or add bonds to a stock portfolio), three things happen:

  1. The MVF shifts to the LEFT — every achievable expected return now has a lower-σ portfolio backing it.
  2. The new tangency portfolio T' has a STEEPER CAL (higher Sharpe ratio) — diversification gives you a better risk-return menu.
  3. EVERY risk-averse investor benefits (assuming no transaction costs). Even if they don't want to hold the new asset directly, the new tangency portfolio is better than the old one.

This is the formal answer to "why bother diversifying internationally?" or "why add bonds to a stock portfolio?" — the menu of (σ, E[R]) combinations strictly improves.

✦ remember this — Lectures 4-5 (N risky ± Rf)
  • Portfolio variance in matrix form: σ²[Rp] = wTΣw. The covariance matrix Σ is N × N; portfolio weights are an N-vector.
  • Diversification theorem (equal-weighted): σ²[Rp] = σ̄²/N + (1 − 1/N) σ̄i,j. As N → ∞, the first term (idiosyncratic risk) vanishes; the second (systematic risk, the average covariance) remains. You can't diversify below the floor of average covariance.

INT 1 - TVM × annuity × starting age

Retirement savings target

You're 30 and want $2,000,000 in your retirement account at age 65. You'll save the same dollar amount at the end of every month between now and then, in an account earning 7% APR with monthly compounding. (a) How much do you need to save per month? (b) If you wait until age 35 to start, how much more per month do you need? Comment on the cost of delay.

Solution

Setup. Per-period rate r = 0.07 / 12 ≈ 0.005833. This is a future-value annuity problem: equal monthly payments C, target FV = $2M at the final payment date.

(a) Starting at 30: N = 35 × 12 = 420 payments.

FVAF(r, N) = [(1 + r)^N − 1] / r = [(1.005833)^420 − 1] / 0.005833 ≈ [11.524 − 1] / 0.005833 ≈ 1,804.7 C = FV / FVAF = 2,000,000 / 1,804.7 ≈ $1,108 per month

(b) Starting at 35: N = 30 × 12 = 360 payments.

FVAF(r, 360) = [(1.005833)^360 − 1] / 0.005833 ≈ [8.117 − 1] / 0.005833 ≈ 1,219.97 C = 2,000,000 / 1,219.97 ≈ $1,640 per month

Cost of delay: $1,640 − $1,108 = ~$532 more per month, every month for 30 years. That's 48% more savings to reach the same target. Time and compounding do almost all the work in long-horizon savings - the extra 5 years at the start matter way more than the 5 years cut off the end.
