Key takeaways (through Class 5)

The whole first month in one screen — the spine is A = L + E → dual effect → accrual timing → adjust at period-end.

  1. The accounting equation always holds. Assets = Liabilities + Stockholders' Equity. Every transaction touches at least two accounts (dual effect), and the equation stays in balance after every one.
  2. Four statements, two time frames. The balance sheet is a snapshot on a day; the income statement, statement of stockholders' equity, and statement of cash flows describe a period. Footnotes are an integral part of the statements.
  3. The statements articulate. Net income flows into retained earnings (via the SSE); ending cash on the cash flow statement is the cash on the ending balance sheet; ending equity lands on the balance sheet.
  4. Debit = left, credit = right — nothing more. Assets sit on the left of the equation, so debits increase them; liabilities and equity sit on the right, so credits increase those. Revenues increase with credits, expenses increase with debits (both through retained earnings).
  5. No entry for exchanges of promises. Signing a contract or hiring a president is not a transaction until goods, services, or cash change hands.
  6. Accrual accounting is the whole game. Revenue is recorded when earned (transfer of control), expenses when incurred to generate that revenue (matching) — regardless of when cash moves. Cash-basis and accrual-basis income can differ wildly on the same facts.
  7. Revenue recognition = the five-step model. Contract → performance obligations → transaction price → allocate → recognize when (or as) each obligation is satisfied.
  8. Cash timing creates four adjustment types. Cash before: deferred (prepaid) expense, deferred (unearned) revenue. Cash after: accrued expense, accrued revenue. Every adjusting entry moves one of these into the right period — and never touches Cash.
  9. Contra accounts preserve history. Depreciation accumulates in Accumulated Depreciation (a credit-balance contra-asset) so the balance sheet shows both cost and net book value = cost − accumulated depreciation.
  10. Temporary accounts close to retained earnings. Revenues, expenses, and dividends zero out at period-end so the next period starts fresh; balance sheet accounts carry forward.
  11. Adjustments are where discretion lives. Estimates (useful lives, accruals) require judgment, managers know the target, and the distribution of analyst forecast errors shows firms "meet or just beat" — earnings quality is a management choice.
  12. Ratios so far: current ratio = current assets / current liabilities (liquidity cushion; too high can mean inefficiency); margins = any income statement line / net sales (gross, operating, net — each isolates one layer of cost, and which layer leaks is the diagnosis); EPS = net income / average shares outstanding; quality of income = CFO / NI (how much profit is backed by cash).
  13. The cash flow statement is accrual accounting run in reverse. Indirect method: start from net income, add back noncash charges (depreciation), back out investing items (gains/losses), then undo every working-capital timing wedge — asset up, cash down; liability up, cash up. The three sections' signs tell a firm's life-cycle story (−/−/+ startup → +/−/− mature).
bookkeeping vocab

The machinery terms, in the order the accounting cycle uses them. On wide screens this stays pinned on the right while you scroll.

the recording machinery
account
Standardized format accumulating the dollar effect of transactions on one statement item (Cash, A/P, …).
chart of accounts
The company's list of all account titles, ordered assets → liabilities → equity → revenues → expenses.
journal entry
One transaction's dual effect in debits-equal-credits format; debits listed first, credits indented below.
compound entry
A journal entry touching more than two accounts (e.g., buy equipment with cash + a note).
general journal
Chronological log of all journal entries — the diary.
posting
Transferring journal-entry amounts into each account's ledger record.
general ledger
The record of every account's effects and running balance — the filing cabinet the journal feeds.
T-account
Ledger account shorthand: debits on the left, credits on the right, balance on the normal-balance side.
normal balance
The side an account increases on — debit for assets/expenses, credit for liabilities/equity/revenues.
period-end
trial balance
List of every account and its balance in two columns, proving total debits = total credits. Catches arithmetic errors only — not wrong-account postings or omitted transactions.
adjusting entries (AJEs)
End-of-period entries recording implicit transactions; always ≥1 income-statement + ≥1 balance-sheet account, never Cash.
adjusted trial balance
Trial balance re-run after AJEs — the version the statements are built from.
closing entries
Zero out temporary accounts (revenues, expenses, dividends) into Retained Earnings at period-end.
post-closing trial balance
Final check: debits = credits and all temporary accounts read zero.
temporary vs. permanent
Temporary = income statement accounts + dividends (reset each period). Permanent = balance sheet accounts (carry forward).
contra-account
Offset account glued to a parent with the opposite balance — Accumulated Depreciation (vs. PPE), Treasury Stock (vs. equity).
revenue & expense timing
cash basis
Record revenue when cash arrives, expense when cash leaves. Not GAAP.
accrual accounting
Record revenue when earned, expense when incurred, regardless of cash timing. Required by GAAP.
revenue recognition
Recognize when control of goods/services transfers to the customer, at the amount expected (five-step model).
matching principle
Expenses go in the same period as the revenues they helped generate.
deferral
Cash first, income statement later — prepaid expense (asset), unearned revenue (liability).
accrual (the entry type)
Income statement first, cash later — accrued revenue (receivable), accrued expense (payable).
unearned revenue
Liability for cash collected before delivery (deposits, gift cards, subscriptions).
prepaid expense
Asset for cash paid before consumption (rent, insurance, supplies).
the accounting cycle
during the period
1. analyze transactions → 2. record journal entries → 3. post to ledger.
at period-end
4. unadjusted trial balance → 5. adjusting entries → 6. adjusted trial balance → 7. statements (I/S → SSE → B/S, in that order) → 8. close.

The four financial statements · Ch. 1 · Class 1 (Jul 1)

Accounting is "the process of identifying, measuring and communicating financial information about an entity to permit informed judgements and decisions by users of the information." Financial accounting is the communicating half, aimed at external users — and the key insight about users: each one (investor, creditor, analyst, regulator, competitor, tax authority) will have a different motive, objective, and bias for looking at the same numbers.

Why it matters — accounting scandals are a global phenomenon. WorldCom hid falling profitability by capitalizing expenses (recording ~$3B of 2001 expenses as investments), reporting a $1.4B profit instead of a net loss; it went bankrupt a month after the auditor's discovery. Livedoor's CEO testified "I never studied accounting… a management book I read said to leave that to specialists" — and went to jail. The recurring lesson: managers can't outsource understanding the numbers, and neither can users.

Two questions, four statements

StatementQuestion it answersTime frame
Balance sheet (statement of financial position)What is the financial condition?A point in time (a given day)
Income statement (P&L)How well did it do?A period
Statement of stockholders' equityHow did owners' claims change?A period
Statement of cash flowsWhere did cash come from and go?A period

Footnotes are an integral part of the financial statements — the numbers alone are never the whole story.

The fundamental accounting equation (investments = financing)

\[ \text{Assets} = \text{Liabilities} + \text{Stockholders' Equity} \]

Think of buying a house: the house (asset) is paid for with a mortgage (liability) plus your down payment (equity). A company is the same picture at scale — everything it owns was financed by someone, either creditors or owners. That's why the equation can never break: you can't own something nobody paid for.

Assets are the economic resources; liabilities are the creditors' claims on them; equity is whatever claim is left over for the owners after creditors are satisfied (which is why equity is defined as "residual interest": E = A − L).

Every transaction preserves the equality because it always touches two sides in an offsetting way — the "dual effect" that makes double-entry bookkeeping self-checking.

Expand equity into its components. Equity comes from two sources: capital paid in by owners and profits kept in the business:

\[ A = L + (\text{Paid-in Capital} + \text{Retained Earnings}^{*}) \]

Retained earnings is cumulative income never paid out (* = accumulated over all years):

\[ \text{Retained Earnings}^{*} = \text{Net Income}^{*} - \text{Dividends}^{*} \]

And net income is revenues minus expenses, so the fully expanded equation is:

\[ A = L + \text{Paid-in Capital} + \text{Rev}^{*} - \text{Exp}^{*} - \text{Div}^{*} \]

This expansion is the entire logic of the course: the income statement is a magnifying glass on one term of the balance sheet equation, and the statement of stockholders' equity is the bridge between them. It's also where debit/credit rules for revenues and expenses come from (Class 3).

Income statement equation

\[ \text{Revenues} - \text{Expenses} = \text{Net Income (or Loss)} \]

Revenues are increases in assets from delivering goods or services to customers; expenses are the costs incurred to generate those revenues. Net income is what's left — the period's scorecard. It is not cash: a sale on credit is revenue today, cash later.

Retained earnings roll-forward (the statement of stockholders' equity)

\[ RE_{\text{end}} = RE_{\text{beg}} + \text{Net Income} - \text{Dividends} \]

Retained earnings is a savings account the business keeps for its owners: each period's profit gets deposited, each dividend is a withdrawal. It accumulates over the company's whole life — which is why a young profitable company that pays no dividends builds retained earnings fast, and why RE is not a pile of cash (the profits are usually reinvested in inventory, equipment, etc.).

Statement of cash flows structure

\[ \Delta \text{Cash} = CFO + CFI + CFF \]

Every dollar that moved is sorted into three buckets by why it moved. Beginning cash plus the three buckets equals ending cash on the balance sheet.

CFO — cash from operating activities. The cash aspects of the day-to-day business: essentially a cash-basis income statement. Inflows: collections from customers, interest received. Outflows: payments to suppliers, salaries, interest paid, taxes. This is the engine — a healthy company generates positive CFO reliably, because it's the only bucket that refills itself.

CFI — cash from investing activities. The cash aspects of transactions in long-term assets used to produce goods and services. Outflows: buying plant and equipment, buying securities or whole companies, lending money to others. Inflows: selling PP&E or investments, collecting principal on loans made. Negative CFI is usually a good sign for a growing firm — it means money is being planted, not that it's being lost.

CFF — cash from financing activities. The cash aspects of transactions with the firm's funders — debt and equity. Inflows: issuing stock, borrowing from banks or bondholders. Outflows: repaying principal, buying back stock, paying dividends. (Note the asymmetry: interest paid is CFO under US GAAP, but the principal repayment is CFF.)

Read as a pattern, the signs tell a life story: a startup is (−CFO, −CFI, +CFF) — burning cash, investing, living on funders. A healthy mature firm is (+CFO, −CFI, −CFF) — operations fund the investing and return the surplus. A firm with (−CFO, +CFI, +CFF) is selling the furniture and borrowing to cover a business that doesn't work.

Why anyone cares, given we already have net income: to see whether reported profits are actually realized as cash (the Enron question), to understand the sources of cash in detail, to forecast future cash flows, to feed DCF valuation, and to judge liquidity (lenders and rating agencies). Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact.

How the statements articulate

The class example is Nike's FY2009 (in $M). Balance sheets bookend the year; the other three statements explain what happened in between:

  • Income statement: revenues $19,176.1 − expenses $17,689.4 = net income $1,486.7 → flows into the SSE.
  • SSE: beginning equity $7,825.3 + NI $1,486.7 − dividends $475.2 − stock items $143.7 = ending equity $8,693.1 → lands on the ending balance sheet.
  • Cash flows: $1,736.1 (CFO) − $798.1 (CFI) − $780.8 (CFF) = +$157.2 → beginning cash $2,133.9 becomes ending cash $2,291.1 → the cash line on the ending balance sheet.

beyond the slides — what the textbook adds (ch. 1)

Le-Nature's Inc. — the chapter's framing fraud. Reported $275M of sales; real sales were ~$32M, liabilities understated by $200M, new loans repaying old ones (a Ponzi). CEO got 20 years; the auditors paid $50M, Wachovia $80M. All three safeguards failed at once: no internal controls, duped auditors, a crony board. Those three safeguards — internal controls, independent auditors, board committee — are the testable list, and primary responsibility for the statements sits with management (CEO + CFO), not the auditors.

Statement mechanics SmartBook likes: every statement heading has four parts — entity, statement title, date ("At Dec 31") or period ("For the Year Ended"), and unit of measure. Assets are listed by ease of conversion to cash, liabilities by maturity. Accounts payable = credit purchases without a written contract; notes payable = borrowing with one. And dividends are a distribution, never an expense.

Conceptual framework (classic quiz): fundamental qualities = relevance (predictive/feedback value, material) and faithful representation (complete, neutral, free from error); enhancing qualities = comparability, verifiability, timeliness, understandability. GAAP ≠ tax rules — companies keep a separate set for the IRS. Foreign firms listed in the US may report under IFRS.

Business entities (Supplement A): sole proprietorship and partnership are legally inseparable from owners (general partners have unlimited liability) but are separate accounting entities; corporations give limited liability, continuity, transferable ownership, and capital-raising power at the cost of double taxation.

New key terms: accounting entity (the organization the data describes), accounting period (the span the statements cover).

Balance sheet & transaction analysis · Ch. 2 · Class 2 (Jul 6)

The three definitions (worth memorizing verbatim)

  • Asset: "a resource controlled/owned by an entity as a result of past events/transactions and from which future economic benefits are expected to flow to the firm."
  • Liability: "a probable future sacrifice/transfer of economic benefits arising from present obligations of the entity as a result of past events/transactions."
  • Stockholders' equity: "the residual interest in (or remaining claims against) the firm's assets after deducting liabilities" — A − L = E.

Recognition (getting onto the balance sheet at all) requires three things: measurability (a relevant attribute measurable with sufficient reliability), reliability (representationally faithful, verifiable, neutral), and relevance (capable of making a difference to decisions). There's a natural trade-off between reliability and relevance — which is why genuinely valuable things like employees, internally built brands, and know-how never appear as assets: relevant, but not reliably measurable. It's also why LVMH could pay $16.2B for Tiffany when Tiffany's market cap was $10.6B and its book equity far less — market value ≠ book value.

Classification: current assets convert to cash (or get used) within a year or one operating cycle; current liabilities are due within a year. The classified balance sheet lists current assets first in order of liquidity, then long-term (tangible vs. intangible, financial vs. non-financial); current liabilities before long-term; equity last, split into common stock (shares × par value), additional paid-in capital (issue price above par), and retained earnings.

Debits and credits

Debit means left. Credit means right. That's the entire content of the words — no moral valence. The direction of "increase" follows from which side of the accounting equation an account lives on:

AssetsLiabilitiesEquity
Debit (left)increasedecreasedecrease
Credit (right)decreaseincreaseincrease
Normal balancedebitcreditcredit
The handy trick: assets are on the left side of A = L + E, so the left side of the T-account (debit) increases them. Liabilities and equity are on the right side of the equation, so the right side (credit) increases them. One sentence replaces a page of memorization.

Every transaction is analyzed in two steps: (1) what was received and what was given — identify the accounts, classify each as A/L/E, determine direction; (2) verify A = L + E still balances and total debits = total credits. Only four patterns exist for pure balance-sheet transactions:

TypePatternExample
IAsset ↑ and Liability/Equity ↑Borrow cash; issue stock
IIAsset ↓ and Liability/Equity ↓Repay a loan
IIIOne asset ↑, another asset ↓ (swap)Buy equipment for cash
IVOne liability/equity ↓, another ↑ (swap)Refinance a payable with a note

The bookkeeping pipeline: transactions → documentation → journal entries → post to the general ledger (T-accounts) → trial balance → financial statements. A journal entry records the dual effect at a point in time; the T-account accumulates a running balance per account.

No entry for exchanges of promises. Signing a $10,000 website contract: no entry. Hiring a president at $85,000/year starting next January: no entry. Ordering supplies: no entry until they arrive. An accounting transaction requires that goods, services, or cash actually change hands — this shows up on every exam.

Current ratio

\[ \text{Current Ratio} = \frac{\text{Current Assets}}{\text{Current Liabilities}} \]

If everything due in the next year came due at once, could you cover it with what turns into cash in the next year? Above 1 means yes with cushion; higher means more cushion — but a very high ratio can signal inefficiency (resources parked in idle cash or slow inventory rather than at work).

Class examples: Tiffany at 5.24 (enormous cushion, mostly diamond inventory — characteristic of luxury jewelry, but arguably inefficient) vs. Delta at ~0.76 (fine for an airline, because big current liabilities like air-traffic liability and loyalty deferred revenue are settled by flying people, not paying cash). Same ratio, opposite stories — always interpret against the business model. Competitor spread: Swatch 5.74, Burberry 1.69, Prada 1.3, Pandora 0.62.

beyond the slides — what the textbook adds (ch. 2)

Four assumptions/principles (the chapter's core additions): separate entity (the business's books exclude the owner's personal transactions — an owner borrowing for personal use = no entry); going concern (assume the business continues; if bankruptcy looms, assets get valued at liquidation instead); monetary unit (report in the national currency, with no adjustment for inflation); historical cost (record at cash-equivalent value on the transaction date, including the value of any noncash consideration like a trade-in).

Transactions come in two types: external (exchanges with other parties) and internal events (measurable but exchange-free — using up prepaid insurance, wearing out equipment). Promises-for-promises still don't count, but big commitments (long-term employment contracts) may need note disclosure. Account-naming rules worth knowing cold: "payable" → always a liability, "receivable" → always an asset, "unearned" → always a liability, "prepaid" → always an asset.

What's not on the balance sheet: internally developed intangibles (Meta's balance sheet shows no Facebook trademark; Coca-Cola shows no Coke-formula asset but does carry $14.2B of purchased trademarks — purchased vs. internally developed is the line). Off-balance-sheet obligations hide in the "Commitments and Contingencies" note — read the notes.

Exam-style skill the book calls out: inferring activity from a T-account — beg + increases − decreases = end, solve for the unknown (A/P: 600 + 1,500 purchases − paid = 300 → paid 1,800). Also: par value has no relationship to market price (it's a legal amount from the charter), and treasury stock — repurchased shares — reduces equity.

Chipotle color: all 3,400+ stores company-owned (no franchising, unlike McDonald's at 95% franchised); its unearned revenue = unredeemed gift cards; McDonald's turned a $360M investment in it into ~$1.4B. Current ratio 1.57, with the same too-high-can-mean-inefficient caveat as the slides' Tiffany example.

state of the equation · after ch. 2 \[ \underbrace{CA + NCA}_{\text{Assets}} = \underbrace{CL + NCL}_{\text{Liabilities}} + \underbrace{(\text{CS}_{par} + \text{APIC}) + RE}_{\text{Equity}} \]

New: the identity is unchanged, but every term now has anatomy — assets and liabilities split current vs. non-current (the current ratio compares the two current buckets), and paid-in capital splits into common stock at par plus additional paid-in capital. Mechanically new: every transaction hits ≥2 accounts (debits = credits, always), recognition requires measurability/reliability/relevance, and promises aren't transactions. Income statement so far: untouched — this chapter's transactions are pure balance-sheet events (investing and financing); retained earnings didn't move once.

Income statement & accrual accounting · Ch. 3 · Classes 2–3 (Jul 6–8)

The operating (cash) cycle: buy or make product on credit → pay suppliers → deliver to customers on credit → collect from customers → repeat. Accrual accounting exists because the interesting events (earning, consuming) and the cash events (collecting, paying) happen at different points on this loop.

  • Revenues: "increases in assets or settlement of liabilities in exchange for delivery of goods or services." They increase retained earnings.
  • Expenses: "decreases in assets or increases in liabilities in the process of delivering goods or services." They decrease retained earnings.
  • Gains/losses: same balance-sheet impact, but from peripheral transactions (selling the old delivery truck, not selling coffee) — kept separate so users can see core operations cleanly.

Cash basis records revenue when cash arrives and expense when cash leaves. Accrual accounting — required by GAAP — records "revenues when earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of the timing of cash receipts or payments."

Revenue recognition: the five-step model

The converged GAAP/IFRS standard, one sentence: revenue recognition depicts transfer of control to the customer in an amount reflecting the consideration the entity expects to be entitled to, applied to products, services, and licenses.

  1. Identify the contract — do we have a deal?
  2. Identify the performance obligations — who is doing what?
  3. Determine the transaction price — what do we expect to be owed?
  4. Allocate the price across the obligations — split it if there are several.
  5. Recognize revenue when (or as) each obligation is satisfied — i.e., when control transfers.

Amazon's 10-K is the class case study, because one company runs nearly every recognition pattern at once: retail revenue when control transfers (delivery to carrier, or to the customer for Amazon-delivered orders); third-party seller commissions when the service is rendered; Prime as a single "stand-ready obligation" recognized ratably over the subscription period; advertising by clicks/impressions delivered; AWS as the customer consumes usage. Same five steps, five different answers.

Before the critical eventUnearned (deferred) revenue — a liability (you owe goods/services)Prepaid (deferred) expense — an asset (benefit already paid for)
At the critical eventCash and revenue togetherCash and expense together
After the critical eventAccrued revenue — a receivable (asset)Accrued expense — a payable (liability)

The critical event for revenue is delivery (transfer of control); for expenses it's consumption. The five mini-cases from class, as entries:

Sell $5 coffee for cash: Dr Cash 5 / Cr Revenue 5 Collect $500 for next month: Dr Cash 500 / Cr Unearned Revenue 500 …trip happens: Dr Unearned Revenue 500 / Cr Service Revenue 500 Sell $5 coffee on credit: Dr Accounts Receivable 5 / Cr Revenue 5 …customer pays next week: Dr Cash 5 / Cr Accounts Receivable 5 ← no I/S impact! Pay 3 months insurance ahead: Dr Prepaid Expense 300 / Cr Cash 300 …coverage expires: Dr Insurance Expense 300 / Cr Prepaid Expense 300 Receive $100 bill, unpaid: Dr Insurance Expense 100 / Cr Insurance Payable 100 …pay the bill: Dr Insurance Payable 100 / Cr Cash 100 ← no I/S impact!

The two "no I/S impact" lines are the accrual-accounting punchline: once revenue or expense has been recognized, the later cash movement is purely a balance-sheet event.

Expense matching principle: "costs incurred to generate revenues should be recognized in the same period, regardless of when cash is paid." Inventory sits as an asset until sold (then becomes cost of sales); a building becomes depreciation expense over decades of use.

Debit/credit rules, expanded to the income statement

Revenues and expenses are just temporary subdivisions of retained earnings, so their rules follow from RE's: revenue increases with credits (like RE), expense increases with debits (it's a reduction of RE). The four I/S transaction patterns:

TypePatternEntry
IAsset ↑, Revenue ↑Dr asset / Cr revenue (cash or credit sale)
IILiability ↓, Revenue ↑Dr unearned revenue / Cr revenue (earning a deferral)
IIIAsset ↓, Expense ↑Dr expense / Cr asset (consume a prepaid, use supplies)
IVLiability ↑, Expense ↑Dr expense / Cr payable (accrue an unpaid cost)

Margins: reading the income statement as percentages of sales

A multi-step income statement subtotals its way down from sales, and each subtotal isolates one layer of cost:

Sales (net) − Cost of goods sold → Gross profit (product economics) − Operating expenses (SG&A, etc.) → Operating income (running the business) ± Interest, other income → Pretax income (+ financing choices) − Income taxes → Net income (bottom line)

The general margin recipe — any income statement line ÷ net sales

\[ \text{X Margin} = \frac{X}{\text{Net Sales}} \quad \text{for } X \in \{\text{Gross Profit, Operating Income, Pretax Income, Net Income, …}\} \]

Every margin answers the same question at a different altitude: of each dollar of sales, how many cents are still alive at this line? Gross margin is the economics of the product itself (price vs. direct cost). Operating margin adds the cost of actually running the business — stores, salaries, marketing. Net profit margin is after everything, including financing and taxes — the compression ratio of the whole company.

The layering is what makes them diagnostic. If gross margin is stable but operating margin is falling, the product is fine and the overhead is bloating. If gross margin itself is eroding, there's price competition or input cost pressure — no amount of SG&A discipline fixes that. And because net sales is the common denominator ("common-size" analysis), margins let you compare a $15B company to a $500M one, or the same company across years, on equal footing.

Two cautions from class: compare within an industry (grocery margins and software margins live on different planets — nature of the product and intensity of competition set the baseline), and expect well-run firms to hold or improve margins over time.

beyond the slides — what the textbook adds (ch. 3)

Time period assumption — the one new key term: a company's long life can be cut into artificial short periods (months, quarters, years) for reporting; it's why recognition timing matters at all. Public companies must file quarterly (interim) reports.

Gain/loss placement rule (testable): gains/losses are disposals of assets for more/less than net book value. Disposals of operational assets (land, equipment) go in the operating section, above income from operations; gains/losses on investments go in Other Items with interest revenue/expense. Format tell: if you see an "Income from Operations" subtotal it's a multiple-step statement; single-step lumps all revenues and all expenses.

The cash-flow mirror (trips everyone): interest paid and investment income received are Other Items on the income statement but operating on the statement of cash flows. Splitting a loan payment: interest portion → CFO, principal portion → CFF.

Small sharp tools: effective tax rate = tax expense ÷ pretax income (Chipotle 2023: 392/1,621 = 24.2%). An expenditure is any cash outlay; not all expenditures are expenses. Dividing every line by net sales gives a common-sized income statement. Chipotle's net profit margin: 12.45% vs El Pollo Loco 5.45%, Shake Shack 1.86%.

Fraud, matched to principle: WorldCom violated expense recognition ($11B of expenses booked as assets); ZZZZ Best violated revenue recognition (fictitious sales). Know which is which.

state of the equation · after ch. 3 \[ A = L + \text{Paid-in Capital} + RE_{beg} + \text{Rev}^{*} - \text{Exp}^{*} - \text{Div} \]

New: retained earnings cracks open — during the period, revenues and expenses are live accounts inside it (revenue increases with credits, expense with debits, because both are fragments of RE). Accrual timing populates both sides of the balance sheet with new accounts: accounts receivable and prepaid expenses on the asset side, unearned revenue and accrued payables on the liability side — each one is a timing gap between the critical event and cash. Income statement so far: now multi-step — Sales − COGS = gross profit, − operating expenses = operating income, ± peripheral gains/losses and interest, − taxes = net income — with each subtotal ÷ sales giving a margin. Revenue enters only on transfer of control (five-step model); expenses follow revenues (matching).

Adjusting entries, closing & earnings quality · Ch. 4 · Class 4 (Jul 13)

The accounting cycle: during the period, analyze → journalize → post. At period-end: unadjusted trial balance → adjusting entries → adjusted trial balance → financial statements → close. Adjustments exist because some transactions are implicit — no invoice or cash movement flags them; they happen through the passage of time (rent expiring, subscriptions being earned, interest accruing, equipment wearing out). Adjusting entries get revenues into the period earned and expenses into the period incurred so the statements are true and fair.

Golden rule: never debit or credit Cash in an adjusting entry. If cash moved, it was an explicit transaction recorded when it happened. Adjustments only re-time revenues and expenses. (Repeated twice in the deck; will be on the exam.)

The four adjustment types

What happened firstAdjusting entryExample
Deferred (prepaid) expensePaid cash, booked an assetDr Expense / Cr Asset (or contra-asset)Rent, insurance, supplies, depreciation
Deferred (unearned) revenueReceived cash, booked a liabilityDr Unearned Revenue / Cr RevenueSubscriptions, deposits, gift cards
Accrued expenseNothing yet — cost incurred, unpaidDr Expense / Cr PayableWages, interest, taxes owed
Accrued revenueNothing yet — earned, uncollectedDr Receivable / Cr RevenueInterest earned, work done unbilled

Deferrals: cash first, income statement later. Accruals: income statement first, cash later. Seller and buyer are mirror images — one company's unearned revenue is its customer's prepaid expense.

Depreciation & contra-assets

Straight-line depreciation and net book value

\[ \text{Depreciation}/\text{yr} = \frac{\text{Cost} - \text{Residual Value}}{\text{Useful Life}} \qquad \text{NBV} = \text{Cost} - \text{Accumulated Depreciation} \]

A $16,500 desk set used for 5 years isn't a $16,500 expense in year one — it helps generate revenue for five years, so matching spreads its cost: $3,300/year, $275/month. Depreciation is cost allocation, not valuation — no one claims the furniture is worth exactly NBV.

The credit doesn't hit Furniture directly; it accumulates in Accumulated Depreciation, a contra-asset (credit-balance account glued to its parent). That preserves the original cost on the balance sheet: readers see both what was paid and how much has been consumed. Delta's balance sheet shows the scale: $18.7B of accumulated depreciation against flight equipment with estimated lives of 20–34 years — and every one of those life estimates is management judgment.

Interest accrual

\[ \text{Interest} = \text{Principal} \times \text{Annual Rate} \times \text{Fraction of Year} \]

Interest is rent paid for the use of money, and it accrues with time whether or not a payment date has arrived. Borrow $10,000 at 10%: each month you owe another $83.33 (10,000 × 0.10 × 1/12), so each month-end brings Dr Interest Expense 83.33 / Cr Interest Payable 83.33. Stated rates are annual unless said otherwise — the fraction-of-year term is where points are lost.

Closing the books

Permanent accounts (balance sheet) carry forward. Temporary accounts (revenues, expenses, gains, losses, dividends) measure one period's performance, so at period-end they're zeroed into retained earnings: debit each revenue for its balance, credit each expense, close dividends — and RE ends at beginning + NI − dividends, exactly matching the SSE. Next period starts from zero.

Earnings per share

\[ \text{EPS} = \frac{\text{Net Income} - \text{Preferred Dividends}}{\text{Avg. Common Shares Outstanding}} \]

Slices the period's profit into per-share pieces so it's comparable to the share price — the number analysts forecast and the bottom line of every income statement. Preferred dividends come out of the numerator because they're spoken for before common shareholders get anything.

Quality of earnings

Adjustments are the soft underbelly of the income statement, for two reasons: managers know where performance stands relative to analyst forecasts before the books close, and many adjustments rest on estimates requiring judgment (useful lives, accrual amounts). The evidence: plot analyst forecast errors (actual EPS − forecast) across thousands of firms and the histogram spikes just above zero — lots of small beats, suspiciously few small misses. Companies "meet or just beat." Amazon's earnings history in class showed surprises from +60.5% to −8.4% against consensus. Read reported earnings knowing the discretion embedded in them.

beyond the slides — what the textbook adds (ch. 4)

Total asset turnover — the chapter's ratio: net sales ÷ average total assets (avg = (beg + end)/2). How many sales dollars each asset dollar generates; rising = more efficient. Chipotle 2023: 9,872 / 7,486 = 1.32, up from 1.20 in 2021 — and higher than franchise-heavy competitors, since franchisors hold fewer assets per sales dollar.

AJE rules the book states outright: every adjusting entry has at least one income-statement account and one balance-sheet account (and never Cash); adjustments only ever increase revenues and expenses; the income-tax adjustment always goes last (adjusted pretax × rate). Land never depreciates. Before adjustment: deferrals leave an asset/liability overstated and the expense/revenue understated; accruals leave both sides understated.

Exam trap: Retained Earnings on the adjusted trial balance is stale — it still excludes the current period's net income until closing. The statements must be built in strict order (I/S → SSE → B/S) so NI can flow through; an income-statement error cascades into both other statements.

Closing extras: a post-closing trial balance verifies debits = credits and all temporary accounts read zero. Treasury stock is a contra-equity account (the book's second contra-account after accumulated depreciation). EPS is the only ratio GAAP requires on the face of the statements.

Earnings quality, quantified: analysts compute CFO ÷ NI — higher means earnings are backed by cash; a large gap (high income, low operating cash) flags aggressive accruals. The SEC's EPS Initiative literally screens filings for "meet or just beat" patterns.

state of the equation · after ch. 4 \[ \underbrace{CA + (\text{Cost} - \text{AccDep}) + \cdots}_{\text{Assets}} = L + \text{Paid-in Capital} + RE_{beg} + \underbrace{\text{Rev}_{adj} - \text{Exp}_{adj}}_{\text{NI, only after adjustment}} - \text{Div} \]

New: assets can now carry contra-accounts — PPE sits on the balance sheet net, at cost minus accumulated depreciation — and the period's revenue and expense totals only mean anything after the four adjustment types (prepaid, unearned, accrued expense, accrued revenue) have re-timed them into the right period, never touching Cash. At period-end the red term is closed: Rev, Exp, and Div zero out into RE, the equation collapses back to A = L + PIC + RE, and the next period starts the expansion fresh. Income statement so far: gains a bottom-most line, EPS = (NI − preferred div) / avg. shares — and a health warning: everything above that line runs through management's estimates, which is why the earnings-quality histogram spikes at "just beat."

Statement of cash flows · Ch. 12 · Class 5 (Jul 15)

The income statement tells you whether the products are economically viable; the cash flow statement tells you whether the same transactions actually generate cash. Three reasons it exists: cash confirms that reported profits are real (cash is hard to manipulate; accruals aren't), it shows which part of the business produces or consumes cash, and free cash flow is the raw input to valuation (DCF). Caveat from class: firms can still reclassify items between sections opportunistically.

SectionInflowsOutflows
Operating (CFO) — day-to-day with customers, suppliers, employeesCollections from customers; interest & dividends receivedPayments to suppliers and employees; interest paid; taxes
Investing (CFI) — long-term assets & investmentsSelling PP&E, intangibles, investments; disposals of businessesBuying PP&E, intangibles, investments
Financing (CFF) — exchanges with fundersIssuing stock; reissuing treasury stock; borrowingDividends paid; buying treasury stock; repaying principal
The three classification traps, one more time: interest paid is operating (US GAAP), while the loan principal is financing — one payment, two lines. Dividends paid are financing (a distribution, never an expense); dividends received are operating. And debt or stock issued for anything other than cash is a noncash transaction — excluded from all three sections, disclosed separately.

Direct vs. indirect

Both methods produce identical CFO, CFI, CFF, and Δcash — they differ only in how the operating section is presented. The direct method lists gross receipts and payments (collections from customers, cash paid to suppliers…) — easier to read, much harder to prepare, preferred by the FASB, used by almost nobody. The indirect method reconciles net income to CFO — harder intuition, easy preparation, used by ~99% of large US companies. Depreciation never appears on a direct-method statement at all (it isn't a cash flow). CVS's 10-K shows both at once — direct section plus the required indirect reconciliation ($15.9B CFO in 2020, either way).

Indirect method — the template

Net income + Depreciation / depletion / amortization + Loss (− Gain) on disposal of long-term assets + Decrease in operating asset − Increase in operating asset + Increase in operating liability − Decrease in operating liability = Net cash flow from operating activities

Net income counted things that weren't cash, and missed cash that wasn't income — the template just un-counts and re-counts. Depreciation reduced income but no one paid anybody (the cash left years ago, when the asset was bought, in investing) — add it back. A gain on selling equipment sits inside net income, but the entire sale proceeds are reported in investing — subtract the gain or you'd count it twice (add back losses for the same reason).

The working-capital rules are all one idea: an operating asset growing means cash got trapped on the balance sheet (receivables up = sales you didn't collect; inventory up = purchases you haven't sold) — subtract. An operating liability growing means someone else is floating you (payables up = expenses you haven't paid) — add. Memorize it as: asset up, cash down; liability up, cash up.

Class Q&A: why start from NI at all? Precisely to display how income and cash differ — the reconciliation IS the information.

From the balance sheet identity, cash is what's left after everything else:

\[ \Delta \text{Cash} = \Delta L + \Delta SE - \Delta \text{Noncash Assets} \]

Split ΔSE into net income and dividends, and split every other account by which activity drives it (operating, investing, financing). Group the operating pieces:

\[ CFO = NI + \text{noncash charges} \pm \text{gains/losses} - \Delta \text{op. assets} + \Delta \text{op. liabilities} \]

— which is the template. The indirect method isn't a trick; it's the accounting equation differenced over a year and sorted into three buckets. That's also why the statement can be built from two balance sheets plus the income statement.

Direct-method amounts: everything is a T-account roll-forward

Every "cash collected/paid" number comes from the same pattern — beginning + ins − outs = ending, solve for the unknown:

Collections: Beg A/R + Sales − Collections = End A/R Purchases: Beg Inventory + Purchases − COGS = End Inventory Payments: Beg A/P + Purchases − Payments = End A/P Dividends: Beg RE + NI − Dividends declared = End RE

Shortcut forms: cash from customers = Sales − ΔA/R; payments to suppliers = COGS + ΔInventory − ΔA/P; payments for an expense = expense + Δprepaid − Δpayable. Same machinery works for investing (Beg PP&E + purchases − book value sold = End PP&E; sale proceeds = book value sold + gain − loss) and financing.

Free cash flow

\[ \text{FCF} = CFO - \text{CapEx} \qquad \text{(unlevered: } EBIT(1-t) + D\&A - \Delta WC - \text{CapEx)} \]

The cash left over after the business has paid to keep itself running and growing — free to fund expansion, retire debt, or go back to shareholders. It's the number DCF valuation discounts (at WACC for the whole firm; subtract interest and debt retirement to value just the equity). Definitions vary — the textbook's version also subtracts dividends.

Why not just use EBITDA as "cash flow"? Buffett's answer from class: "Does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?" EBITDA ignores the reinvestment a real business can't skip; FCF doesn't. The professor's mental image: cash flow is the tap the firm actually drinks from.

beyond the slides — what the textbook adds (ch. 12)

Cash means cash + cash equivalents — investments with original maturity ≤ 3 months, measured from when the holder bought it (a 3-year T-note purchased 3 months before maturity qualifies; one held from issuance never does). Moving money between cash and equivalents is not a cash flow at all.

Account-tagging fine print (SmartBook bait): short-term investments are investing, not operating current assets. Three current liabilities are financing: dividends payable, short-term bank debt, current maturities of long-term debt. Retained earnings gets tagged both O (the net-income part) and F (the dividends part). Asset-sale proceeds go to investing at the full cash received regardless of gain or loss — the gain/loss only ever appears as the operating back-out.

Two ratios: quality of income = CFO ÷ NI (National Beverage 1.15; Coca-Cola 1.08, PepsiCo 1.47) — four reasons it strays from 1.0: growth inflating receivables/inventory, seasonality, aggressive recognition, sloppy working-capital management. Capital acquisitions ratio = CFO ÷ cash paid for PP&E (NBC 7.41 vs Pepsi 2.44) — how much of capex operations fund internally; compare only within an industry. W. T. Grant went bankrupt in 1975 with healthy net income and collapsing CFO — the divergence is the classic fraud/decline early warning.

Required supplemental disclosures: significant noncash investing/financing transactions (buy a building with a seller mortgage, convert debt to stock) in a separate schedule, and — under the indirect method — cash paid for interest and for income taxes.

Format trivia: the FASB recommends the direct method; ~99% of large US companies use indirect anyway (cost). Under IFRS, interest paid may go in financing and interest/dividends received in investing — GAAP fixes all three in operating (dividends paid excepted: financing).

state of the equation · after ch. 12 \[ \Delta \text{Cash} = \underbrace{NI + \text{noncash} \pm \text{wedges}}_{CFO} + CFI + CFF \] \[ \text{because} \quad \Delta \text{Cash} = \Delta L + \Delta SE - \Delta \text{Noncash Assets} \]

New: nothing was added to the equation — it was differenced. Take A = L + E at two dates, subtract, solve for ΔCash, and sort every account's change into three buckets: that's the entire statement of cash flows. The indirect method is the operating bucket rearranged to start from net income, which makes the NI-vs-cash gap — the accrual wedges from Ch. 3–4 — the visible headline. Every adjusting-entry concept now has a mirror image here: the accruals that moved income without cash get reversed out, one by one. Income statement so far: unchanged, but now auditable — quality of income (CFO/NI) asks whether the income statement's story is corroborated by the cash account, and free cash flow (CFO − capex) is what's left for the owners of the story.
