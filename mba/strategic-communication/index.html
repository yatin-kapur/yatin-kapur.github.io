Overview

Why this course exists

You can have the right idea and still fail to move anyone. The course is the discipline of translating ideas so multiple audiences (cultural, intellectual, professional) actually act on them. The mantra is the four S's:

  • Strategy - who are you trying to move, to do what?
  • Substance - what's the evidence and big idea behind your ask?
  • Structure - what order makes the ask irresistible?
  • Style - how do tone, pace, and design land for this audience?

Skills the course builds

  • Develop a communication strategy from scratch
  • Analyze an audience to be appropriately persuasive (not generically loud)
  • Structure a message that holds attention
  • Deliver prepared and impromptu presentations under pressure
  • Write professional documents that pass SEC Plain English guidelines
  • Collaborate as an effective team member, in person and online

t+AIM - the core planning framework

Used to plan every deliverable in the course: emails, presentations, executive memos. The single most leverage-y idea in the syllabus.

LetterQuestionWhy it comes first
tTask - what is the deliverable, in concrete terms?Forces you to name the artifact (a 350-word email, an 18-min pitch) before you start writing into the void.
AAudience - who is reading or listening, and what do they already know, want, fear?You write for them, not for yourself. Most "bad" comms are well-written for the wrong audience.
IIntent - what do you want the audience to do, think, or feel?Persuasion has a verb. "Inform" is rarely the real intent - usually it's "approve," "fund," "back off," "show up."
MMessage - your one big idea, short and memorable.If they remember nothing else, what's the line they walk away with? Earn the right to one.
Tested heuristic: if you can't fill in t+AIM in two minutes for the thing you're writing, you don't yet know what you're writing. Stop, fix the brief, then start.

Why it works

  • Audience before content. Most communicators draft slides, then ask "who is this for?" - t+AIM flips that.
  • Intent forces a verb. "I want them to fund the pilot" is testable. "I want to share an update" is not.
  • Message anchors structure. Every body paragraph or supporting topic should ladder up to the one big idea.

Plain English (per the SEC Handbook)

The course used SEC's A Plain English Handbook (chapters 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7) as the standard for written deliverables. The argument: clear writing isn't a stylistic preference - it's an obligation when busy people are deciding whether to act on what you wrote.

Principles

  • Active voice. "We will refund your fee" beats "Your fee will be refunded." Names the actor, takes responsibility, uses fewer words.
  • Short sentences. Aim for one idea per sentence. Long sentences are where readers get lost and stop.
  • Concrete, everyday language. Cut legalese, jargon, and Latin where a normal English word does the job.
  • Tabular when listing. Lists and tables beat dense paragraphs for anything with structure (criteria, options, steps).
  • No superfluous words. Cut "in order to" → "to". "At this point in time" → "now". "Due to the fact that" → "because".
  • Design as a feature, not a finish. Headings, white space, and visual hierarchy guide the reader; bury the lede in a wall of text and they're gone.
Before: "It is recommended by the team that consideration be given to the implementation of a strategy that would address the reputational concerns currently impacting the organization."

After: "We recommend a reputation strategy that fixes three problems: data trust, child safety, and content moderation."

Presentation structure

The Presentation Planner: a fill-in template for organizing any talk. Three blocks - Opening, Body, Closing - each with a job to do.

Opening

  • Get attention. Open with an image, story, surprising stat, or question.
  • Establish purpose. One sentence on why this audience should care.
  • Preview. Briefly signal what's coming (an agenda, in plain words).

Body

  • Three supporting topics is the sweet spot - enough to feel substantive, few enough to remember.
  • Each topic should ladder up to your big idea (the M in t+AIM).
  • Mix evidence types: a statistic, a quote, a chart, a brief story. Single-mode bodies are forgettable.
  • Visual aids exist to support the speaker - at least two charts or graphs for a benchmark talk. Slides aren't a teleprompter.

Closing

  • Recap. Remind the audience of your intent and message.
  • Strong closing line. Image, story, or sentence that re-emphasizes the central message.
  • Q&A management. Anticipate the top 5 questions in advance and pre-write tight answers.
  • Final close. Don't trail off after Q&A - return to the message and end clean.

Delivery techniques

  • Eye contact across the room - not at one friendly face.
  • Vary pace and pitch. Monotone is what kills attention, not lack of charisma.
  • Pause after key points. Lets the message land. Most presenters under-pause.
  • Manage your hands. Don't pin them; don't fidget. Open palms, open ideas.
  • Warm up physically and vocally before high-stakes talks.

Reviewing your own video (Schenkler & Russell method)

The course required watching your own presentation video and self-evaluating. The discipline:

  1. Watch once for content (did you say what you meant to say?).
  2. Watch a second time on mute, looking only at body language.
  3. Watch a third time eyes-closed, listening only to pace, energy, filler words.
  4. Pick one thing to improve before next time. Just one. Improvement compounds.

Persuasion & handling Q&A

Session 4 was dedicated to elements of persuasion and Q&A practice. The persuasive presentation (A5) and executive email (A6) operationalize what's here.

Elements of a persuasive case

  • Specificity. Vague recommendations get vague responses. Name the strategy, the steps, the scope.
  • Benefits - short and long term. Executives want both. Why does this matter this quarter and three years out?
  • Feasibility. Is this realistically implementable? What does it cost in money, time, political capital?
  • Evidence. What data backs your claims? Any anecdote needs a number; any number needs an anecdote.

Diagnostic questions before you persuade

  • What are the root causes of the issue (not just the symptoms)?
  • Why is the current approach not enough?
  • How are peer companies handling the same problem? Better or worse?
  • What are industry/academic experts recommending?
  • What's on the minds of the leaders you're addressing? What headwinds will you hit?
  • Reputation depends on doing the right thing AND communicating it the right way - is the company's communication timely, transparent, authentic?
  • The X factor - anything else that matters that you noticed but isn't on this list?

Q&A: responding well under pressure

  • Listen to the whole question. Don't start composing while they're still talking.
  • Acknowledge before answering. "Good question" is hollow; "That's the toughest part of the proposal" is real.
  • Bridge back to your message. Every answer is another chance to reinforce the M in t+AIM.
  • Say "I don't know" when you don't. Then commit to following up. Faking erodes credibility instantly.
  • Anticipate. Pre-write answers to your top 5 likely questions. The good speakers aren't quicker - they're more prepared.

The executive email (A6 model)

~350-word business email to a single executive after a team persuasive presentation. Goes beyond reiterating the recommendation - it advances the original argument by addressing what that executive specifically needs to be convinced. Why should they personally support it? What questions did the live presentation leave open that a written follow-up can close?

Reminders: tailor message, evidence, structure, and tone to the individual reader. Have a clear intent. Engaging design. Succinct language.

Belbin team roles

Meredith Belbin's framework: nine roles people naturally play on a team. Used pre-class to declare your top two preferred roles and bottom one, so teams could be formed with deliberate role mix rather than randomness.

Useful as a diagnostic when a team is stuck: are you missing a Plant (no new ideas), a Completer Finisher (drafts never ship), or a Co-ordinator (people rowing in different directions)?

  • Plant (PL)- innovative, original, problem-solver; introverted Generates new proposals and solves complex problems. Needed in the initial stages of a project or when one is stalling. Founders and originators.
  • Resource Investigator (RI)- enthusiastic, networker, negotiator; loses interest quickly Explores opportunities outside the group, develops contacts, picks up others' ideas and runs with them. Best for external negotiations and scouting.
  • Monitor Evaluator (ME)- shrewd judgment; slow, can read as dry Analyzes problems, weighs pros and cons. Ideal for the small number of crunch decisions where being right matters more than being fast.
  • Co-ordinator (CO)- mature, confident; can clash with Shapers "Consultation with control." Best with peers of equal rank. Spots talent and uses it. (Called "Chairman" in the original Belbin book.)
  • Shaper (SH)- driven, action-oriented; argumentative Generates action, sparks teams to life, pushes through political complications. Most competitive role; great for change and unpopular decisions.
  • Teamworker (TW)- diplomatic, perceptive; indecisive in crunch moments Prevents interpersonal problems; lubricating effect on teams. Especially valuable when Shapers are at risk of running over people.
  • Implementer (IMP)- reliable, disciplined; can be rigid Tackles problems systematically. Loyal to the company over self-interest. Does what needs to be done - including the parts others avoid.
  • Completer Finisher (CF)- detail-obsessed; doesn't like to delegate Won't start what they can't finish. Invaluable on tasks that demand precision - the last 10% that ships product instead of leaving it 90% done.
  • Specialist (SP)- deep expertise; narrow focus The expert. Indispensable when the team's product depends on a specific deep skill. Commands support because they know more than anyone in the room.
How to use it: when forming a team, aim for at least one of each function - an ideas role (PL/RI), an action role (SH/IMP), a people role (CO/TW), a thinking role (ME/SP), and a finisher (CF). Too many of the same role causes predictable failure modes (too many Plants = endless competing ideas, no execution).
